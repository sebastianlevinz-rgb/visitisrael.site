The Dead Sea is the most popular day trip from both Jerusalem and Tel Aviv — but the two departures give you different days. Jerusalem is closer and better for combining with Masada; Tel Aviv is the right base if you want to skip Masada and spend a relaxed few hours floating. Here is an honest comparison to help you decide.

## At a glance

| | From Jerusalem | From Tel Aviv |
|---|---|---|
| **Drive to Ein Bokek** | 45–90 min | 2–2.5 hrs |
| **Tour duration (typical)** | 9–12 hrs | 10–13 hrs |
| **Pair with Masada?** | Easy — the natural combo | Possible but a very long day |
| **Float time on a tour** | 1–2 hrs (Dead Sea only); ~1 hr on Masada combo | 1.5–2.5 hrs (more time because no Masada) |
| **Price range (group tour)** | $89–120 per person | $60–100 per person |
| **Best for** | First-timers who want Masada too | Those skipping Masada; relaxed float day |

## Departing from Jerusalem

Jerusalem is the closest city to the Dead Sea and the most popular departure for tour groups. The drive south-east on Route 1 takes 45–90 minutes, meaning tours can combine Masada in the morning with a float in the afternoon and still return to Jerusalem by early evening.

**The classic Masada + Dead Sea combo** leaves Jerusalem at 07:00–08:00 (or 03:00–04:00 for the sunrise version), climbs Masada via cable car or the Snake Path, stops at [Ein Gedi](/dead-sea/ein-gedi) if itinerary allows, and arrives at [Ein Bokek](/dead-sea) by early afternoon for roughly one hour in the water. It is a full day but an efficient one.

**Dead Sea only tours from Jerusalem** depart later (around 09:00) and give you two to three hours at the shoreline — enough time to float properly, apply the famous mineral mud, rinse and sit in the sun. These suit returning visitors who have already done Masada, or anyone who would rather have a relaxed wellness day without the early alarm.

For self-drivers: [Qumran](/qumran-guide) — the site where the Dead Sea Scrolls were found — sits 30 minutes north of Ein Bokek on Route 90 and adds very little driving time. It is not accessible on most group tours but pairs easily with a self-drive day from Jerusalem.

See our [Masada and Dead Sea day trip guide](/masada-dead-sea-day-trip) for the full itinerary detail, sunrise vs daytime options and what to bring.

## Departing from Tel Aviv

Tel Aviv is roughly 90 minutes further from Ein Bokek than Jerusalem, which means tours add about an hour each way to the journey. The tradeoff is that **most Tel Aviv tours are Dead Sea only** — they skip Masada precisely because the combined trip from Tel Aviv becomes too long. You arrive at Ein Bokek by late morning and typically get 1.5–2.5 hours at the water.

This is actually ideal if Masada is already crossed off your list (or you plan to do it separately), and it means more time floating than you get on a Jerusalem Masada-combo tour. Tel Aviv tour prices also tend to be lower — $60–100 per person — because the format is simpler.

For the **Masada + Dead Sea from Tel Aviv**: tours exist but expect a 12–14-hour day with very early pickups (some start at 02:00–03:00 for the sunrise version). It is doable but demanding. If you are based in Tel Aviv and want Masada, a day trip to Jerusalem first — it is only 55 minutes by rail — followed by a Jerusalem-based Masada tour the next morning is often more comfortable.

## Self-driving from either city

If you have a [rental car](/car-rental-israel), the Dead Sea is genuinely easy to reach from both cities:

- **From Jerusalem:** Route 1 east → Route 90 south. About 90 km, 45–90 minutes depending on traffic.
- **From Tel Aviv:** Route 1 east via Jerusalem (or Route 6 south then east) → Route 90 south. About 160 km, 2–2.5 hours.

The **free public beach at Ein Bokek** has outdoor showers and changing rooms — no entry fee. Resort hotels along the promenade sell day passes with better facilities and sun loungers (book in advance in peak season). The [Israel National Parks Pass](/israel-national-parks-pass) covers [Qumran](/qumran-guide) and Ein Gedi nature reserve (canyon hikes, not the beach — Ein Gedi public beach is permanently closed due to sinkholes). ## Which departure is right for you?

- **Want Masada and the Dead Sea in one day?** Depart from Jerusalem — shorter drive, more manageable day.
- **Already done Masada, want a relaxed float?** Either city works. Tel Aviv tours often have better Dead Sea time; Jerusalem is still closer.
- **Travelling from Tel Aviv without a car?** Book a Tel Aviv Dead Sea only tour — you get a good float without an extreme day.
- **Have a rental car?** Self-drive from either city; Jerusalem is the faster option.
- **Want sunrise at Masada?** Base yourself in Jerusalem to keep the 03:00 pickup from destroying your sleep more than necessary.
