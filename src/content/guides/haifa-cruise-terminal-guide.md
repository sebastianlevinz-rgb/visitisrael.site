Haifa's new cruise terminal, inaugurated on **14 May 2026**, has significantly changed the experience for cruise passengers arriving at Israel's primary northern port. The 5,500m² facility — the largest in Israel and among the most capable in the Eastern Mediterranean — handles two mega-ships simultaneously, cuts immigration queuing with 28 dedicated border-control booths, and places the terminal within walking distance of Haifa's train station and city centre. This guide covers the upgraded terminal itself, the best shore excursion options from Haifa, and the practical logistics that make the difference between a stress-free port day and a rushed one.

## What's new at the 2026 Haifa cruise terminal

The previous Haifa port facility was functional but dated. The **NIS 16M (~$5.5M) investment** delivered a purpose-built cruise gateway with:

- **Capacity for two mega-ships simultaneously** — roughly double the prior facility's throughput, aiming for up to one million passengers annually
- **28 border-control stations** reducing average immigration wait times significantly
- **600m² duty-free retail zone** for last-minute purchases departing Israel
- **Currency exchange, cafeteria, Wi-Fi and luggage storage** within the terminal
- **Covered bus terminal** at the exit, connected to Haifa's downtown bus station and HaMifratz train station — both under five minutes away

Norwegian Cruise Line and Royal Caribbean were among the first major lines to reinstate or expand Haifa itineraries following the upgrade. For the most current ship schedules and terminal operating hours, verify at **haifaport.co.il** before your cruise.

## Getting out of the terminal

The new terminal exit feeds directly into the port district near downtown Haifa. Three options for reaching the city:

| Option | Destination | Time | Cost (rough range) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Foot or free bus** | Downtown Haifa / train station | 5 min | Free (shuttle) |
| **Taxi from rank** | Bahá'í Gardens lower entrance | 10–15 min | ₪30–50 |
| **Train (HaMifratz station)** | Akko | 20 min | ₪12–15 |
| **Train (HaMifratz station)** | Tel Aviv | 55–65 min | ₪30–40 |

The **German Colony boulevard** — Haifa's most visitor-friendly street, lined with cafés and restaurants in restored Ottoman Templar buildings — is about 10–15 minutes by taxi from the port, or a pleasant 20-minute walk through the lower city.

## Best shore excursion options from Haifa port

### Within Haifa — half-day or full-day

**Bahá'í Gardens** (UNESCO World Heritage Site) are the most visited landmark in northern Israel. The 19 terraces cascade 300 metres down Mount Carmel from the golden-domed Shrine of the Báb to the lower gateway; guided terrace tours are free but require advance booking at bahai-haifa.org. The terrace entrance is about 15 minutes by taxi from the cruise terminal. Allow **1.5–2 hours** for the terraces; add **30–45 minutes** for a walk along the German Colony boulevard and a coffee at one of its cafés.

The **Wadi Nisnas** neighbourhood — a short taxi ride further into the lower city — offers some of the best falafel and hummus in northern Israel, open on Shabbat since the neighbourhood is predominantly Arab-Christian.

### Akko — the first choice for history

[Old Akko](/akko-acre-guide) is 20 minutes north by train from HaMifratz station (trains run approximately every 30 minutes). The UNESCO-listed Crusader underground city — Knights' Halls, the Templar Tunnel, the Al-Jazzar Mosque and the souk — is best appreciated with 2–3 hours. For a comfortable day without rushing:

- **Depart the ship** as soon as immigration clears
- **Bahá'í Gardens** (or German Colony coffee) for 1.5–2 hours
- **Train to Akko** (20 min), returning by train in time for the all-aboard
- Total: a comfortable 7–8 hour day

The classic **Haifa + Akko** combination is the most popular northern-coast day and is offered by most Haifa-based guided tour operators. For the comparison between joining a group tour versus going independently, see the [Haifa tours compared guide](/haifa-tours-compared).

### Caesarea — Roman ruins by car

[Caesarea National Park](/caesarea-guide) is about 45 minutes **south** by car from Haifa, making it a natural pre-docking or post-excursion stop. The site includes a Roman harbour, a Byzantine street, a Crusader-era fortified city, and one of the best-preserved Roman theatres in the Middle East. Allow **2–2.5 hours** at the site. There is no train to Caesarea; a taxi or private guided car is required. The combination of Caesarea in the morning (heading south toward Tel Aviv) and Haifa/Bahá'í in the afternoon is a practical two-site day.

### Nazareth — for Christian pilgrims

[Nazareth](/nazareth-travel-guide) is **35–45 minutes by car** from the cruise terminal, making it a feasible half-day for passengers with a specific pilgrimage interest. The Basilica of the Annunciation is the highlight; the White Mosque and the Old Souk are a short walk. Allow **2.5–3 hours in Nazareth** plus the driving time. There is no direct train from Haifa to Nazareth; a private car or guided tour is necessary. Nazareth works best on a longer ship day (8–9 hours ashore) or combined with a more relaxed Haifa morning.

### Rosh Hanikra — the northern sea caves

[Rosh Hanikra](/rosh-hanikra-guide) sits on Israel's northern Mediterranean coast, 45 minutes from Haifa by car. The chalk cliff sea caves — reached via a 90-second cable-car descent — are one of Israel's most dramatic natural sites. The experience at the grottos takes about **1.5 hours**. The classic cruise excursion combines Haifa, Akko and Rosh Hanikra as a three-site northern day, which requires a guide with a private car and a longer ship window (9+ hours ashore).

## Practical tips for cruise passengers

**Timing immigration:** Plan for **60–90 minutes** from docking to exiting the terminal, especially when two ships are in port simultaneously. The 28 booths speed processing considerably versus the old facility, but peak-season queues can still build. Have your ship card, passport and boarding documentation at hand.

**Cash and currency:** Israeli shekels (₪) are available from the currency exchange inside the terminal and from ATMs in the immediate port area. The exchange desk gives reasonable rates for a port facility; for larger amounts, the ATMs in Haifa's city centre or the HaMifratz station are competitive. Credit cards are widely accepted in restaurants and tour offices, but taxis and market stalls often prefer cash.

**SIM cards and connectivity:** Israeli SIM cards are available from the duty-free area and mobile carrier shops in downtown Haifa (Pelephone, Partner, Cellcom). If you have an unlocked smartphone, a local SIM with a 10-day data plan is inexpensive and eliminates roaming charges. Most Haifa restaurants and the terminal itself offer Wi-Fi.

**What to wear:** Religious sites — the Bahá'í Gardens, the Al-Jazzar Mosque in Akko — require covered shoulders and knees. A scarf or light layer covers all bases and is useful in any weather.

**Returning to the ship:** Israel Rail runs from Haifa HaMifratz to Tel Aviv, then south to Jerusalem. The train station is a five-minute walk from the terminal; direct return trains from Akko take 20 minutes. Always allow **at least 45 minutes** between leaving your last excursion point and the ship's all-aboard time. Independent passengers who miss the ship's departure bear full responsibility for reaching the next port independently — this is a meaningful risk when travelling long distances from Haifa.
