Caesarea's Roman Theatre — built by Herod the Great around 22 BCE, partially restored in the 1960s and seating 4,000 — is the most atmospheric live music venue in Israel. Concerts here feel unlike anything else in the country: an ancient semicircle of stone with the Mediterranean visible beyond the stage, the warm night air carrying the music, and 2,000 years of history under your feet. Major Israeli pop stars, international rock acts, classical orchestras and jazz performers all take the stage here between April and October each year.

This guide covers how to book, which seats to choose, how to get there and how to combine a concert with a full day of Caesarea's archaeology.

---

## The Caesarea concert season

The [Caesarea Development Corporation](https://www.caesarea.com) operates the theatre as a live event venue each year, typically running shows from **April through October**. The programme is eclectic and high-quality:

| Type | Examples | Ticket range |
| --- | --- | --- |
| International rock/pop | Major European and American acts (has hosted the Rolling Stones, Elton John, Paul McCartney) | ₪300–600+ |
| Israeli pop & rock | Israel's biggest names — Eyal Golan, Idan Raichel, Mashina | ₪150–400 |
| Classical / orchestras | Israel Philharmonic, visiting chamber groups | ₪100–250 |
| Jazz & Blues nights | Israeli jazz soloists, international ensembles | ₪120–280 |
| Comedy & theatrical | Occasional Israeli theatre productions | ₪100–200 |

The full programme is announced on a rolling basis — check **caesarea.com** for the current season's lineup as it updates. Dates and tickets are also listed on the major Israeli platforms: Ticketmaster (ticketmaster.co.il), Eventim (eventim.co.il) and Leaan (leaan.co.il).

> Ticket prices are correct only via official channels. Verify current prices before purchasing from third-party resellers.

## How to buy tickets

1. **Go to caesarea.com** for the authoritative season programme — each show entry links directly to the ticketing page.
2. **Buy early.** Major Israeli artists and any international headliner sell out weeks or months in advance. When a popular show is announced, buy immediately.
3. **Choose your tier.** The theatre has lower orchestra (closest to stage, most expensive), middle tier (best price-to-view balance) and upper terraces (sea view backdrop, lower prices). All areas have good acoustics.
4. **Print or download your ticket** — physical tickets are accepted at the gate alongside digital QR codes.

## Getting there

### By car

Drive Highway 2 northbound from Tel Aviv (~45 minutes) or southbound from Haifa (~30 minutes). Follow brown tourist signs to "Caesarea National Park" — the main visitor-centre car park is the arrival point for both daytime visits and concerts. The car park is paid during the day (park ticket can be validated), and concert-night parking is included in most ticket packages — check the specific show's details.

**Arrive early.** The car park fills quickly on concert nights. Allow 60–90 minutes before showtime to park, pass security and find your seat.

Post-concert traffic on Highway 2 southbound is heavy for 30–45 minutes after a large show. Budget for this, or arrange a designated driver plan.

### By train

Take the northbound coastal Israel Railways service to **Caesarea–Pardes Hanna station** (~35 minutes from Tel Aviv HaShalom, ~25 minutes from Haifa Hof HaCarmel). From the station, a 10-minute taxi or pre-booked shuttle covers the 7 km to the National Park entrance.

**Critical:** There are no trains back to Tel Aviv after approximately 11pm. Pre-book a return taxi or car service (Gett operates in the area) before the show — or plan to overnight in Caesarea or Zichron Yaakov. Do not rely on finding a taxi after midnight.

## Seating: what to choose

**Lower orchestra** — closest to the stage; highest prices; best for headliner pop and rock shows where being near the performance matters. The ancient stone is at face level — the visual backdrop is the stage and night sky.

**Middle tier** — the most popular choice. Good sightlines, reasonable prices, and you can see both the stage and the sea beyond the theatre's open rear.

**Upper terraces** — the widest view: stage in the foreground, the Mediterranean glimmering behind. The effect is spectacular for the first hour. Performers look small from here; best for orchestral and atmospheric shows where ambience matters more than proximity. Cheapest tickets.

### Seat comfort

Ancient stone is hard. Bring a **thin seat cushion** or rent one at the venue entrance for approximately ₪20. Seat rental usually runs out for large shows — bring your own to be safe. A thin blanket is useful for late-evening classical concerts when the sea breeze cools things down significantly, even in July.

## Food and drink

The in-venue food stalls are basic — the quality is not what you would expect for the ticket price. A better approach:

- Eat **before arriving**: the Caesarea harbour restaurants (within the park complex) serve decent Mediterranean food and are open until the early evening. Book ahead for concert nights.
- Alternatively, stop in **Zichron Yaakov** (12 km south) on the way up — the main pedestrian street has cafés, a Lebanese restaurant and several good dinner options.
- Inside the venue: snacks, drinks and the usual festival-food options are available. Alcohol is served.

## Special events: Caesarea Festival of Light

In addition to the summer concert season, Caesarea runs a separate **Festival of Light** event in winter — typically on selected evenings from January to March. Large-scale light installations and projection mapping cover the Roman Theatre, the ancient aqueduct, the harbour ruins and the Crusader walls. Entry costs are lower than concert tickets and the event is family-friendly.

The Jerusalem Festival of Light (June, Old City) is the more internationally known of the two events, but the Caesarea version has a different character — the ruins are more dispersed, the site is less crowded and the Mediterranean setting is dramatic in winter lighting. Check caesarea.com for the current year's dates and programme.

## Combining a concert with daytime Caesarea

The ideal Caesarea day runs like this:

| Time | Activity |
| --- | --- |
| 10:00–11:30 | [Caesarea National Park](/caesarea-guide): Roman Theatre, Hippodrome, Herodian harbour walk |
| 11:30–13:00 | [Aqueduct Beach](/caesarea-guide#aqueduct-beach): swim and photographs |
| 13:00–14:30 | Harbour restaurants: lunch |
| 14:30–16:00 | Ralli Museum (free; sculpture collection + Latin American art) |
| 16:00–19:00 | Drive or browse Zichron Yaakov (12 km south); optional wine tasting at one of the Carmel/Tishbi wineries |
| 19:30–20:30 | Return to Caesarea; pre-show dinner if needed |
| 21:00 | Show begins |
| 23:00–23:30 | Concert ends; drive or taxi back |

The National Parks Pass (INPA pass) covers the daytime park entry but does NOT cover concert tickets — these are separate commercial events managed by the Caesarea Development Corporation.

## Overnight options

Staying overnight removes the late-night highway-drive problem and lets you open the next morning with Aqueduct Beach at sunrise — one of the best photography windows in Israel.

**In Caesarea**: the Park Inn Caesarea and several boutique options are within the modern town. Pricier than the surrounding area, and they fill fast for concert weekends.

**In Zichron Yaakov** (12 km south): the better-value option — boutique guesthouses in a charming Ottoman-era village on the Carmel ridge. Several walking-distance restaurants and a weekend artisan market. A 15-minute taxi from the theatre.

Book well in advance for any Saturday-night concert, when hotels across both locations fill weeks ahead.
