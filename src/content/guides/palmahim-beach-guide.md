**Palmahim Beach National Park** (Hebrew: **חוף פלמחים**) is an Israel Nature and Parks Authority (INPA) coastal reserve on the Mediterranean, 30 kilometres south of central Tel Aviv. It combines a working sea turtle nesting beach, a rare intact kurkar ridge dune system, supervised swimming, and designated camping — all within range of a morning drive from the city. For a country with an intensely urbanised coastline, Palmahim represents one of the few remaining stretches of genuinely wild Mediterranean shore in the Tel Aviv metropolitan area.

---

## Why visit Palmahim

Most visitors to Israel know Tel Aviv's city beaches — Gordon, Frishman, Hilton — busy stretches of urban waterfront with beach bars and sun-lounger vendors. Palmahim is the opposite: a national park beach where the infrastructure is minimal and the landscape is largely as the coast has been for millennia. The primary draws are:

- **Sea turtle nesting** — one of Israel's most active loggerhead sea turtle (Caretta caretta) nesting beaches, with INPA rangers monitoring active nests during the summer season
- **Kurkar ridge dunes** — a rare intact coastal dune system formed from cemented ancient sandstone, running parallel to the waterline
- **Uncrowded swimming** — lifeguarded in summer, with far lower visitor density than city beaches
- **INPA National Parks Pass** — entry is covered by the Israel National Parks Pass, making it a natural addition to a parks-pass itinerary that might include Caesarea, Ein Gedi or Masada

---

## The sea turtle nesting programme

Palmahim Beach is among the most significant sea turtle nesting sites on Israel's Mediterranean coast. Loggerhead turtles (Caretta caretta) haul themselves onto the beach between May and August each year to lay their eggs above the waterline. INPA rangers and volunteers from the Israeli Sea Turtle Rescue Center (ISTRC) at Mikhmoret monitor the nests through the season.

During the nesting period, active nests are marked with a ring of wooden stakes — **never cross into a marked nest zone or touch the markers**. The eggs incubate for approximately 50–60 days before hatching, typically producing 60–100 hatchlings per nest. Nest disturbance is a criminal offence under Israeli wildlife law.

If you visit in summer (particularly June–August), ask the INPA rangers at the entrance whether there are active nests visible on the beach. Rangers sometimes arrange evening walks to observe nesting activity from a safe distance when conditions permit — ask at the gate about current programming.

## The beach and dunes

### Kurkar ridge landscape

The coast at Palmahim is shaped by a kurkar ridge — a formation of aeolianite (ancient sand dunes cemented into rock by calcium carbonate over thousands of years) running south to north parallel to the waterline. This ridge creates low sandstone cliffs and rock platforms between the sea and the flat coastal plain behind, and traps windblown sand behind it to form the dune system.

The dunes between the ridge and the highway are among the few intact coastal dune fields remaining near Tel Aviv — most of the coastline has been developed, and Palmahim's park status has protected this pocket of natural landscape. The dune vegetation (sea rocket, sea spurge, medick and various grasses) is fragile and important for stabilising the sand above turtle nest zones — **stay on marked paths through the dune areas**.

### Swimming

The main swimming beach sits at the seaward base of the ridge. Lifeguards are present during the main season (approximately June–September); operating hours follow INPA guidelines, which are confirmed at the park entrance. The flag system applies:

| Flag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| White | Safe; supervised swimming |
| Red | Rough conditions; no swimming |
| Black | Beach closed |

The beach shelves gradually, making it suitable for families. There are no sun-lounger vendors or beach bar facilities; bring your own umbrella, chairs and supplies. Fresh water is available at the car park and camping facilities, not on the beach itself.

## Camping

Palmahim has designated camping zones managed by INPA. This is the only INPA-managed campsite on the Mediterranean coast in the Tel Aviv area, which makes it popular for summer weekenders.

**Key facts:**
- Booking is required through the INPA online system at parks.org.il — walk-in camping is not available
- Summer weekends (July–August) fill 3–4 weeks in advance; book early
- Facilities: marked fire pits in designated zones, chemical toilets, fresh water access; no electricity hookups
- The Israel National Parks Pass may reduce or cover the camping fee — check current terms on the Palmahim page at parks.org.il
- Campfires are restricted to designated pits; lighting fires on the dunes or beach is prohibited (fire risk and turtle disturbance)

## Practical information

### Entry and the INPA pass

Palmahim Beach National Park is an INPA site — the **Israel National Parks Pass is valid here**. If you are visiting two or more INPA parks on your trip (Caesarea, Masada, Qumran, Ein Gedi, Beit She'an, and many others all accept the pass), it pays for itself quickly. See the full [Israel National Parks Pass guide](/israel-national-parks-pass) for current pass tiers and pricing.

Without the pass, per-vehicle or per-person entry fees are charged at the gate. Current prices are published on the INPA website at parks.org.il/palmahim — always check before visiting as fees are updated periodically.

The park gate operates during daylight hours and is staffed during peak season. Entry after sunset is not generally available except for pre-booked camping arrivals.

### Getting there

**By car** (recommended): From central Tel Aviv, take Highway 1 south then Highway 4 (the coastal road) south through Bat Yam and Rishon LeZion. The Palmahim park entrance is signposted from Highway 4 south of the Palmahim junction. Total drive time: 30–40 minutes depending on Tel Aviv traffic. Parking is available at the park entrance.

From Ben Gurion Airport: Head south on Highway 40 or Highway 4 — approximately 25 minutes without traffic.

**By public transport**: There is no direct bus service to the national park. The nearest bus routes run along Highway 4 through Rishon LeZion and Yavne; from there a taxi to the park entrance adds approximately ₪30–50. This approach requires pre-planning a return taxi or calling one in advance — there is no taxi rank at the park gate. A rental car makes the logistics significantly simpler.

### Shabbat

As an INPA site, Palmahim is generally **open on Shabbat** during the main season — this is unlike KKL-JNF sites such as Rosh Hanikra, which close on Shabbat. However, the park can be extremely busy on Saturday (it is a popular local family day-out); arriving early on Saturday morning (before 9am) is strongly advised to secure parking and a good beach spot. Verify current Shabbat hours on parks.org.il as seasonal schedules vary.

### What to bring

- **Sunscreen SPF 50+** — the beach has minimal natural shade; the dune area has low scrub only
- **Umbrella and chairs** — there are no vendors; bring your own shade
- **Fresh water and food** — a small kiosk operates at the entrance in peak season, but availability varies; pack for a self-sufficient day
- **Beach shoes or sandals** — the kurkar rock platforms can be sharp underfoot
- **Camera with zoom** — for watching turtle nests from a distance in nesting season without approaching the marked zones
- **Early arrival** — the park fills on summer Fridays and Saturdays; aim for 8–9am to secure parking and beach space

## Nearby sites

**Tel Aviv beaches (30 km north)** — Gordon, Frishman and Hilton beaches for the city beach experience. See [Best beaches in Israel](/best-beaches-israel) for a full comparison of Mediterranean and Red Sea beaches.

**Day trips south of Tel Aviv** — Palmahim connects naturally with the coastal route toward Ashdod and Ashkelon; both have beach parks and accessible antiquities. Add Palmahim as a morning stop on a [south Israel day](/south-israel-itinerary).
