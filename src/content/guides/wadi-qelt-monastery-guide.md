**Wadi Qelt** (Hebrew: **נחל פרת**, Nahal Prat) cuts through the Judean Desert east of Jerusalem — a 27-kilometre canyon dropping from 850 metres near the capital to sea-level minus 250 metres at Jericho. Along its walls, sustained by a perennial spring, a ribbon of tamarisks, willows and oleanders runs the entire length of the gorge, and at its midpoint, clinging to a sheer ochre cliff, hangs one of the most dramatic monasteries in the world: St. George of Koziba.

This guide covers what to expect at Wadi Qelt and the monastery, how to reach both by car or on foot, safety fundamentals for desert hiking, and how to combine the site with a Jericho day trip from Jerusalem.

---

## What makes Wadi Qelt distinctive

Three things set Wadi Qelt apart from the dozens of desert canyons accessible from Jerusalem:

**The permanent spring.** Ein Qelt, the wadi's source spring, flows year-round. In an otherwise arid landscape, this creates a continuous oasis — a stripe of deep green pressed between pale limestone cliffs, audible as running water even in dry summer months when every surrounding hillside is parched. The spring sustained a Byzantine-era aqueduct that ran all the way to Jericho, sections of which are still visible along the trail.

**St. George Monastery.** The monastery of St. George of Koziba, founded in the 5th century CE and partly carved directly into the cliff, is one of the finest examples of early Christian monastic architecture in the Middle East. Its white-and-ochre facade rising above the gorge floor has been photographed from every angle, but nothing prepares you for seeing it from the clifftop road above, or for walking the gorge floor and watching it appear around a bend.

**The desert-to-city contrast.** Within 45 minutes of Jerusalem's urban density, you are walking through biblical desert landscape — the same wadis where hermit monks sought isolation 1,500 years ago, where Byzantine pilgrims walked between Jerusalem and Jericho, and where the gorge walls preserve the geological record of the Judean highlands' eastward plunge toward the Jordan Rift Valley.

## Getting there

**By car from Jerusalem**: take Route 1 east (toward Jericho and the Dead Sea). After approximately 22 km, watch for the signed turn-off to **Wadi Qelt / Ein Prat Nature Reserve** (INPA signs in Hebrew, Arabic and English). The trailhead car park is a further 4 km down a secondary road.

For the **cliff-road viewpoint** above the monastery (no hiking required), a separate turn continues further east on Route 1 and then descends toward Wadi Qelt via a road signposted toward the monastery. Standard GPS navigation (Waze or Google Maps: search "Wadi Qelt Monastery") works reliably for the cliff-road approach from the Jericho side.

**By guided tour**: the most common option for visitors who prefer not to navigate the West Bank road network independently. Jericho day tours from Jerusalem typically pass the Wadi Qelt viewpoint; some longer tours include a brief walk on the canyon floor. See the tour options above.

**By rental car**: a rental car gives you full flexibility for combining the trailhead, viewpoint, and Jericho in a single day. Vehicles rented in Israel are permitted on the roads leading to the INPA trailhead (Area C) but **Israeli car rental agreements typically prohibit travel into Area A** (the city of Jericho proper). Read your rental agreement and plan accordingly. See the [driving in Israel guide](/driving-in-israel) for rental details.

## St. George Monastery

The **Monastery of St. George of Koziba** (in Greek: Μονὴ Ἁγίου Γεωργίου Χοζεβᾶ) takes its name from a 6th-century monk, George of Koziba, who joined an earlier monastic community founded around 420 CE. That first community was established by a Syrian hermit named John of Thebes, who carved out a series of caves in the cliff face overlooking the gorge — recognising the site as one where the prophet Elijah was said to have sheltered and received food from an angel (1 Kings 17:3–5).

The original community was destroyed by the Persian invasion of 614 CE and rebuilt sporadically through the early Islamic period. The monastery was abandoned entirely during the Crusader era and lay in partial ruin until the late 19th century, when a Greek monk, Kalinikos, led an ambitious restoration that returned it to functioning monastic life. That restoration, completed in the 1880s–1900s, created most of what visitors see today: the characteristic white chapel domes, the arched gallery across the clifftop, and the terraced garden above the gorge floor.

**Inside the monastery**: the interior is modest in scale but richly layered. The main church (katholikon) houses icons of considerable age and a reliquary said to contain the skulls of 14 monks martyred in the 614 Persian invasion — a sobering counterpoint to the site's visual beauty. A second, older chapel is carved directly into the cliff rock. The monks maintain a small pilgrim reception area, and coffee or water is sometimes offered to respectful visitors.

**The cliff-road view**: before or instead of walking the gorge floor, drive to the cliff-road viewpoint to see the monastery from directly above. The perspective — a sheer vertical drop of approximately 60 metres to the monastery roof, the gorge floor another 30 metres below that, with the ribbon of green stretching east toward Jericho — is one of the most dramatic vantage points in the entire country.

## Hiking the wadi

### Shorter option: the viewpoint and monastery (4–7 km, 2–3 hours return)

From the Ein Prat trailhead, the marked trail follows the gorge floor eastward. The path is straightforward but uneven — rocky in places, with sections where you step over boulders or along narrow ledges above the stream. The monastery appears dramatically around a bend after approximately 90 minutes of easy-moderate walking.

From the monastery, most day visitors return the same way rather than continuing the full 27 km to Jericho. Allow 2–3 hours total for the return walk, including time at the monastery.

### Full traverse: Ein Prat to Jericho (27 km, 8–10 hours)

This linear route from the INPA trailhead to Jericho is a serious desert hike suitable for experienced hikers with proper planning: pre-arranged transport at the Jericho end, a very early start (before 7 am), 3+ litres of water per person, high-calorie food, and knowledge of flash flood weather. The trail passes sections of the Byzantine-era Jericho aqueduct, multiple spring pools, and one sustained scramble section near the monastery. The descent is gradual for the first two-thirds, then drops more steeply toward the Jordan Valley floor.

**Do not attempt the full traverse in summer** (June–September). INPA may close the trail during extreme heat. Check trail status at parks.org.il before your visit.

### Flash flood warning

The Judean Desert receives little rain, but what falls comes in concentrated bursts. A wadi like Nahal Prat can flash-flood violently with no warning at ground level — the rain generating the flood may fall kilometres upstream, out of sight, with blue skies directly overhead. **Do not enter any wadi** if rain is forecast anywhere in the Jerusalem hills or Jordan highlands, or if the sky upstream is dark. The INPA and Israeli weather service (ims.gov.il) publish flash flood warnings — check before departing.

## Combining with Jericho

Jericho — the lowest city on Earth at roughly 250 metres below sea level — is 30 minutes by car from the Wadi Qelt trailhead. It makes a natural pairing for a full Jordan Valley day from Jerusalem.

**Tell es-Sultan (ancient Jericho)**: the tel at the centre of Jericho preserves 10,000 years of continuous human settlement, making it one of the oldest known inhabited sites in the world. The Neolithic tower excavated here (c. 8000 BCE) predates the Egyptian pyramids by five millennia. The tel is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (Palestinian nomination); visitor access is straightforward from the Jericho city centre.

**Hisham's Palace (Khirbet al-Mafjar)**: 3 km north of Jericho, this 8th-century Umayyad winter palace features one of the finest floor mosaic surviving from the Islamic world — the Tree of Life mosaic in the bathhouse diwan. The site is under PA authority and accessible to foreign tourists.

**Qasr al-Yahud baptism site**: 10 km south of Jericho on the Jordan River, this is the traditional site of Jesus's baptism by John the Baptist. Greek Orthodox, Catholic and Protestant churches maintain chapels here. Access from the Israeli side via Route 90; the Jordanian side faces across a narrow water channel. Open daily with free entry.

**Practical note on Jericho access**: the city of Jericho is Area A (Palestinian Authority). Israeli nationals are not legally permitted to enter. Foreign nationals (including tourists from all countries) can enter freely with their passport — there is no checkpoint in practice on the main access roads from Route 1. Guided tours cover all of this without visitor management.

## Photography notes

Wadi Qelt is one of the most photographed sites in the Judean Desert. The monastery from the cliff road offers the classic long-lens view — the white chapel domes against the cliff, the gorge floor visible below, a background of pale desert hills. Best light is morning (before noon) when the sun is still to the east; afternoon puts the monastery in the cliff's shadow.

From the gorge floor, the monastery appears around a rock bend — a genuinely surprising moment. The green of the spring vegetation against the ochre walls provides strong colour contrast that works in any light.

**Do not photograph the monks** without permission. If a monk is in frame, either compose to exclude them or ask first. Monastic communities at desert sites in Israel and the Palestinian Territory have had intrusive photography become a consistent friction point; respectful visitors help maintain access for future pilgrims and tourists.
