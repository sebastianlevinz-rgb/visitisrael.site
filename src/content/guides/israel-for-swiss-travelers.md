Switzerland and Israel share a quietly significant connection — the ICRC (International Committee of the Red Cross / Comité international de la Croix-Rouge), founded in Geneva in 1863, maintains a permanent delegation in Israel and operates extensively in the region. For many Swiss travellers, Israel combines religious heritage, Middle Eastern culture and a degree of historical resonance. This guide covers the practical specifics for Swiss visitors: the EDA/FDFA travel advisory, the ETA-IL requirement, flights from Switzerland, the Type J plug adapter (the most commonly overlooked preparation), Swiss health insurance limitations, banking and embassy contacts.

For the complete trip-planning picture, see the [first-time visitor guide](/first-time-in-israel), the [safety guide](/is-israel-safe) and the [ETA-IL and visa guide](/visa-information).

## EDA/FDFA travel advisory

The Swiss Federal Department of Foreign Affairs (EDA in German — **E**idgenössisches **D**epartement für **a**uswärtige Angelegenheiten; FDFA in English) publishes regularly updated travel advisories at **eda.admin.ch**. This is the authoritative source for Swiss citizens travelling to Israel — not social media summaries, tour operator FAQs or third-party travel news.

The EDA advisory is tiered by zone and updated in real time:

- **Gaza Strip** — Travel not advised; civilian access is not possible.
- **Areas near the Lebanese and Syrian borders** — Highest caution; specific zones carry travel warnings.
- **West Bank** — Elevated caution; specific roads and towns carry advisories.
- **Principal tourist areas** — Jerusalem, Tel Aviv, the Dead Sea, Galilee (south of the border zone), the Negev and Eilat are the destinations regularly visited by Swiss tourists.

The EDA advisory is available in **German** (Reiseinformationen), **French** (Informations aux voyageurs) and **Italian** (Informazioni per i viaggiatori) — Switzerland's three main official languages.

**Register with ITINERIS.** The EDA operates the ITINERIS traveller registration system at **itineris.eda.admin.ch** — Switzerland's equivalent of other countries' embassy registration schemes. Registering your trip in Israel is free, voluntary, and lets Swiss consular staff contact you in an emergency. Particularly recommended for longer stays.

## Flying from Switzerland

### From Zurich

**SWISS (LX)** operates daily non-stop service between **Zurich Kloten (ZRH) and Tel Aviv Ben Gurion (TLV)**. The flight time is approximately **4 hours**. SWISS typically schedules morning or afternoon departures, arriving Tel Aviv in the early afternoon or evening. **El Al** also serves ZRH–TLV with several weekly direct flights and is often price-competitive with SWISS.

### From Geneva

There is no regular direct service from Geneva (GVA) to Tel Aviv. Swiss travellers from the French-speaking Romandy region typically:

- Connect in **Zurich on SWISS** — a short domestic hop + direct ZRH–TLV
- Take **Air France** GVA→CDG→TLV (Paris Charles de Gaulle connection)
- Use **Turkish Airlines** via Istanbul (IST) — a popular and often lower-cost option from GVA
- Occasionally use **easyJet** or other LCCs from GVA via Western European hubs

### From Basel

Basel (BSL/MLH/EAP) is a tri-country airport (Switzerland / France / Germany); EasyJet and other LCCs serve it with onward European connections. Most Swiss travellers from the Basel region find ZRH the most convenient international long-haul hub, 90 minutes by train.

**When to book.** Fares from Switzerland are typically lowest in January–February and October–November. The most expensive periods coincide with the Jewish High Holidays (late September–October), Israeli school holidays, Passover (March–April) and the summer peak (July–August). Swiss school holiday windows also affect prices. Booking 10–20 weeks in advance generally delivers the best value. See the [cheap flights to Israel guide](/cheap-flights-to-israel) for fare-comparison tools.

## ETA-IL entry requirement

Since January 2025, **all Swiss passport holders must obtain an ETA-IL** (Electronic Travel Authorisation) before boarding any flight to Israel. Switzerland is on Israel's visa-exempt list — the ETA-IL is not a visa but a mandatory pre-travel electronic authorisation.

Key facts:

- **Cost:** ₪25 per application (approximately CHF 7–8 at recent exchange rates; charged in Israeli shekels)
- **Validity:** Up to 2 years from approval, or until passport expiry — whichever comes first; valid for multiple entries
- **Processing:** Usually 72 hours; can take several days during high season — apply at least one week before departure
- **Apply at:** **iaa.gov.il** — the Israel Airports Authority official portal. Third-party sites that charge higher fees are not official.
- **Stay length:** Up to 90 days per visit as a tourist

The ETA-IL is linked electronically to your passport number — there is no physical document to carry. Keep a note of your application reference number. See the [visa and entry guide](/visa-information) for the step-by-step application process.

**No passport stamp at Ben Gurion.** Israel stopped stamping tourist passports in the early 2010s. You will receive a small paper **entry slip** on arrival — keep it; hotels and some checkpoints may ask to see it.

## Time zone

Israel Standard Time is **UTC+3 in summer** (late March to late October, during Israeli daylight saving) and **UTC+2 in winter** (late October to late March).

| Swiss time | Summer (CEST, UTC+2) | Winter (CET, UTC+1) |
|---|---|---|
| Difference to Israel | Israel is **+1 hour ahead** | Israel is **+2 hours ahead** |

The time difference between Switzerland and Israel is minimal — one to two hours — so jet lag is essentially absent. A SWISS morning departure from Zurich at 09:00 CET arrives in Tel Aviv around 14:00–14:30 IST (summer). Returning, you travel "back in time" by one or two hours.

## Health insurance — the Grundversicherung gap

**Swiss mandatory basic health insurance (OKP/AOS/assicurazione di base) does not cover medical treatment outside Switzerland**, except for a narrow emergency provision within the EU/EEA countries — and Israel is not in the EU or EEA.

If you need medical care in Israel, you will be billed as an uninsured patient. Costs can be high: an emergency room assessment may cost several hundred to over a thousand CHF; a hospital stay runs considerably more; medical evacuation by air ambulance to Switzerland is among the most expensive scenarios, easily exceeding CHF 50,000.

**What to do:**

- **Purchase Reiseversicherung / assurance voyage** before departure. Swiss options include those from CSS, Helsana, Swica, TCS (Touring Club Schweiz), Allianz Partners Switzerland and AXA — or international providers such as SafetyWing.
- **Check your Zusatzversicherung.** Some Swiss supplementary insurance policies (Zusatzversicherung — Halbprivat or Privat tiers) include international emergency medical cover. Read the policy documents carefully, specifically the geographic scope and evacuation limits.
- **Credit card insurance is usually insufficient.** Swiss credit cards (UBS Visa, Raiffeisen, Cumulus Mastercard, etc.) sometimes include limited travel assistance, but medical evacuation limits are usually well below actual air ambulance costs.

See the [Israel travel insurance guide](/israel-travel-insurance) for what to look for in a policy covering Israel.

## The Type J plug adapter — the most-overlooked preparation

This is the most commonly overlooked packing item for Swiss travellers to Israel, and the source of most frustration on arrival.

**Switzerland uses the Type J plug** (SEV 1011 standard) — three pins in a T-arrangement, with two round pins and a third grounding pin placed separately. This standard is unique to Switzerland and Liechtenstein. It is **not** the same as the standard European Type C or Type F (Schuko) plug used in Germany, France, Austria and most of continental Europe.

**Israel uses the Type H socket** — three flat pins in a V or Y-arrangement, also unique to Israel.

| Country | Socket type | Voltage |
|---|---|---|
| Switzerland | Type J (SEV 1011) | 230V / 50Hz |
| Israel | Type H | 230V / 50Hz |

**The voltage is the same — no converter needed.** Switzerland and Israel are both on 230V / 50Hz; your Swiss appliances, phone chargers and laptop power supplies will operate normally in Israel on voltage.

**You need a Type J-to-Type H plug adapter.** This specific adapter is less common than standard European travel adapters (Type C/E/F) — it is not interchangeable with French, German or Italian adapters. Buy a Type J-to-H adapter (sometimes sold as "Switzerland-to-Israel" or listed with pin codes "J to H") online before your trip. Look for universal travel adapters that specifically list Type H (Israel) in their compatibility; most quality universal adapters include it.

**Where to buy:** Readily available online (Digitec, Galaxus, Amazon.ch); less reliably stocked in airport shops. Do not assume Ben Gurion Airport arrivals will have Swiss-specific adapters.

## Banking and cards

Israel's currency is the **New Israeli Shekel (₪, ILS)**. Card payments are accepted at most tourist-facing businesses in Tel Aviv, Jerusalem, Eilat and other tourist areas — contactless Visa and Mastercard are widely supported.

**Twint does not work in Israel.** Twint is a Swiss domestic payment system with no international network integration. It cannot be used at Israeli card terminals or ATMs. For payments in Israel, rely on your Visa or Mastercard.

**Recommended Swiss cards for Israel:**

- **Visa or Mastercard from UBS, Raiffeisen, ZKB or any cantonal bank** — accepted at Israeli ATMs and card terminals. Inform your bank before departure to prevent international transaction alerts.
- **Revolut or Wise** — popular among Swiss travellers for their real-exchange-rate conversions and low fees; widely used in Israel.
- **PostFinance:** The **PostCard** (yellow debit card) works only at Swiss Post terminals and is **not accepted in Israel.** If PostFinance is your primary bank, bring a PostFinance Visa or Mastercard, or carry a separate international card.
- **American Express:** Accepted at some larger hotels and restaurants in Tel Aviv and Jerusalem, but coverage is more limited than Visa/Mastercard.

**ATMs.** Bank Leumi, Bank Hapoalim and Israel Discount Bank ATMs are widely distributed in cities. Choose to pay in shekels when the ATM offers "dynamic currency conversion" (pay in CHF) — the ATM's own conversion rate is usually worse than your bank's rate. Withdraw ₪500–1,000 on arrival at the airport ATMs for initial transport, tips and small purchases; city-centre ATM rates are similar.

**Cash for markets and small vendors.** Jerusalem's Old City bazaars, Tel Aviv's Carmel Market and Mahane Yehuda Market, and rural areas are cash-preferred. ₪200–400 in cash is useful at all times.

See the [Israel money and currency guide](/israel-money-guide) for current rate benchmarks and tipping customs.

## Mobile phones and connectivity

Israeli networks operate on GSM/LTE/5G — all current Swiss phones work without hardware modification.

**Swisscom, Sunrise, and Salt roaming in Israel** are available but at international (non-EU) rates. Check your current plan's international day-pass or per-MB rates before departure — costs vary significantly by provider and subscription tier.

**For the most cost-effective connectivity:**

- **Local Israeli SIM card:** Available at Ben Gurion Airport arrivals (24-hour counters) and in phone shops in Tel Aviv and Jerusalem. A 30-day unlimited-data SIM from Golan Telecom, Hot Mobile or Pelephone typically costs ₪50–90 (~CHF 12–22) and includes calls within Israel.
- **eSIM:** If your phone supports eSIM (most iPhones since iPhone XS; most recent Android flagships), an eSIM from Airalo, Nomad or a local Israeli provider can be activated before departure for a convenient data-only option.

Wi-Fi is widely available in hotels, restaurants and cafés across Israel's main tourist areas.

## Switzerland–Israel historical and cultural connection

The most direct Switzerland–Israel institutional connection is the **International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC / Comité international de la Croix-Rouge / Internationales Komitee vom Roten Kreuz)**, based in Geneva since its founding by Henry Dunant in 1863. The ICRC maintains a permanent delegation in Israel and visits prisoners of war and civilian detainees under the terms of the Geneva Conventions — a humanitarian mandate closely tied to Switzerland's role as a neutral state.

Switzerland also hosts the **United Nations Human Rights Council (UNHRC)** in Geneva, which addresses Israel-related resolutions among its many mandates. For Swiss travellers with professional or academic interest in international law or humanitarian work, visiting sites such as **Yad Vashem** and the **Museum on the Seam** in Jerusalem adds a specific contextual dimension.

The significant **Ashkenazi heritage** common to Swiss and Israeli Jewish communities creates a cultural affinity for Jewish Swiss visitors exploring family genealogy and heritage — the [Jewish genealogy and roots travel guide](/jewish-genealogy-israel-trip) covers heritage research options.

## Planning your Israel trip from Switzerland

With a direct 4-hour SWISS flight from Zurich, Israel is among Switzerland's more accessible non-European long-haul destinations. A typical Swiss itinerary of 7–10 days divides between **Tel Aviv** (2–3 nights), **Jerusalem** (2–3 nights) and a regional excursion — the Dead Sea, Galilee or Eilat. The [5 vs 7 vs 10 days guide](/israel-5-vs-7-vs-10-days) helps calibrate the right trip length. Swiss travellers comfortable with driving will find Israel manageable by rental car — roads are well-maintained, GPS navigation is clear in English, and Israel drives on the right.
