Israel is one of the world's most dog-loving countries — Tel Aviv alone is estimated to have more than 500,000 registered dogs in a metropolitan population of around 4 million, one of the highest per-capita ownership rates on Earth. That dog culture translates into a genuinely pet-friendly travel experience in the coastal cities. The flip side: bringing a pet *into* Israel from abroad involves strict import requirements that demand careful advance planning. This guide covers both the logistics and the destination experience.

## Importing your pet to Israel

**Start at least 3–4 months before travel.** The Ministry of Agriculture (MOA) sets the requirements, and some steps cannot be rushed:

1. **ISO 15444 microchip** — must be implanted before any vaccinations that count toward the import permit.
2. **Rabies vaccination** — must be current. There is a mandatory 30-day wait period after the vaccine before the antibody test can be taken.
3. **Rabies antibody titre test** — taken at an MOA-approved laboratory. Results must meet a minimum threshold.
4. **MOA entry permit** — applied for online via the Ministry of Agriculture website ([gov.il/moa](https://www.gov.il/en/departments/topics/bringing-pets-to-israel)); allow 30+ days for processing.
5. **Veterinary health certificate** — issued by your vet within 10 days of travel, often requiring official endorsement from your country's national veterinary authority.

Arriving without the correct documents risks **mandatory quarantine** — not a brief inconvenience but a significant delay and expense. Never rely on summaries alone; verify the current checklist directly with the MOA and your airline before booking.

### Airline rules at a glance

| Airline | In-cabin | Cargo hold |
|---------|----------|------------|
| El Al | Pets under 8 kg incl. carrier | Pets over 8 kg; some breed restrictions |
| Arkia | Small pets in-cabin (domestic) | Larger pets as cargo |
| Wizz Air / Ryanair | Generally no pets | Varies by route |

Confirm your airline's current pet policy when booking — policies change. Breed restrictions for guard-type dogs (including Pit Bulls, Rottweilers, Dogo Argentino, and related breeds) apply at the border level regardless of the airline used.

## Dog-friendly Tel Aviv

Tel Aviv is where Israel's dog culture is most concentrated and most obvious.

### Hilton Dog Beach

The most famous dog spot in the country: an official off-leash zone on the north end of the Tel Aviv beachfront, just below the Hilton hotel. The beach has a fenced section, waste-bag dispensers, and water fountains. It runs roughly from the old Reading Power Station headland south toward the marina. It's free to use and genuinely well-maintained — busy on weekend mornings. See the [Tel Aviv beach guide](/tel-aviv-beach-guide) for the broader beachfront layout.

### Hayarkon Park

Tel Aviv's main urban park runs along the Yarkon River from east to west through the city. Dogs are welcome throughout and off-leash in designated areas. The park has shaded riverside walking paths, rowboat hire, and grassy lawns — a good midday option when the beach is too hot. See the [Hayarkon Park guide](/hayarkon-park-tel-aviv).

### Café culture

Tel Aviv has an informal dog-welcome café culture, particularly around Rothschild Boulevard, Dizengoff Centre, and Sarona Market. Most large outdoor terraces tolerate well-behaved leashed dogs; there is no national regulation mandating this, so it reflects individual owner discretion — if in doubt, ask before you sit.

### Shopping and markets

The **Carmel Market** and **Jaffa Flea Market** are outdoor and generally dog-tolerant on leash. Sarona Market's outdoor plaza is similarly relaxed. Indoor malls and supermarkets do not permit dogs.

## Pet-friendly accommodation

**Booking.com** has a "travel with pets" filter for Israel that returns both hotels and apartments — use it. Tel Aviv has the widest selection of genuinely pet-tolerant properties, including boutique hotels and vacation apartments where dogs are explicitly welcome rather than merely tolerated.

A few patterns that make pet accommodation work:

- **Apartments and holiday flats** are often the easiest option — more space, no hotel lobby to navigate, and landlords tend to be flexible about small to medium dogs.
- **Dan Hotels and Isrotel chains** each accept pets at select properties; call ahead to confirm the weight limit (typically 10 kg) and whether a cleaning deposit applies.
- **Kibbutz guesthouses** in the Galilee and Golan are variable — rural settings might suggest dog-friendliness, but working farms have their own animals and strict rules; always confirm in writing before booking.
- **Airbnb**: use the "pets allowed" filter; hosts tend to be explicit about rules and any extra fees.

Always confirm the property's specific rules (weight limits, breed restrictions, indoor/outdoor access, fees) before booking, even for properties labelled "pets allowed."

## Veterinary care in Israel

Israel has a dense network of veterinary clinics, particularly in Tel Aviv and Jerusalem. Emergency 24-hour clinics operate in both cities — search for **"מרפאה וטרינרית 24 שעות"** (veterinary clinic 24 hours) or ask your hotel reception for the nearest one.

**Medications:** Israeli vets stock the major international veterinary drug brands, but brand names sometimes differ from US or UK equivalents. Bring a written prescription or the original packaging for any medications your pet takes regularly.

**Tick prevention:** tick-borne diseases are present in Israel, especially in green rural areas (Galilee, Golan). Ensure your pet's tick prevention is up to date before travel, and check for ticks after walks in vegetation.

## Summer heat

Israel in July and August is genuinely hot — temperatures in Tel Aviv regularly reach 32°C, with extreme heat in the Negev and Dead Sea basin exceeding 40°C.

**For dogs:**
- Walk only in early morning (before 8 am) or after sunset.
- Flat-faced breeds (Bulldogs, Pugs, Boxers) are at particular risk of heat stroke; consult your vet before any summer travel.
- Never leave a dog in a parked car, even in shade with windows open.
- The Dead Sea area, Negev, and Eilat in July–August are not suitable for most dogs.
- Carry significantly more fresh water than you think you need.

Spring (March–May) and autumn (October–November) are much more comfortable for pets and humans alike. See the [best time to visit Israel](/best-time-to-visit-israel).

## Practical tips

- **Tap water** is safe for dogs throughout Israel.
- **Dog food and supplies** are widely available — Zap.co.il (Israel's main price-comparison site) lists major Israeli pet-supply chains; Royal Canin, Purina, and Hill's are all stocked in large supermarkets and pet shops.
- **Transit:** Egged buses and most public transport do not permit dogs. Sheruts (shared taxis) are at the driver's discretion. A rental car, taxi, or ride-share (Gett) is the practical solution; notify the Gett driver in advance.
- **Religious sites:** Dogs are not appropriate at the Western Wall plaza, Temple Mount, Church of the Holy Sepulchre, or similar active religious spaces. Plan these visits separately without your pet.
- **Exit from Israel:** Israeli authorities do not require export documentation for most pets, but your home country may require additional health certificates, vet checks, or quarantine on re-entry. Check your home country's pet import rules before you leave.
