Hayarkon Park — known locally as **Gan HaYarkon** — is Tel Aviv's green lung: a 950-acre ribbon of lawns, river, gardens and cycle paths that runs along the Yarkon River through the northern part of the city. It is where Tel Aviv residents come to breathe, run, picnic, kayak, cycle and do everything that the city's dense streets do not allow. For visitors, it offers a genuinely different side of Tel Aviv — one that has nothing to do with beaches, nightclubs or galleries.

The park stretches roughly 4 kilometres from the Rokach bridge area in the west toward the eastern edges of the city. Within that corridor is an unusual concentration of things to do: a bird sanctuary with over 170 species, a tropical conservatory, a cactus garden, boat rental, cycling paths, a rock-climbing wall, mini-golf, a summer water park (Meimadion), and one of Israel's largest outdoor concert venues.

---

## Getting there

Hayarkon Park sits in the northern part of Tel Aviv, roughly 15 minutes on foot from the main beach hotel strip (Gordon/Frishman area). The most enjoyable way to arrive is by bicycle: Tel-O-Fun public bike docks cluster around the Hayarkon Street seafront and the path north along the beachfront feeds naturally into the park's western cycling entrance.

By public transport, the Red Line light rail (opened 2023) stops at **HaMuzeon Station** (Ibn Gabirol / Tel Aviv Museum of Art) — a 10-minute walk north brings you to the park's southern entrance near the Rokach bridge area.

By car, parking is available in the Ganei Yehoshua car parks on the northern side of the park, and along Rokach Boulevard. Weekend parking fills quickly — arrive before 10am on Fridays or Saturdays if driving.

## What's in the park

### Cycling and running paths

The park's flat terrain and well-maintained paved paths make it one of Tel Aviv's premier cycling destinations. The main cycling route runs east–west along the river for the full 4-kilometre length of the park; smaller loops branch off through the lawns and garden areas.

Tel-O-Fun bikes work well for the western section; rental bikes are also available from several concession stands inside the park. Cyclists share paths with joggers, walkers and rollerbladers — the paths are wide enough that this generally works, though weekend mornings can feel like a polite but dense parade.

For runners, Hayarkon Park is one of the city's main training circuits. A 3-kilometre loop around the main river section is a popular standard route; longer stretches east or across the full park can extend this to 8–10 kilometres.

### Boat and kayak rental on the Yarkon River

The Yarkon River is calm and wide through the park, and **boat rental** is one of the park's most popular activities. You can rent rowing boats, pedal boats and kayaks at the central rental area near the Kanyon Ha'ir entertainment complex, typically for 30- or 60-minute sessions.

Water quality in the Yarkon has improved significantly since major clean-up efforts in the 2000s, though swimming is still not recommended. The rental experience is primarily about being on the river rather than in it: a gentle paddle through the park's tree-lined waterway with families, couples and the occasional competitive kayaker.

**Practical note:** Boat rental is popular on weekends. Arrive early (before 10:30am on Fridays) or expect a queue.

### Bird Sanctuary

Hayarkon Park contains one of Israel's best urban birdwatching sites: a dedicated **bird sanctuary** in the central section that hosts over 170 recorded species. Israel sits on the great Afro-European migration corridor, and the park's wetland zone and river vegetation attract significant numbers of migratory species in spring (March–May) and autumn (September–November).

Year-round residents include herons, kingfishers, cormorants, white-breasted kingfishers and a large flock of ring-necked parakeets (an introduced species now firmly established in Tel Aviv). The sanctuary has a dedicated viewing path and basic information boards.

Binoculars are worthwhile; the bird sanctuary section is least crowded on weekday mornings.

### Tropical Garden and Cactus Garden

The **Tropical Garden** (Gan HaTripiim) in the park's eastern section is a small but well-curated botanical collection of tropical and subtropical plants from around the world — rubber trees, tree ferns, palms, bamboo and flowering tropical shrubs. A modest admission fee applies (check the Tel Aviv Municipality site for current pricing; typically ₪15–25 per adult).

Alongside it, the **Cactus Garden** (Gan HaCactusim) contains one of the region's more unusual botanical collections: hundreds of cactus species and other succulents from arid environments around the world, including some striking specimen plants several metres high. The collection reflects Israel's own desert heritage — the Negev and Arava deserts host significant native succulents — and the garden has a pleasing otherworldly quality.

### Rocks (climbing wall)

The **HaTsuk** (The Cliff) artificial climbing wall on the western section of the park is one of Tel Aviv's main outdoor climbing venues: a multi-height concrete climbing wall with routes for beginners through to advanced climbers, plus bouldering sections. Equipment rental is available on-site. The wall is particularly popular with young climbers and families with older children on weekend mornings.

### Mini-golf and children's areas

Mini-golf courses, playgrounds and activity areas are spread through the central and western sections of the park. These are aimed primarily at families and run at moderate prices. The weekend atmosphere around these areas is quintessentially Israeli: relaxed, loud, family-oriented and good-natured. Picnic tables and grassed areas surround the activity zones.

### Ganei Yehoshua concert venue

The eastern end of the park opens into **Ganei Yehoshua**, one of Israel's largest outdoor concert venues. This is where Tel Aviv's biggest shows happen — stadium-capacity outdoor events drawing tens of thousands of attendees. When a major concert is running, the entire northeastern corner of the park transforms: shuttle buses from central TLV, food and merchandise stalls, and the sound of the venue audible from several streets away.

Check local listings (Bandsintown, Ticketmaster Israel, or the park's official events page) before your visit if large crowds would be an issue. Away from event nights, Ganei Yehoshua's open lawns are excellent for a quieter picnic.

## When to visit

Hayarkon Park is open year-round and genuinely enjoyable in all seasons:

| Season | Character |
|---|---|
| Spring (Mar–May) | Best birdwatching; wildflowers; warm but not hot; ideal cycling conditions |
| Summer (Jun–Sep) | Hot (28–34°C); early morning visits essential; very busy on weekends; evening picnics popular after 18:00 |
| Autumn (Oct–Nov) | Migration season returns; comfortable temperatures; school-term weekdays are quiet |
| Winter (Dec–Feb) | Mild (12–18°C); lush and green after rains; fewer visitors; Yarkon River runs higher |

**Best times for a quiet visit:** Sunday to Thursday mornings in spring or autumn. Worst for crowds: Friday and Saturday afternoons in summer.

## Practical information

- **Opening hours:** The park itself is open 24/7. Individual facilities (gardens, boat rental, climbing wall) typically open 9:00–10:00am and close at dusk or by 18:00–20:00 depending on the season.
- **Entry:** Free for the main park. Individual activities and gardens charge separately.
- **Food:** Kiosks, café stands and snack vendors are scattered through the park. The largest concentration is near the Kanyon Ha'ir area. Alternatively, pick up supplies at the Carmel Market or a supermarket and bring a picnic — the park has no shortage of lawns and tables.
- **Toilets:** Public facilities are available near the main activity areas. Bring hand sanitiser for quieter sections.
- **Cycling:** Helmets are not legally mandatory in Israel but strongly recommended. Tel-O-Fun bikes can be used for the first 30 minutes free with a registered account.
- **Dogs:** Dogs are welcome in most park areas on a lead; there are dedicated off-lead zones. The park has drinking water stations for dogs.
- **Accessibility:** The main cycling and walking paths are flat and paved. The Tropical Garden has some narrow paths and steps. The park is broadly wheelchair-accessible on the main routes.

## Combining Hayarkon Park with other Tel Aviv sights

Hayarkon Park sits naturally between two other half-day destinations:

**North:** The Tel Aviv Port (Namal Tel Aviv) is a 10-minute walk or cycle from the park's western end — good coffee, Saturday farmer's market, casual dining and a pleasant boardwalk.

**South:** The cycling path south from the park's beach-side entrance runs along the seafront all the way to Jaffa (about 5 kilometres) — past Gordon Beach, Frishman Beach, Hilton Beach, and eventually Old Jaffa.

A practical half-day combines a Hayarkon Park morning (bird sanctuary + cycling + picnic lunch) with an afternoon cycling south along the beachfront to Jaffa for sunset. Rent a bike at the park and drop it at a Tel-O-Fun dock near the Jaffa Clock Tower.

For families, pairing Hayarkon Park with the nearby **Tel Aviv Museum of Art** (about 1.5km south) makes a cultural-natural day out without crossing the city.
