For most travellers, the Israeli passport stamp is a non-issue: Israel does not routinely stamp passports at Ben Gurion Airport and has not done so for over a decade. But for anyone planning a multi-country Middle East trip — or visiting a destination where evidence of Israel travel is a concern — understanding what does and does not end up in your passport matters.

**This is general guidance. Always check your home government's current official travel advisory for any destination before you book. Entry requirements change with geopolitics and cannot be guaranteed by any travel guide.**

## Ben Gurion Airport: no stamp since around 2013

Since approximately 2013, Israel has not placed a stamp directly in passports for arrivals at Ben Gurion International Airport. Instead, you receive a **paper entry slip** at passport control — a small card noting your name, nationality, passport number, entry date and permitted length of stay.

The slip is **not optional** and is **not a courtesy**. Keep it with you for the entire trip. You will likely be asked to present it at:

- Hotel check-in (standard practice across Israel, especially Jerusalem)
- Checkpoints in the West Bank / Palestinian Authority areas
- Some internal bus routes and tourist attractions
- Departure when you leave

Losing the slip does not prevent you from leaving the country, but it adds processing time and questions at the departure terminal. If you lose yours, report it to the nearest Population and Immigration Authority office.

## Land border crossings: a more complicated picture

The no-stamp policy applies to Israel's own actions — but land borders involve **two countries**, and the neighbouring country stamps its own passports on its own side.

### Jordan: Allenby Bridge / King Hussein Bridge

The Allenby Bridge (also called the King Hussein Bridge) is the main crossing between the West Bank and Jordan. The Jordanian border officers stamp their own passport — **in Jordanian territory** — and that stamp will name the crossing. Since Allenby Bridge is exclusively used for the West Bank / Israel approach, the Jordanian stamp is effectively a record of the journey.

Some travellers request a separate paper entry rather than a in-passport Jordanian stamp. This is **sometimes accommodated** but not guaranteed. Ask at the Jordanian crossing booth.

### Jordan: Wadi Araba / Yitzhak Rabin crossing (Eilat–Aqaba)

The Wadi Araba crossing connects Eilat and Aqaba. Jordanian entry officers here generally stamp passports as standard. Again, a paper entry request is worth attempting.

### Egypt: Taba crossing

The Taba / Eilat crossing into Egypt stamps Egyptian passports on the Egyptian side and notes the Taba crossing point — which implies the Israeli-side origin. Many travellers use Taba for a Sinai / Sharm el-Sheikh extension to their Israel trip.

**Important:** Check your home government's current travel advisory for the Sinai region before planning this crossing — North Sinai and South Sinai have different risk profiles under most Western advisories, and advice can change. See [is Israel safe to visit?](/is-israel-safe) for Israeli-side context, and consult your own government's advisory for the Egyptian side.

## The practical reality: paper slip ≠ invisible visit

The absence of a stamp in your passport does **not** make your Israel visit invisible to determined border officers at future destinations. The following may be checked or asked about:

- **Neighbouring country stamps**: a Jordanian Allenby Bridge stamp, an Egyptian Taba stamp, or an Amman Queen Alia Airport stamp appearing in the same trip as a Jordan Pass purchase are all contextually readable.
- **Phone and device checks**: officers at certain border crossings occasionally check phone content — apps, photos, maps history, WhatsApp conversations, receipts, and recent web searches. This is a documented practice at some crossings. It is rare, but it does happen.
- **Airline records**: some border control systems query passenger name records and flight histories. A flight from Tel Aviv Ben Gurion (IATA: TLV) is part of your travel history.
- **Direct questioning**: security officers can and do ask travellers directly whether they have visited Israel.

This context does not mean travel to Israel is incompatible with other destinations — it means that a strategy based solely on "no stamp = no problem" has limitations.

## Where evidence of Israel travel is not a concern

Several countries that are neighbours of or partners with Israel impose no restrictions based on Israel travel history:

- **Jordan** — full peace treaty since 1994; Israel visits are routine and expected
- **Egypt** — peace treaty since 1979; Israel visitors cross Taba regularly
- **United Arab Emirates** — Abraham Accords normalisation 2020; direct El Al flights operate
- **Bahrain** — Abraham Accords normalisation 2020; diplomatic relations
- **Morocco** — normalisation agreement 2020
- **Oman** — has historically maintained quiet diplomatic channels; receiving Israeli tourists
- **Kosovo** — bilateral diplomatic relations; no restrictions

Direct flights between Israel and these countries operate openly. Evidence of a visit to any of them is treated normally.

## Where travellers sometimes exercise caution

A group of countries — primarily in the Arab League and parts of Southeast Asia — have historically restricted entry to travellers whose passports show evidence of Israel travel, or have required additional scrutiny. **This list changes with geopolitics.** The Abraham Accords and subsequent normalisation agreements have shifted the landscape significantly since 2020.

**Never rely on any static travel guide — including this one — as the definitive authority on a specific country's current entry policy.** The accurate source is always your **home government's official travel advisory** (for example, the US State Department at travel.state.gov, the UK FCDO at gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice, or Australia's Smartraveller at smartraveller.gov.au). Policies can change quickly, and what was true two years ago may not be true today.

## Practical tips for multi-country itineraries

**Visit Israel last.** If your itinerary includes Israel and a destination where you want to minimise paperwork questions, schedule Israel as the final stop before flying home. This means the Jordanian or Egyptian stamps appear before the Israel leg, not after.

**At land borders, ask for a paper slip.** You can request that both the Israeli and Jordanian (or Egyptian) sides issue paper entries rather than passport stamps. Israel routinely does this; the neighbouring country may or may not accommodate. Make the request at the first booth, before the officer opens your passport.

**Keep your ETA-IL approval handy.** The ETA-IL document (required since January 2025 for most visa-exempt travellers — see [Israel visa & ETA-IL requirements](/visa-information)) is a useful document to have accessible at border crossings, since it confirms you were authorised for legitimate tourist travel.

**Check your phone.** If you're heading to a destination where phone checks at the border are a documented practice, be aware that Israel-related apps, maps searches, WhatsApp chats, and receipt photos are potential evidence regardless of your passport's contents. This is a personal decision — many travellers take no precautions and have no issues; others delete apps and clear history. Research your specific destination's current practices.

**Consult border-crossing communities.** Online communities of recent travellers (Lonely Planet's Thorn Tree, Reddit r/solotravel, Reddit r/travel) contain up-to-date reports from travellers who have made specific crossings in the past weeks. Their experiences are more current than any published guide.

## The bottom line

For the vast majority of travellers — including those visiting Jordan, Egypt and the Gulf states — Israel's no-stamp policy at Ben Gurion and the paper entry slip mean that your passport contains no direct record of an Israel visit. For anyone planning a complex multi-country itinerary involving destinations where such evidence might be a concern, the practical advice is consistent: **visit Israel last, request paper slips at land borders, and verify current entry policies with your own government's official travel advisory before booking**.
