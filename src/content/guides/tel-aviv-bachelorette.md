Tel Aviv is a genuinely world-class destination for a bachelorette or hen party. It has the Mediterranean beach that Ibiza promises; a food and cocktail scene that genuinely matches any European capital; a warm, body-positive, LGBTQ-friendly culture that makes everyone feel welcome; and the novelty factor of being somewhere most of the group will never have been. Here is how to plan it well.

---

## Why Tel Aviv works for a group trip

- **Compact city** — beach, market, Neve Tzedek restaurants and Florentin bars are all walkable or a short taxi ride apart. No car needed.
- **Long seasons** — April–May and September–October give warm-sea swimming without the August crush. Spring especially has perfect evening temperatures for al-fresco drinking.
- **Nightlife starts late** — bars fill from 22:00; clubs after midnight. The Israeli weekend runs Friday–Saturday, making Thursday evening a serious night out.
- **Flights from Europe** — direct from most major European cities in 4–5 hours. Several carriers (easyJet, Ryanair, Wizz Air, El Al) now serve Tel Aviv from UK and continental hubs.

## Suggested 3-night itinerary

### Day 1 — Jaffa arrival + Neve Tzedek evening

Fly in, check in, decompress. The [Jaffa port](/jaffa-travel-guide) neighbourhood is an ideal first evening: walk the old Ottoman alleyways, browse the galleries, watch the sunset from the port promenade, then move to the Neve Tzedek restaurant strip for dinner. The neighbourhood's converted stone-cottage aesthetic makes for excellent group photos. The [Carmel Market](/tel-aviv-carmel-market) is a 15-minute walk if anyone wants to pick up fresh fruit, tahini or wine.

### Day 2 — Beach morning + cooking class afternoon

Arrive at **Gordon Beach** or **Hilton Beach** by 9–10am before it fills up. Sun lounger hire runs from beach operators at the northern end of the strip (live rates vary by season). Bring a wireless speaker, sunscreen and a cooler if you want to self-cater; there are beach bars and snack kiosks for everything else.

In the afternoon, a group **Israeli cooking class** at a Carmel Market kitchen or Jaffa-area studio is the standout shared experience. Most 3-hour sessions teach mezze-style Israeli dishes — hummus from scratch, shakshuka, various salads, burekas — which suits groups of mixed cooking ability. The shared table at the end doubles as lunch or an early dinner. Sessions typically run Friday morning or Sunday morning for the freshest market produce, but afternoon slots exist. Book through the links above for real-time availability and group pricing.

Evening: back to Florentin for the cocktail-bar circuit.

### Day 3 — Dead Sea spa day (optional) or Tel Aviv relaxed

A Dead Sea spa day is the classic add-on for a wellness-focused group. The drive from Tel Aviv is 90 minutes each way; tour operators handle transport, entry and towel hire. The combination of floating in the mineral-rich water and covering your body in the famous black mud has genuine sensory novelty. Ein Bokek resort hotels along the southern shore sell day-pass access (pool, beach, mud, shower facilities) to non-guests; the experience is best in spring or autumn when the heat is manageable. For the group logistics, a private minibus from a [tour operator](/best-holy-land-tours) is the most practical option.

If the group prefers to stay in the city, the [Sarona Market](/sarona-market-tel-aviv) food hall makes a pleasant brunch stop, and the Rothschild Boulevard–White City area rewards a gentle morning stroll before a final afternoon on the beach.

### Day 4 — Late checkout + Carmel Market farewell

Most Tel Aviv direct flights to Europe depart early evening, so a final Carmel Market run for take-home gifts (za'atar spice mixes, tahini, Medjool dates, Dead Sea cosmetics, local wine) before heading to the airport works well. Allow 90 minutes from Tel Aviv city centre to Ben Gurion Airport on the express rail; check your ticket for the terminal.

## Florentin cocktail bars — the essential circuit

[Florentin](/tel-aviv/florentin) is the dense, walkable heart of Tel Aviv's bar scene. The streets between Florentin Street and Vital Street concentrate more bars per block than anywhere else in the city. The neighbourhood's look is exposed brick, neon signage and mismatched furniture; the vibe is genuinely local rather than tourist-packaged.

The bar scene in Florentin changes constantly — venues open, rebrand and close. Check current listings via the [Spotimeapp](https://spotimeapp.com) platform or the Facebook events feeds of individual bars for what is actually running on your dates. A local-guided bar tour (bookable above) is the most reliable way to hit the good spots on a first visit without wasting time on places that have declined or closed.

**Practical notes for bar evenings:**
- Dress code is relaxed everywhere in Florentin; smart casual is more than enough even for the more fashionable rooftop venues.
- Bars fill from 22:00; expect queues at popular spots from 23:30 onwards on Thursday, Friday and Saturday.
- Alcohol prices: expect ₪50–90 for a cocktail at most Florentin bars; craft beer ₪30–50. Local Israeli wines are often the best-value order.
- Food before bars: eat by 21:00 — the kitchens at the better restaurants close around 22:30.
- Transport home: the Gett app (Israeli equivalent of Uber) operates group taxis; order a 7-seater for groups of 5–6. Have it installed and an account ready before the trip.

## Rooftop sundowners before the main event

Tel Aviv has an outstanding rooftop bar scene for the golden-hour window before the Florentin crawl begins. The Mamilla Hotel (Jerusalem) rooftop is the most dramatic in the country for panoramic views over the Old City — worth building a day trip around if the group can get to Jerusalem for a night. In Tel Aviv itself, hotel rooftop bars on the Tayelet promenade and Rothschild Boulevard catch the sea-horizon sunset. See the [rooftop bars guide](/israel-rooftop-bars) for current recommendations.

## Wellness and spa options in Tel Aviv

For groups who want a spa morning rather than (or before) the beach:

- **Hotel spa day-passes** — several large beach hotels on the Tayelet (e.g. Dan Tel Aviv, David InterContinental) sell day passes to their spa facilities including pool, sauna and treatment rooms. Book ahead — group slots fill on weekends. Live pricing via the hotel websites.
- **Boutique spa studios** — Neve Tzedek and Rothschild Boulevard have a cluster of independent spa studios offering group facial and massage packages. Treatments run 60–90 minutes; group bookings typically need 4+ people and 2–3 weeks advance notice.
- **Dead Sea day trip** — see Day 3 above. The combination of flotation, black mud ritual and swimming pool access at Ein Bokek is the highest-novelty wellness experience available from Tel Aviv in a single day.

## Practical logistics

| Detail | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **Airport to city** | Ben Gurion Airport → Tel Aviv: 30 min by express rail (₪30/person); 45 min by taxi (~₪220–280 for a large group taxi — use Gett app at the taxi stand) |
| **Within the city** | Walk Neve Tzedek ↔ Florentin ↔ Carmel Market; Gett app for anything further or after midnight |
| **Group sizes** | Most hotel rooms sleep 2; boutique properties can often reserve adjacent rooms for a group block. Apartments via short-stay platforms work well for groups of 6–10 wanting a shared living space |
| **Currency** | Israeli shekel (₪). Contactless card payment works widely; cash useful for market stalls and small bars |
| **Language** | English widely spoken in hotels, restaurants and tour contexts |
| **Entry** | Most European and North American passport holders need an ETA-IL (Israel Electronic Travel Authorisation, ₪25, apply online at least 72h before travel) — see the [ETA-IL guide](/visa-information) for your nationality |
| **Shabbat** | Tel Aviv is secular; restaurants, bars and shops continue operating Friday evening through Saturday |
