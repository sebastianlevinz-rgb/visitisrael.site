For millions of Jewish families worldwide, a trip to Israel is not only a journey to a living country — it is a journey into family history. Whether your ancestors came from Eastern Europe, Yemen, Iraq, Morocco or dozens of other origin communities, Israel holds the world's deepest concentration of Jewish genealogical resources. Three institutions in particular — the **ANU Museum's genealogy center** in Tel Aviv, **Yad Vashem** in Jerusalem, and the **National Library of Israel** — offer research access and expert guidance that no other country can match.

This guide explains what each institution offers, how to prepare before you arrive, and how to structure a visit that combines meaningful research with the living experience of being in Israel.

---

## Prepare at home first

The most common mistake genealogy travelers make is arriving in Israel without preparation. The research staff at all three institutions are expert and generous with their time — but they can do far more for you if you walk in having already done your homework.

### Build your baseline on JewishGen.org

[JewishGen.org](https://www.jewishgen.org/) is the global hub for Jewish genealogical research and the essential starting point. Before booking flights, spend several sessions:

- **ShtetlSeeker** to find your family's town of origin by historical name (many towns appear under Russian Empire, Austro-Hungarian or Polish names that no longer exist)
- **JGSS database** (Jewish Genealogical Society searches, pooled from hundreds of communities)
- **JRI-Poland, Lithuania, Romania, Ukraine databases** for vital records from Eastern European communities
- **Yizkor books** — community memorial books written by survivors, often in Yiddish, covering towns that were destroyed; English summaries are available for many

### Gather family documents

Bring copies — not originals — of any documents in your possession: immigration papers, naturalization records, old photographs, letters, ketubot (marriage contracts), even handwritten notes. The institutional researchers will help you interpret Hebrew, Yiddish, Ladino and Russian-script documents that family members may have assumed were unreadable.

### Search Yad Vashem in advance

The [Central Database of Shoah Victims' Names](https://yadvashem.org/database.html) is freely searchable online. Do this before your trip: note which relatives appear, their entry types (Page of Testimony, transport list, community record), and any gaps. When you sit with a Yad Vashem researcher in person, you will be able to ask specific questions rather than starting from scratch.

## ANU — Museum of the Jewish People, Tel Aviv

The **ANU Museum of the Jewish People** (formerly Beit Hatfutsot) at Tel Aviv University campus is the world's most comprehensive museum of Jewish diaspora history — and it houses a genealogical resource without parallel anywhere in the world.

### Douglas E. Goldman Jewish Genealogy Center

The **Douglas E. Goldman Jewish Genealogy Center** at ANU offers:

- **Community databases** covering over 5,000 Jewish communities from every continent
- **Name databases** drawing on immigration records, community registers, Holocaust memorials and genealogical submissions from around the world
- **Surname search tools** that trace family names across emigration corridors (Eastern Europe → United States, Argentina, Australia, Israel)
- **Expert staff** who conduct in-person research consultations at no charge (appointment required)
- **Digitized community memorial books** (Yizkor books), community maps and archival photographs

Many visitors who found nothing online discover significant family connections with in-person assistance. The staff know which databases are incomplete, which community records were preserved and which were destroyed, and they can suggest parallel research paths that online databases do not surface.

**Practical details:** The genealogy center is a free service within the ANU Museum. Museum admission is charged separately (₪60–70 adult as of 2026; check anumuseum.org.il for current prices). **Book your research consultation weeks in advance**, especially during peak months (March–May, September–November). The full museum is worth a half-day in its own right: the "Golden Path" central exhibition traces Jewish diaspora identity from ancient Israel through to contemporary Israeli society.

**Getting there:** Tel Aviv University campus, Gate 2, Ramat Aviv. Buses from central Tel Aviv (Dizengoff Centre area) take around 25 minutes. Taxi or ride-share from the city center is 15–20 minutes.

## Yad Vashem, Jerusalem

**Yad Vashem** — the World Holocaust Remembrance Center on Jerusalem's Mount Herzl — is the primary address for Holocaust-era genealogical research. Even for families whose relatives survived, Yad Vashem holds community-level records that document the world their ancestors left.

### Central Database of Shoah Victims' Names

The database currently documents approximately **4.8 million names** drawn from:

- **Pages of Testimony** — personal testimony forms submitted by survivors and relatives, often with photos and biographical detail
- **Nazi administrative records** — transport lists, ghetto records, camp records
- **Community memorial books** (Yizkor books)
- **Post-war community registers** and survivor testimony

The database is large but incomplete: entire communities that were destroyed before any records could be preserved are not represented, and many victims have been documented multiple times under different name spellings. Finding nothing does not mean your relative is not documented — it may mean their record has not yet been submitted. Yad Vashem actively solicits new submissions from families worldwide.

### What in-person research adds

The online database shows you name, dates and the type of document. In-person at Yad Vashem you can:

- Request **associated documents** — if a Page of Testimony exists, you can see the physical form and any attached photographs
- Access **pre-war community photographs and maps** that place the victim in their world before the war
- Consult **the Hall of Names** — the repository of submitted Pages of Testimony, with viewing access for researchers
- Speak with **research staff** who know which community collections were digitized versus held in physical archives only

**Practical details:** Free admission. **Advance timed-entry registration is mandatory** for the Holocaust History Museum (yad-vashem.org); walk-in access to the grounds, Children's Memorial and Hall of Names is generally available. Guided tours require booking weeks or months ahead. Allow a minimum of three to four hours; a full day is better if you plan to use the research facilities. Yad Vashem is closed on Saturdays. Photography is not permitted inside the Holocaust History Museum.

**Getting there:** Western Jerusalem, Har Herzl. Jerusalem Light Rail (Ram line) to Mount Herzl station, then a 10-minute uphill walk. Buses 27 and 68 from the city center. Taxi from the Old City takes 15–20 minutes.

## National Library of Israel, Jerusalem

The **National Library of Israel** — housed in its striking new [Herzog & de Meuron building](https://nli.org.il/en/visit) on Jerusalem's Givat Ram campus (adjacent to the Israel Museum and the Knesset) — offers genealogical resources that complement the ANU and Yad Vashem with different strengths.

### What the NLI holds for genealogists

- **Historical Hebrew and Yiddish newspapers** — for name searches, obituaries, immigration announcements and community news from the 19th and early 20th centuries
- **Landsmanshaft records** — archives of the immigrant mutual-aid societies that documented community members across emigration corridors
- **Rare manuscript collections** — correspondence, responsa literature and communal records from pre-modern communities
- **Genealogical reference librarians** who provide free in-person research guidance sessions

The NLI's collection is strongest for communities with significant Hebrew-language documentation — particularly those from the Ottoman-era Levant, Yemen, Iraq, and the early Zionist immigration waves — and for 19th–20th century Eastern European communities that had a Hebrew-language press.

**Practical details:** The public galleries and reading rooms are open Sunday–Thursday; hours change — check nli.org.il/en/visit for current schedule. General public areas are free; some collections require reader registration. **Advance registration is required for reading room access and research consultations**; request this through the NLI website. Photography in public areas is permitted; reading rooms have their own rules.

**Getting there:** Eliezer Kaplan St 1, Givat Ram. A 10-minute walk from the Israel Museum. Jerusalem Light Rail to the Central Station / Binyanei HaUma stop, then a short taxi or 20-minute walk. The Israel Museum and Wohl Rose Garden sit immediately adjacent, making a combined half-day visit natural.

## Suggested research itineraries

### Two-day genealogy focus

**Day 1 — Jerusalem**
- **Morning:** Yad Vashem (timed entry 9–11am; allow 2–3 hours for the Holocaust History Museum, then research session or Hall of Names visit)
- **Early afternoon:** Mount Herzl adjacent — 30-minute walk through Israel's national cemetery before returning to the city
- **Late afternoon:** National Library of Israel, Givat Ram (pre-booked research consultation; 1.5–2 hours)
- **Evening:** Dinner in the Mahane Yehuda market area

**Day 2 — Tel Aviv**
- **Morning–afternoon:** ANU Museum of the Jewish People (pre-booked genealogy center consultation 10am–12pm; museum galleries 12–2pm; allow 3.5 hours total)
- **Late afternoon:** Free time in Tel Aviv — Jaffa, the beachfront or Carmel Market

### Combining research with heritage travel (seven days)

A seven-day trip structure that weaves genealogy into a broader [Jewish heritage](/jewish-heritage-israel) experience:

- **Days 1–2:** Jerusalem — Western Wall and Jewish Quarter, Old City orientation
- **Day 3:** Yad Vashem AM + National Library PM (research day in Jerusalem)
- **Day 4:** [Masada](/dead-sea/masada) and the Dead Sea
- **Day 5:** Tel Aviv — ANU Museum research day + Jaffa afternoon
- **Day 6:** Safed and the Galilee — the city of Kabbalah with one of Israel's best-preserved historic Jewish quarters
- **Day 7:** Departure from Ben Gurion Airport

## Honesty note

Genealogical research does not always yield the results you hope for. The databases are large but incomplete: communities destroyed before records could be preserved are often not represented; many victims were documented under different name spellings or not at all; and diaspora records vary enormously by origin community. **Finding nothing is common and does not mean your family had no history in the documented communities.**

The research staff at all three institutions are experienced in delivering this reality honestly and compassionately — they are also expert at suggesting alternative search paths, related surname clusters, and parallel archives in Israel and abroad. Approach your visit with curiosity rather than a fixed expectation, and allow extra time so the search itself can be meaningful.
