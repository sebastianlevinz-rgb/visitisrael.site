**Beit She'an** is the most complete Roman city ruin in Israel — a place where fallen columns, mosaic pavements, a standing 7,000-seat theater, and a 1,300-year-old earthquake layer combine into one of the most viscerally powerful archaeological experiences in the country. Most visitors on the Galilee circuit pass it by; those who stop for two hours rarely forget it.

The city's ancient name was **Nysa-Scythopolis** — one of the ten cities of the Roman **Decapolis** league, and the only one on the western side of the Jordan River. At its peak in the 4th–6th centuries CE it held a population estimated at 30,000–40,000, with a forum, theater, bathhouses, a nymphaeum, a colonnaded main street and an elevated ceremonial area above the older Philistine tel. The earthquake of 749 CE ended it in a night.

## Why Beit She'an is unmissable

### The earthquake that preserved everything

On 18 January 749 CE, the **Galilee earthquake** — one of the largest seismic events in eastern Mediterranean history — struck at night, toppling the colonnaded streets of Scythopolis while most of the population was indoors. The columns fell outward along the cardo in a sequence now clearly visible in the excavated ruins, their capitals still attached, lying exactly where they landed.

This disaster created an extraordinary archaeological gift: **a city preserved mid-collapse**, its materials too numerous and scattered for later builders to scavenge effectively. Excavations from the 1920s through the 1980s (and ongoing to today) have exposed a remarkably complete civic centre from the 4th to early 8th centuries CE — streets, shops, temples, a bath complex, public latrines, and the grand theater — all frozen at the moment of destruction.

### The biblical tel above the ruins

Rising 80 metres above the Roman remains is a **Philistine-era mound** (the *tel* itself) with 18 distinct occupation strata reaching back to the Chalcolithic period (~4000 BCE). The tel is where **Saul's body was hung** on the city walls after his defeat and death at the Battle of Gilboa (1 Samuel 31:10–12) — one of the most geographically precise biblical narratives, and one that can be read standing on the tel with the Gilboa range visible to the south-west.

Egyptian, Canaanite, Israelite, Assyrian, Persian, Hellenistic and Roman layers are all represented in the mound. The tel is less excavated than the Roman city below it but offers the best panoramic views — and the most direct sense of why this valley junction was occupied continuously for 6,000 years.

## What to see in the national park

### The Roman theater

The **theater** is the first major structure you encounter and the site's defining image: a semicircular cavea with seating for approximately 7,000 spectators, cut into the hillside of the tel, with the stage wall (scaena frons) rising to three stories. The theater dates to the 2nd century CE, was modified in the 3rd and 4th centuries, and was still functioning when the earthquake struck.

The seated tiers are largely intact; the stage floor and orchestra pit are excavated; the lower seating sections are stabilised and can be walked. **Allow 20–30 minutes here** — the space is genuinely dramatic, and the acoustics (test them) are still remarkable. Some live performances are staged here in summer; check events at parks.org.il.

### The colonnaded cardo

From the theater, the excavated city stretches south along the **cardo maximus** — the Roman main street, here approximately 8 metres wide and lined with column bases and the columns themselves, most lying exactly where the earthquake dropped them. The visual impact of fallen columns stretching for hundreds of metres is the defining image of Beit She'an for most visitors.

At the south end of the cardo, the **intersection with the decumanus** (east-west cross-street) is marked by columns and a nymphaeum — a monumental public fountain complex. The mosaic floor of the surrounding colonnaded gallery (the sigma-shaped semi-circular portico) is partially preserved and visible behind protective fencing.

### The bathhouse and public latrine

The **western bathhouse** is one of the best-preserved Roman bath complexes in Israel: the changing room (apodyterium), cold pool (frigidarium), warm room (tepidarium), and heated rooms (caldaria) are all identifiable from floor plans visible at the site. The hypocaust system (raised floor with brick pillars for hot-air circulation) is visible in the heated rooms.

Adjacent to the bathhouse is the **public latrine** (foricae) — a large communal facility seating approximately 50, with a marble bench running around three sides above a continuous water channel. These are among the best-preserved examples in the Levant and an unexpectedly compelling stop on any Roman-history tour.

### The Sylvanus mosaic and civic buildings

The excavated area between the theater and the tel includes a cluster of **civic buildings** from the late Byzantine period (5th–6th centuries CE), including a building with a well-preserved mosaic floor bearing a Greek inscription naming the city magistrate Sylvanus — giving the structure its modern name. The level of craft in the geometric mosaic borders is representative of the quality of public works in Scythopolis at its height.

### The tel summit

A path (moderately strenuous, allow 15–20 minutes each way) climbs the tel to an **excavation site and panoramic platform** at the summit. The views cover the full Jordan Valley to the east, the Gilboa mountains to the south-west, the Harod Valley (the route of Gideon's army in Judges 7) opening to the west, and the hills of Samaria and lower Galilee framing the scene. The excavated layers of the tel — Egyptian mudbrick, Israelite stone, Hellenistic walls — are visible in the exposed sections.

This is the most historically resonant spot on the site: from this vantage point, every military campaign from Thutmose III (1458 BCE) to Saladin (1183 CE) that passed through the valley can be traced.

## Combining Beit She'an with Gan HaShlosha (Sachne)

The most popular day-trip combination pairs Beit She'an with **Gan HaShlosha National Park** (known as *Sachne*, from the Arabic for "warm") 3 km to the south-west.

Sachne is a series of large natural pools fed by the **Harod River springs**, which emerge at a constant **28°C year-round** from the Gilboa aquifer. The pools — linked by small waterfalls, with grass banks, shade trees and a small children's water park — are one of Israel's finest freshwater swimming sites. The deeper pools have diving rocks; the shallower areas are suitable for young children.

**Recommended sequence:** Arrive at Beit She'an by 08:00–08:30 (before the heat peaks in summer, before the coach groups arrive in spring); spend 2–2.5 hours in the ruins; drive 10 minutes to Sachne; swim and picnic through the afternoon. The Gan HaShlosha archaeological garden adjacent to the pools has Canaanite-era remains worth a 20-minute walk.

Both sites are **covered by the INPA annual pass**.

## The Jezreel and Jordan Valley circuit {#valley-circuit}

Beit She'an anchors an excellent **full-day circuit** of the northern Jordan Valley and Jezreel plain that can be done as a day trip from Haifa, Nazareth, Tiberias or even Tel Aviv:

| Site | Distance from Beit She'an | Highlight | INPA Pass |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Beit Alfa Byzantine Synagogue** | 10 km west (Route 669) | 6th-century CE mosaic zodiac wheel — finest in Israel | Yes |
| **Gan HaShlosha / Sachne** | 3 km south-west | Spring-fed 28°C natural swimming pools | Yes |
| **Tel Megiddo (Armageddon)** | 30 km west (Route 669 to 65) | 26-layer UNESCO archaeological mound; water tunnel; Jezreel panorama | Yes |
| **Zippori (Sepphoris)** | 45 km north-west | Roman mosaic "Mona Lisa of the Galilee"; Jesus's likely birthplace of Mary | Yes |
| **Sea of Galilee / Tiberias** | 40 km north (Route 90) | Boat tours, baptism sites, lakefront dining | Partly |

A practical full day from Tiberias: **Beit She'an** (morning, 08:00–10:30) → **Beit Alfa synagogue** (45 min, 10:45–11:30) → **Sachne** (lunch and swimming, 12:00–15:00) → **Megiddo** (afternoon, 15:30–17:30). Total driving: ~90 km. This circuit rewards an overnight base in Tiberias or the Sea of Galilee.

## Getting there

Beit She'an is most practical by **rental car** or a day tour. Public transport exists but limits flexibility for the Sachne and Beit Alfa additions.

**By car from Tel Aviv:** Route 2 or Route 1 to Route 6 north (toll road); exit at Junction 19 (Gilboa/Beit She'an); Route 90 south into the Jordan Valley — approximately 100 km, 1.5–2 hours.

**By car from Tiberias:** Route 90 south along the Jordan Valley — 40 km, 35–40 minutes.

**By car from Jerusalem:** Route 1 east to Jericho junction, then Route 90 north — approximately 2 hours.

**By public transport:** Egged bus 961 (Tel Aviv → Beit She'an, ~2.5 hrs), or buses 28/29/36 (Tiberias → Beit She'an, ~1 hr). The central bus station is a 10-minute walk from the national park entrance.
