**The Judean Hills** contain Israel's most historically resonant wine country: forested limestone slopes rising to 900 metres west of Jerusalem, where boutique producers have been quietly building an international reputation since the 1990s. A wine day in the Judean Hills is genuinely different from any other wine-country experience — the landscape is striking, the producers are mostly small-family operations still willing to pour for individual visitors, and the proximity to Jerusalem (30–50 minutes by car) makes it one of the most accessible wine day trips in the Middle East.

This is not a casual drop-in circuit. Most Judean Hills wineries are **appointment-only** — booking ahead is essential. But for travellers willing to plan ahead, a morning among the vines and a lunch in the nearby Ein Kerem village makes for one of Jerusalem's best half-day escapes.

---

## Why the Judean Hills produce interesting wine

The Judean Hills sit at **400–900 metres above sea level**, which gives them a climate distinct from Israel's coastal regions: cooler nights, a pronounced diurnal temperature shift (warm days, cold nights), and exposure to Mediterranean weather systems from the west. The dominant soils are **limestone and terra rossa clay** — similar to parts of Burgundy and the southern Rhône — which drain well and impart a characteristic minerality to the wines.

Israeli viticulture modernised rapidly in the 1980s, led by the Golan Heights Winery's breakthrough in temperature-controlled fermentation. The Judean Hills followed in the 1990s, with a wave of boutique producers — many with training from Burgundy, Bordeaux, or California — establishing small, quality-focused estates. Today, Judean Hills wines regularly win international medals and appear on the lists of high-end restaurants in Israel and internationally.

The region's signature style tends toward **structured reds** (Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Syrah) with firm tannins and good acidity, and **aromatic whites** (Viognier, Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay) that retain freshness from the altitude.

## Top Judean Hills wineries for visitors

### Tzora Vineyards (Shoresh)

**Tzora** is widely considered the Judean Hills' benchmark winery — a **biodynamic estate** on the ruins of a Crusader village in the Shoresh area, producing some of Israel's most internationally recognised wines. The Shoresh label (entry-level), Judean Hills (mid-tier), and Neve Ilan (single-vineyard, very limited) represent a clear quality progression. Tastings are by appointment; the vineyard setting is beautiful. About **30 minutes from central Jerusalem** via Route 1 west then Route 375 south.

*Booking:* winery website; typically weekday appointments only; groups welcome.

### Flam Winery (Beit Shemesh area)

**Flam** is a family winery now in its second generation, producing around 200,000 bottles per year — making it one of the larger Judean Hills producers, but still boutique by global standards. The Noble red blend and Classico white are their best-known wines; both are exported internationally. The tasting room is open **Friday and Saturday with advance booking** and some weekday arrangements. About **40 minutes from Jerusalem** via Route 38 south toward Beit Shemesh.

*Tip:* Flam pairs tastings with **local cheese and charcuterie** by arrangement — mention it when booking.

### Domaine du Castel (Ramat Raziel)

**Castel** was one of the first Judean Hills producers to achieve international recognition, with Serge Hochar (Chateau Musar) citing it as a benchmark. The Grand Vin and Petit Castel are the flagship labels; allocation-level wines at the top end sell out quickly each vintage. Visits are strictly **appointment-only** and harder to arrange than at some other wineries — but for serious wine enthusiasts, this is a priority stop. Located in the Ramat Raziel area, about **35 minutes from Jerusalem**.

*Booking:* winery website well in advance; limited visitor slots.

### Nevo Winery (Nataf)

**Nevo** is a small boutique producer with a reputation above its size — appointment-only, with the owner typically pouring the tastings personally. The wines focus on single-vineyard Judean Hills expressions, particularly Cabernet Franc and Merlot. A tasting at Nevo feels more like a private visit to a passionate winemaker than a commercial tasting room experience.

*Booking:* contact directly; limited to small groups; essential to book weeks ahead.

### Psagot Winery (Psagot)

**Psagot** is one of the Judean Hills' better-organised visitor wineries, with a visitor centre, scheduled tastings, and panoramic views over the Benjamin region. Note the location: Psagot is in an Israeli community in the Binyamin region, **in the West Bank** (Israeli-administered; see the FAQ above on the political context). Visitors should check current travel advisories for their nationality before planning. The winery produces well-regarded reds and has invested significantly in visitor infrastructure.

*Visiting:* More walk-in friendly than most Judean Hills wineries, though booking ahead is still recommended.

## A self-drive day-trip route from Jerusalem

A practical half-day wine circuit from Jerusalem covering 3 wineries:

**Morning start (08:30):** Drive west from Jerusalem on Route 1 toward Tel Aviv. After approximately 15 km, take the Route 375 exit toward Shoresh. **Tzora Vineyards** (Shoresh) is the first stop — 30 minutes from the city. Arrive for your morning tasting appointment (book 09:00–10:30).

**Mid-morning (10:45):** Continue south on Route 375 to Route 38, then south toward Beit Shemesh. **Flam Winery** is approximately 20 minutes from Tzora. Arrive for your second tasting appointment (11:00–12:30). Request the cheese pairing if pre-arranged.

**Lunch (13:00):** Drive back east on Route 38 toward Jerusalem. **Ein Kerem** village (Route 386, at the western edge of Jerusalem) is an ideal lunch stop — the neighbourhood has several quality restaurants and cafés, a 12th-century Crusader church, and Hadassah Hospital's famous Chagall windows nearby.

**Afternoon:** Return to Jerusalem city centre via Route 1 east — about 20 minutes from Ein Kerem. The circuit total is approximately **90–110 km** of driving; wine tasting allows 2–3 glasses per winery at a tasting; **designate a non-drinking driver** or arrange a guided tour (recommended for groups).

## Pairing the wine circuit with other Judean Hills sights

The Judean Hills offer more than wine — the same day can combine a winery visit with:

- **[Ein Kerem village](/jerusalem-neighborhoods-guide)** — historic village absorbed into western Jerusalem; Crusader-era Church of John the Baptist; art galleries; excellent café and restaurant scene for post-tasting lunch.
- **Beit Guvrin – Maresha National Park** — UNESCO-listed underground cave complex south of Beit Shemesh, on the way back from the Beit Shemesh winery cluster.
- **Nahal Sorek Nature Reserve** — a woodland canyon walk (moderate difficulty, 2–3 hrs) near the Shoresh area; combines naturally with a late-morning winery visit.
- **[Latrun](/latrun-guide)** — Yad La-Shiryon tank museum and the Trappist monastery winery (open weekdays), 35 minutes west on Route 1 — an easy add-on to or from the Shoresh wineries.
