Haifa's food scene is quieter and less publicised than Tel Aviv's or Jerusalem's, but it reflects something the larger cities often trade away: genuine everyday cooking shaped by communities living alongside each other. The Arab-Christian neighbourhood of Wadi Nisnas has some of the finest falafel and fresh pastry in the north, open through Shabbat when much of Israel closes. The German Colony restaurant strip, laid out along a 19th-century Templer boulevard, brings serious mezze and Lebanese-influenced cooking to one of Israel's most atmospheric streets. And the port area adds a seafood dimension that the landlocked cities cannot match.

This guide covers the main food neighbourhoods, the key addresses in each, and how to plan an eating day around Haifa's particular character.

---

## Wadi Nisnas — the Arab-Christian food quarter

Wadi Nisnas is the anchor of Haifa's street food scene: a dense Arab-Christian neighbourhood of narrow alleys, mural-covered walls and family-run food businesses that have occupied the same addresses for generations. It sits in the lower city, about a 10-minute walk northeast of the German Colony, and unlike most of Haifa's commercial streets it is busy and open seven days a week — including Shabbat — because the population is Arab-Christian rather than Jewish.

**Falafel Ha'zkenim** (the Elders' Falafel) on Wadi Street is one of the most consistently cited falafel addresses in Haifa — a small, family-owned counter that has been running since the 1950s and draws a steady morning queue of regulars. The falafel here is made from a base of chickpeas and broad beans, fried to order, and served in fresh pita with an array of pickles, tahini and harif (spiced chilli sauce). Arrive before noon on weekdays; the kitchen winds down in the early afternoon.

**Em Sabri Bakery**, also in Wadi Nisnas, sells the morning staples of northern Arab bread culture: fresh pita from a wood-fired tabun oven, flatbreads topped with white jibni cheese and mahamra (a red pepper and walnut paste), and sesame rings known locally as ka'ak. The best moment is early morning when the bread is hot — a flatbread with cheese and strong tea is a complete Haifa breakfast for under ₪30.

For sweets, the neighbourhood confectioneries on and around Wadi Street sell kanafeh (warm white-cheese pastry in sugar syrup), baklava in multiple varieties (walnut, pistachio, cashew) and mamoul (date- or nut-filled semolina cookies). These are typically priced by weight — ₪50–80 per kilogram is the usual range — and sold loose for immediate eating or packaged to take home. The kanafeh is best eaten hot from the tray in the morning.

**Al-Pasha** restaurant on Jaffa Street at the lower end of Wadi Nisnas offers a sit-down version of the same food culture — Haifa-style Palestinian-Arab home cooking, including slow-cooked meats, rice dishes and a rotating selection of salads and mezze. It is a local institution rather than a tourist-facing restaurant; the menu is in Arabic and Hebrew, and a shared lunch here is an encounter with neighbourhood food culture rather than a polished dining experience.

**Practical note:** Wadi Nisnas is most alive on weekday mornings (08:00–13:00), when the market stalls, falafel counters and bread sellers are all operating together. The neighbourhood quiets in the afternoon. On Shabbat it is open and relatively calm — a good alternative to a closed Jerusalem for Saturday morning visitors.

## German Colony — Ben Gurion Avenue restaurants

At the foot of the Bahá'í Gardens axis, the **German Colony** on Ben Gurion Avenue is Haifa's best street for a sit-down meal. The stone buildings — constructed by German Templer settlers in the 1860s–1880s and carefully restored — house restaurants and wine bars that are the city's established dining addresses.

**Fattoush** is the most famous name on the boulevard: a Lebanese and Arab-Israeli mezze restaurant that has occupied its corner of Ben Gurion Avenue for decades. The menu centres on large sharing plates — hummus topped with spiced mushrooms and garlic, slow-cooked aubergine, okra in tomato sauce, spiced lamb and seasonal salads. The portions are built for sharing; order three or four plates between two for a full meal. It gets very busy on Friday and Saturday evenings; an early arrival or advance reservation is advised for weekends. Fattoush is not the place for a quick or quiet dinner — it is the place for a long, sociable one.

**Hanamal 24** (the name refers to port street number 24 in the adjacent Hanamal district, though the restaurant itself sits near the Colony) represents a newer wave of Haifa cooking: French-Israeli fusion dishes drawing on northern Israeli produce. The kitchen works with smoked aubergine, coastal fish and slow-cooked lamb, presenting them through a European technique lens. It is smaller and more intimate than Fattoush — better suited to a couple or small group who want a quieter dinner than the mezze-sharing crowd.

**Nabila** on Ben Gurion Avenue specialises in *kubeh* (also spelled kobeh or kuba), a dish of northern Iraqi-Jewish origin: torpedo-shaped dumplings of bulghur wheat filled with minced meat and onion, typically served in a sour soup (soup hamusta) or pan-fried. It is not a widely available dish outside of northern Israel; eating it at a specialist restaurant in Haifa is the clearest expression of the city's Mizrahi food culture — Iraqi-Jewish cooking passed through three generations.

**The Colony Hotel courtyard** is less a restaurant than a place to eat well and drink coffee in a genuinely historic building. The hotel's inner courtyard occupies what was the main hall of a Templer settler's home — carved stone arches, a garden and the unhurried atmosphere of a Haifa morning. The food is café-oriented (pastry, light dishes, weekend brunch) rather than a formal restaurant, but the setting is the point.

## Port and Hanamal district — seafood and waterfront

Haifa's port area, north of the German Colony, has a developing cluster of restaurants in the **Hanamal** (port) district. **Mul HaYam** (literally "Facing the Sea") is a seafood restaurant on the harbour promenade offering Israeli preparations of Mediterranean fish — whole bream and mullet grilled or roasted, with mezze accompaniments. The port view and the proximity to the working container terminal give it a distinct atmosphere from the manicured German Colony: this is a working port city's seafood restaurant, not a tourist promenade.

The Hanamal district more broadly is Haifa's up-and-coming nightlife and dining zone — a number of wine bars and casual restaurants have opened around the old warehouses. It is still evolving compared to the established Colony strip, so specific addresses change; check current listings when visiting.

## Haifa's food character

Haifa's food reflects its political and cultural identity as Israel's most coexistent major city: Jewish, Arab-Christian, Arab-Muslim and Druze communities live in close proximity, and their food traditions are all visible in the city's restaurants, markets and daily eating habits.

The result is more genuinely diverse than Tel Aviv or Jerusalem. In Wadi Nisnas you eat food that has not been adjusted for a tourist audience. In the German Colony you eat Lebanese-influenced cooking that stands on its own merits. In the port district you eat the fish of the northern Mediterranean coast.

Haifa's hummus is typically **warmer, more olive oil-forward and slightly more liquid** than the Jerusalem style — a northern Palestinian-Arab preparation that uses less tahini and more raw olive oil. It often arrives with whole chickpeas and raw onion alongside rather than the spiced fava beans of the south. Eating hummus in Wadi Nisnas is experiencing the tradition directly.

**Logistics:** Wadi Nisnas and the German Colony are about 15 minutes apart on foot through the lower city — walkable in a single morning and afternoon. The Carmelit funicular (₪7/ride) connects the lower city to the Carmel summit for the Druze-influenced food and pastry shops in the upper areas and Daliyat el-Carmel. **Haifa is the only major Israeli city with public transport on Shabbat**, making it possible to visit food destinations on Saturday that would be unreachable in Jerusalem.

The German Colony is directly below the Bahá'í Gardens exit — the natural sequence is to walk the terraces in the morning, exit at the bottom, and eat at Fattoush or explore Ben Gurion Avenue before taking the Carmelit up to the summit.
