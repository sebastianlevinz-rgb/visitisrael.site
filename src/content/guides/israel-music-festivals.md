Israel packs an extraordinary range of live music and arts festivals into its annual calendar — from international-calibre electronic music on the edge of a desert crater to intimate classical concerts against the backdrop of the Dead Sea, and a massive free light-art festival that transforms Jerusalem's Old City for ten nights each June.

This guide covers what each major festival is actually like to attend — logistics, ticket tips, what to pack, and how to combine festival visits with the surrounding sights.

## Meteor Festival

**Ramon Crater, Negev Desert · September · 3–5 days**

Meteor is Israel's flagship electronic and ambient music festival, held on or near the rim of [Makhtesh Ramon (Ramon Crater)](/negev) — a natural erosion crater 40 kilometres long and 8 kilometres wide, certified as the Middle East's first International Dark Sky Park. The combination of world-class DJ and live acts with one of earth's most otherworldly landscapes has made Meteor internationally recognised in the festival world.

### What to expect

The main stage overlooks the crater rim. Sunrise sets — performed as the Milky Way fades and the crater fills with morning light — are among the most talked-about moments of the Israeli festival calendar. Alongside the headline electronic acts, Meteor programmes ambient stages, yoga at dawn, visual art installations, and desert meditation sessions.

The festival runs over three to five days and includes on-site camping. Desert nights at 900 metres altitude are cold even in September (temperatures can drop below 10°C after midnight), so a warm sleeping bag is non-negotiable. The terrain is rocky; bring proper footwear.

### Tickets and logistics

Tickets are sold via **meteorfestival.co.il**. They sell out — often months in advance. Buy as soon as dates and tickets are announced (typically spring/summer of the festival year). Prices have historically ranged from ₪450 for a weekend pass to ₪900+ for the full multi-day camping experience; verify current prices on the official site.

Transport to the crater rim is easiest via the official festival shuttle buses from Tel Aviv and Jerusalem — public transport to the Negev crater rim is not practical for this event. The nearest town is Mitzpe Ramon, roughly 10 kilometres from the main site; some visitors stay there and shuttle in.

## Jerusalem Festival of Light

**Jerusalem Old City · June · ~10 nights · FREE (main route)**

The Jerusalem Festival of Light is one of the largest urban light-art events in the world. Each June, international artists from France, Germany, the UK, Israel, and beyond use projection mapping, LED sculpture, and light installations to illuminate the Old City's stone walls, narrow alleys, churches, mosques, synagogues, and 2,000-year-old Herodian streets. Entry to the main walking route is completely free.

### What to expect

The route runs roughly three kilometres through all four quarters of the Old City — the Christian Quarter, Muslim Quarter, Armenian Quarter, and Jewish Quarter — and includes Jaffa Gate and the Tower of David moat. The most dramatic projections typically cover the Tower of David itself, which serves as the largest "canvas" of the festival. A premium ticketed show is often staged at the Tower of David (separate from the free route); check **festivaloflight.co.il** for the current year's premium programme.

The festival operates nightly from approximately 9pm to midnight over its ten-day run. Weekday evenings are significantly less crowded than weekends; the first and last nights tend to be quieter than the middle days.

### Practical tips

- Arrive by 8:30pm to secure a good starting position at Jaffa Gate
- Jerusalem evenings in June are 18–22°C — bring a light layer for after midnight
- Wear flat, comfortable shoes for cobblestones
- Avoid bringing a stroller into the narrow Old City alleys
- No glass bottles inside the Old City during the festival
- Consider entering via Zion Gate or Damascus Gate to avoid the Jaffa Gate crowd concentration
- Accommodation in Jerusalem surges during the festival — book 2–3 months ahead

## Tamar Music Festival

**Dead Sea shores · October · 3–4 days**

Tamar is Israel's premier classical and world music festival, held on an open-air stage at the edge of the Dead Sea. The combination of high-quality acoustic music and the surreal landscape of the Dead Sea salt flats — 430 metres below sea level, ringed by the Judean Desert and the mountains of Jordan — creates an atmosphere unlike any other classical festival in the world.

### What to expect

Performances take place on an outdoor stage in the late afternoon and evening, when the desert light turns golden and the Dead Sea surface reflects the Jordan mountains opposite. The programming combines Israeli orchestras and chamber ensembles with international soloists and world-music acts.

Many visitors combine the festival with a morning float in the Dead Sea (Ein Bokek beach is minutes from the main stage area) and an early-morning visit to Masada — the cable car opens before the heat of the day, and the fortress is at its best in the golden morning light.

### Tickets and logistics

Tickets are sold via **tamarmusicfest.com**. Prices have ranged from approximately ₪150 to ₪400 depending on the concert and seating area. The festival village typically includes food stalls, wine, and artisan vendors alongside the music programme.

Accommodation options near the stage are the Ein Bokek resort hotels (several 4- and 5-star properties directly on the Dead Sea shore). Book significantly in advance for festival dates — the hotels here are the only accommodation in the area.

## Tel Aviv International Jazz Festival

**Multiple venues across Tel Aviv · May · 5–7 days**

The Tel Aviv Jazz Festival brings international and Israeli jazz artists to clubs, auditoriums, and outdoor stages across the city for roughly a week each May. A mix of free street performances and ticketed evening concerts makes it accessible at any budget.

### What to expect

Free daytime and early-evening performances take place in public spaces — Habima Square, Rothschild Boulevard, and the Tel Aviv Port have all hosted open-air stages in recent editions. Ticketed concerts at venues such as the Charles Bronfman Auditorium, Beit HaAmudim, and various TLV jazz clubs run in the evenings and can sell out for headline acts.

The festival's urban, street-level character makes it easy to encounter live music by accident while exploring the city — a distinctive quality compared to destination festivals requiring travel.

### Tickets and logistics

Check the official festival website (search "Tel Aviv Jazz Festival" + current year) for the programme and ticket links. Israeli ticketing platforms Leaan (leaan.co.il) and IsraTicket (israelticket.co.il) also list tickets for major Israeli festivals. Headline ticketed concerts should be booked in advance; free performances require no advance planning.

## Eilat Chamber Music Festival

**Multiple venues in Eilat · December · 5 days**

Israel's southernmost festival brings classical chamber music to Eilat — Israel's Red Sea resort city — each December. The combination of fine chamber performances in intimate indoor venues and Eilat's year-round sunshine makes this a popular choice for music-loving tourists who want warm December weather alongside cultural programming.

### What to expect

Concerts are held in smaller auditoriums, hotel performance spaces, and occasionally on Red Sea waterfront stages. The repertoire covers traditional chamber repertoire alongside Israeli contemporary compositions. December is one of Eilat's best months: dry, sunny, around 22–25°C, with excellent conditions for snorkelling or diving alongside festival evenings.

### Tickets and logistics

Tickets are available via **eilatfestival.co.il** (verify current site for the festival). Prices have typically ranged from ₪80 to ₪250 per concert. Eilat accommodation — particularly for the popular December–January winter sun period — should be booked well in advance.

## Purim in Tel Aviv: the world's most colourful street party

**Dizengoff Square, Tel Aviv · February or March · Free**

Purim is a Jewish holiday celebrating the deliverance of the Jewish people as described in the Book of Esther. In Tel Aviv, it has evolved into Israel's most vibrant secular celebration: an entire city in costume, with the epicentre at Dizengoff Square.

The celebration runs from the evening before Purim (Purim Eve) through the following day. Dizengoff Square hosts a free outdoor stage with live music, DJ sets, and a costume competition — families with children during the day, adults through the evening into the night. The surrounding streets — Dizengoff Street, Ibn Gabirol, Rothschild Boulevard — fill with costume-clad revellers moving between bars and house parties.

### Practical tips

- Purim dates shift each year according to the Hebrew calendar (typically late February or mid-March)
- You do not need to be Jewish to join the celebration — visitors in costume are welcomed
- Most supermarkets sell hamantaschen (triangular poppy-seed or fruit pastries, the traditional Purim food) for weeks ahead
- Bars and clubs around Dizengoff run themed Purim parties into the early hours — many sell out, so check IsraTicket or Leaan for advance tickets
- Tel Aviv Pride, a few months later in June, is a different event — see [Israel events & festivals](/israel-events-festivals)

## Planning your festival trip

### Buying tickets

No single platform covers all Israeli festivals. The main resources:

- **Official festival websites** — always the primary source; the most accurate prices and availability
- **Leaan** (leaan.co.il) — the largest Israeli ticketing platform; covers many festivals and concerts
- **IsraTicket** (israelticket.co.il) — alternative national platform
- **GetYourGuide / Viator** — for guided festival-adjacent experiences (not for festival tickets themselves)

### Accommodation strategy

Book accommodation as soon as festival dates are confirmed — not when you buy the festival ticket. Jerusalem and Eilat hotels near major festival sites can be fully booked months in advance.

- For **Meteor Festival**: stay in Mitzpe Ramon or book on-site camping through the festival
- For **Jerusalem Festival of Light**: stay in Jerusalem (Mamilla, City Centre, German Colony); a same-day train from Tel Aviv works for early evenings but leaves you scrambling for the last services home
- For **Tamar Festival**: book the Ein Bokek resort strip — the only practical option given the Dead Sea's remote location
- For **Eilat Chamber Music**: Eilat hotels are abundant; book the north beach strip for the best value-to-comfort ratio

### Transport to remote venues

**Meteor Festival (Negev):** Official festival shuttles from Tel Aviv and Jerusalem are the standard option. Renting a car from [DiscoverCars](/car-rental-israel) and driving the 230km from Tel Aviv (via Route 40 through the Negev) is practical but requires parking arrangements at the festival site — check the current year's car-park logistics on the festival website.

**Tamar Festival (Dead Sea):** A rental car from Jerusalem (90 minutes via Route 1 East + Route 90 South) is the most practical approach. There is no convenient public transport to the Ein Bokek festival area.

**Eilat (all events):** Fly from Ben Gurion Airport (El Al, Arkia, and Israir; 45–55 minutes) or drive (4.5–5 hours from Tel Aviv via Route 40). The Eilat bus from Tel Aviv takes 5–6 hours — practical for a multi-day festival stay, less so for a single night.

## Safety and current context

Israel's major tourist areas — Tel Aviv, Jerusalem, the Dead Sea, Eilat — have operated normally throughout 2025–2026. Music festivals in these areas have continued with standard Israeli security arrangements (bag checks at entrances, security personnel on-site).
