The Western Wall — the Kotel in Hebrew — is the holiest site in Judaism accessible to the public and one of the most powerful places in the world to visit regardless of your background. Open 24 hours, free to enter, and charged with 2,000 years of continuous prayer: it is Jerusalem's most essential experience.

This guide covers the Plaza (the open-air Wall). For the underground archaeological route beneath the Muslim Quarter, see the [Western Wall Tunnels guide](/western-wall-tunnels-guide) — it requires advance booking and is a completely separate experience.

---

## What you are looking at

What visitors see in the Plaza is 57 metres of exposed limestone wall. The full structure is 488 metres long — the remainder runs underground, buried beneath the Muslim Quarter of the Old City.

This is not a wall of the Temple itself. It is the western retaining wall of the platform Herod the Great constructed in the first century BCE to expand and support the Temple Mount (the vast esplanade above, known to Muslims as the Haram al-Sharif and to Jews as the Temple Mount). Herod's engineers filled in valleys, quarried enormous limestone blocks from the Judean Hills, and built retaining walls rising approximately 32 metres to create the largest sacred platform in the ancient world.

The Temple was destroyed by the Roman general Titus in 70 CE. The retaining walls survived. Centuries of post-Roman construction — Byzantine, Umayyad, Crusader, Mamluk, Ottoman — filled in the streets around the base of the wall and buried the lower courses under accumulated rubble and buildings. What stands exposed today above the medieval ground level is the upper section of the original Herodian structure, the 57 metres that were never buried.

For observant Jews, proximity to this wall — the closest accessible point to where the Temple's Holy of Holies once stood — carries profound religious significance. For visitors of all backgrounds, standing before stones that were laid 2,000 years ago and have been prayed at ever since is quietly extraordinary.

## The Plaza: what to expect

### Security and entry

The Plaza is reached through a security checkpoint, the same as elsewhere in the Old City. The main approaches are via **Dung Gate** (the nearest city gate, a 2-minute walk from the checkpoint to the Plaza) and through the **Jewish Quarter** (from Jaffa Gate or Zion Gate, following Cardo street south).

There is no ticket, no booking and no entry fee. Bring a valid passport or ID for the security checkpoint. The checkpoint operates at all hours.

### Dress code

Covered shoulders and knees are required for both men and women at all times. This applies regardless of season — bring a scarf or lightweight layer to drape over your shoulders in summer. At the men's section entrance, paper kippot are provided free of charge; wearing one is required to approach the Wall. Women do not need to cover their heads.

See the [holy sites dress code guide](/holy-sites-dress-code-etiquette) for the full detail that applies across Jerusalem's sacred sites.

### The prayer sections

A partition (mechitza) divides the Plaza in front of the Wall:

- **Men's section** — the larger, northern portion. Active prayer happens throughout the day; Torah scrolls are brought out at prayer services.
- **Women's section** — the smaller, southern portion. Equally direct access to the Wall.
- **Robinson's Arch** — at the far southern end of the external Wall, a short walk beyond the main Plaza, is a designated mixed-prayer and egalitarian area. This is the space used primarily by Reform and Conservative Jewish communities for mixed prayer services.

Visitors who are not Jewish may enter either section (men in the men's section, women in the women's section) and approach the Wall directly.

## The Kotel notes tradition

One of the Western Wall's most universally practised customs is the **note in the Wall** — writing a prayer, a wish or a message on a small piece of paper, folding it tightly, and pressing it into a crevice between the ancient stones.

The tradition is practised by observant Jews, secular Jewish visitors, pilgrims of other faiths, and travellers with no religious affiliation at all. The Wall has been receiving prayers in this form for centuries. If you want to leave a note, bring a small piece of paper and a pen (or write at home and carry it folded). A tightly folded, small note fits more easily into the narrowing stone crevices than a large sheet.

The **Western Wall Heritage Foundation** collects the accumulated notes twice a year — before Passover in spring and before Rosh Hashanah in autumn — and buries them with ceremony on the **Mount of Olives**. The notes are not read. No prayer or wish left at the Wall is ever discarded.

## Best times to visit

### Friday evening (Kabbalat Shabbat)

This is the single most atmospheric time to visit the Western Wall. As the sun sets over Jerusalem, hundreds of worshippers stream through the Jewish Quarter singing Shabbat songs — visible and audible from the Plaza long before they arrive. The Plaza fills; the Wall is bathed in warm evening light; the communal energy is remarkable. Non-Jewish visitors are entirely welcome to observe. Arrive 30–45 minutes before local sunset time to find a good vantage point.

### Saturday night (havdalah)

The end of Shabbat is marked at the Wall with the havdalah ceremony — prayers, the braided havdalah candle, spices and wine. Smaller crowds than Friday evening but equally meaningful.

### Jewish holiday mornings

**Birkat Kohanim** — the ancient Priestly Blessing — is performed publicly at the Western Wall on the mornings of **Passover** and **Sukkot**. Several thousand people fill the Plaza; Kohanim (Jews of priestly descent) gather before the ark and recite the three-verse blessing over the assembled crowd. Arrive by 7:30 am for a good position. Tickets are not required; the Plaza is free and open to all.

### Before dawn (4am–7am)

The Western Wall is one of the few major sites in Jerusalem that rewards a pre-dawn visit. In the early hours, the Plaza is nearly empty, the stones are visible without crowds pressing close, and a kind of intimate quiet replaces the daytime noise. The Wall is floodlit. Individual worshippers come and go. It is the easiest time to approach the Wall and stand quietly without disruption.

### What to avoid

Mid-morning on Sundays (large tour group arrivals), Friday afternoons during Passover and Sukkot, and the days of major Jewish national holidays see the heaviest general crowds. The Wall remains accessible throughout — it simply becomes busier.

## Combining with other Jerusalem visits

### Western Wall Tunnels

The [Western Wall Tunnels](/western-wall-tunnels-guide) are the excavated underground passage that runs along the full 488-metre length of the original Herodian wall, passing the massive Western Stone (estimated at 570 tonnes), Warren's Gate (sealed since the early Islamic period), and Hasmonean-era water systems. The Tunnels are a completely separate experience from the outdoor Plaza — they require advance booking through english.thekotel.org and run as guided tours of 60–70 minutes. Visiting both on the same morning is very manageable: do the Plaza first, then cross to the Tunnels entrance.

### Jewish Quarter

Immediately north and west of the Plaza is the **[Jewish Quarter](/jewish-quarter-jerusalem-guide)** — the rebuilt neighbourhood of the Old City with Byzantine Cardo remains, the Broad Wall (Iron Age fortification), and the Burnt House (first-century CE destruction layer). A Jewish Quarter walk before the Wall grounds the history in layers.

### Old City walking tour

The Western Wall fits naturally into any [Old City walking tour](/jerusalem-old-city-walking-tour). The standard circuit from Jaffa Gate passes the Armenian Quarter, Jewish Quarter, and descends to the Plaza before continuing through the Muslim Quarter along the Via Dolorosa and into the Christian Quarter. Walking in this direction means arriving at the Wall from above and then exiting through the Muslim Quarter — the same route as the Tunnels tour exit.

### Temple Mount / Haram al-Sharif

The Western Wall abuts the **Temple Mount** — the raised esplanade above, containing the Dome of the Rock and Al-Aqsa Mosque. Non-Muslim visitors may access the Temple Mount through the **Mughrabi Gate** (a wooden ramp gate immediately to the left as you face the Wall), during restricted visiting hours (typically Sun–Thu mornings; check the current schedule). Non-Muslims may not enter the Dome of the Rock or Al-Aqsa Mosque interior. Visiting hours are separate and independent from the Western Wall Plaza, and the two sites have different security arrangements. See the Temple Mount visitor note in the [holy sites dress code guide](/holy-sites-dress-code-etiquette).

## Etiquette

- Approach the Wall quietly. Loud conversation directly at the Wall during prayer services is not appropriate.
- Photography of worshippers at close range without consent is discouraged, particularly during active prayer moments.
- Men's and women's sections should not be crossed — each gender accesses their designated section.
- The Wall is an active place of worship: be aware of when services are in progress and give worshippers space.
- Non-Jewish visitors are genuinely welcome. There are no expectations placed on secular or non-Jewish visitors beyond dress code and quiet respect.
