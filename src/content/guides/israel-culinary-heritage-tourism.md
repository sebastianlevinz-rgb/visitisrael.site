Nobody tells you, before you go, that Israel might be one of the world's most interesting food destinations. The conversation about the country gets dominated by other things. Then you land in Tel Aviv, find a market stall run by a woman whose grandmother cooked the same dish in Casablanca in 1950, and the picture shifts.

This is the food heritage story — what makes Israeli cuisine extraordinary, which diaspora traditions built it, where to taste those traditions today, and how to plan a trip around the culinary culture rather than alongside it.

---

## The 70-diaspora thesis

Modern Israel was built by immigrants from more than 70 countries. They arrived in waves: before the state (Yemenite and Ashkenazi communities in the 19th century), during and after the Shoah (European survivors, mostly Ashkenazi), and then in mass immigration from Arab countries following 1948 — Iraq, Morocco, Yemen, Tunisia, Libya, Egypt, Iran, and more — as Jewish communities were expelled or fled rising nationalism. The Soviet Union added another enormous wave in the 1990s.

Each community arrived with their food intact: the preserved lemons of Fez, the dried limes of Baghdad, the zhug of San'a, the sour cream and rye bread of Warsaw. In Israel, these traditions did not merge smoothly or quickly. Ashkenazi and Mizrahi communities often lived in separate neighbourhoods, and the food stayed distinct for decades. What happened gradually — through the markets, through inter-community marriages, through shared military service and the cosmopolitan culture of Tel Aviv — was a cross-pollination that produced something genuinely new.

**The result is a food culture that is still being written.** Israeli chefs trained in Paris and New York are now returning and layering French technique over Iraqi kubbeh broth. Arab-Israeli restaurateurs in Haifa are applying fine-dining presentation to Circassian and Druze village cooking. Yemenite women in Tel Aviv still make jachnun on Friday night for Saturday morning exactly as their great-grandmothers did in Yemen.

## The key culinary traditions

### Yemenite: the most distinctive diaspora cuisine in Israel

Yemenite Jews arrived primarily in two waves — 1881-1914 and the 1949-1950 Operation Magic Carpet airlift (47,000 people in one year). Their cuisine is one of the most immediately recognisable in Israel, built around a handful of dishes that are unlike anything else in the country.

**Jachnun** is a slow-baked pastry roll cooked overnight (Friday night) and served warm on Saturday morning. Made from laminated dough (similar in technique to croissant dough but simpler), it emerges dark and caramelised, sweet-savoury and unlike anything else. Eaten with a grated tomato sauce and hard-boiled egg, and the obligatory pot of **zhug** — a ferocious green hot-sauce made from coriander, garlic and green chillies that functions in Yemenite cooking the way soy sauce functions in Japanese cooking.

**Malawach** is a pan-fried round pastry, crisp and layered, cousin to jachnun but cooked to order and served hotter and crispier. In the Kerem HaTeimanim (Yemenite Quarter) in south Tel Aviv, a cluster of family restaurants has served these dishes to the same neighbourhood clientele for 70 years.

**Where to taste it:** Kerem HaTeimanim, Tel Aviv (Saturday morning especially); Yemenite stalls at Machane Yehuda market (Jerusalem) on Friday mornings. Some of the best jachnun in the country comes from home kitchens rather than restaurants — a cooking class format gives access to these.

### Iraqi-Jewish: kubbeh, amba and the origins of sabich

Iraqi Jews — from one of the oldest Jewish communities in the world, dating back to the Babylonian exile — arrived in Israel primarily between 1948 and 1952. About 130,000 came in two years, making the Iraqi-Jewish arrival one of the most dramatic mass migrations in Israel's history. They brought one of the most sophisticated cuisines in the Middle East.

**Kubbeh** is the flagship dish: a semolina-and-meat dumpling traditionally served in broth. There are three main versions — kubbeh halab (broth-poached, clear broth), kubbeh adom (red beetroot broth), and kubbeh matfuniya (a deep-fried version). Each community family tends to defend one version ferociously. In Jerusalem, the Machane Yehuda restaurant Azura serves an Iraqi-Levantine menu that has barely changed in decades.

**Amba** is a tangy, fenugreek-scented mango pickle that Iraqi Jews brought from Bombay (many Iraqi Jews had a Bombay-Baghdad commercial corridor) and which became the defining condiment of the sabich sandwich. Today amba is everywhere — on falafel, shawarma, hummus plates — but it is an Iraqi-Jewish contribution.

**Sabich itself** — fried aubergine, hard-boiled egg, amba, tahini and salad in pita — is an Iraqi-Jewish invention. It was originally eaten cold on Saturday mornings (when cooking is restricted) and became a street food when Iraqi-Jewish immigrants started selling it in Ramat Gan and then Tel Aviv. One of the most distinctively Israeli dishes has Iraqi roots.

**Where to taste it:** Azura, Machane Yehuda, Jerusalem (stovetop Iraqi cooking); dedicated sabich counters in Tel Aviv (Sabich Tchernikhovsky is the most-cited); most falafel stalls carry amba — look for the amber-coloured condiment jar.

### Moroccan and North African: spice architecture and slow cooking

Moroccan Jews arrived from the early 1950s onwards, eventually making Moroccan-origin Israelis one of the largest ethnic communities in the country (~800,000 people today). North African Jewish cuisine — from Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria, Libya, Egypt — brought a very different spice vocabulary to Israel: ras el hanout, harissa, preserved lemons, and a tradition of long-cooked one-pot dishes that contrast with both Ashkenazi and Yemenite cooking.

**Couscous** is a Friday tradition in Moroccan-Israeli homes — hand-rolled (not machine-made in the traditional version) and served with a rich vegetable and meat broth. The quality gap between home-cooked Moroccan couscous and restaurant versions is enormous; cooking classes that access home kitchens offer the most authentic encounter.

**Matbucha** — slow-cooked tomato and roasted pepper salad — is a North African contribution that has become ubiquitous across Israeli mezze spreads, often without anyone acknowledging its origin.

**Harissa** in Israel is almost always Moroccan-style: smoky, rich, oil-slicked red pepper paste. Not the Thai style; not the Tunisian tube; the Moroccan long-cooked version. Many Israelis simply call it "the red paste."

**Where to taste it:** The Hatikva neighbourhood market in south Tel Aviv (also called the Mizrahi market or Shuk Hapishpeshim) is the most concentrated North African-Israeli food enclave in the country. Jerusalem's Machane Yehuda has North African pastry specialists; Marzipan is the most famous, known for their rugelach.

### Persian (Iranian): saffron, dried limes, and herb-forward cooking

Iranian-Jewish immigration came primarily in two waves: following the 1948 war and then massively after the 1979 Islamic Revolution, when about 35,000 Jews left Iran for Israel. Persian-Jewish cooking is among the most technically sophisticated in Israel — built around long-marination, multiple fresh herbs, dried limes and sour plums, and the use of saffron in ways the rest of Israeli cooking rarely touches.

**Ghormeh sabzi** — herb, kidney bean and dried-lime stew — is the signature Persian-Jewish dish, traditionally served with saffron rice. The dried lime (limoo amani) gives it an irreplaceable sourness that no substitute achieves. It is one of those dishes that improves over days and is nearly always better at someone's home than in a restaurant.

**Ash reshteh** — thick noodle and legume soup with kashk (dried whey) — is the Persian New Year (Nowruz) dish that Iranian Jews have continued to make in Israel. A bowl in a Persian-Israeli household on Nowruz (which the Iranian-Jewish community continues to celebrate) is one of the most transported food experiences in the country.

**Where to taste it:** The Levinsky Market area in Tel Aviv's Florentin neighbourhood has several Persian-Israeli spice and dried-goods vendors. The neighbourhood around south Tel Aviv has a meaningful concentration of Iranian-Israeli restaurants. The [Galilee Culinary Institute at Kibbutz Gonen](/galilee-culinary-institute-gonen) works with Persian spice traditions as part of its Israeli heritage cooking curriculum.

### Ashkenazi: the baking tradition

Ashkenazi Jewish cooking — from Eastern and Central Europe (Germany, Poland, Russia, Ukraine, Hungary, Romania) — forms a different strand: baking-heavy, dairy-and-pickle-forward, and tied to the Sabbath and holiday calendar in ways that have kept traditional dishes alive even as restaurant fashion has moved away from heavy Eastern European food.

**Challah** — braided egg bread — is the Friday Shabbat bread across all Jewish communities now, but its form is Ashkenazi. The quality of challah in Israeli bakeries, particularly Jerusalem's Mahane Yehuda, is extraordinary.

**Rugelach** — crescent pastries filled with jam, cinnamon, or chocolate — are an Ashkenazi Polish confection that has become universal in Israeli bakeries. Marzipan Bakery in Machane Yehuda does a version with chocolate rugelach that has spawned queues for decades.

**Gefilte fish** is less visible in restaurants but remains a Shabbat table staple in traditional Ashkenazi households — poached white fish balls in a sweet broth, always with red horseradish. Tasting it in a home context (a Shabbat dinner hosted through organisations like Shabbat.com or heritage tour operators) is more representative than any restaurant version.

### Arab-Israeli: the Levantine foundation

Arab-Israeli food — from the Arab citizens of Israel who remained in the country after 1948 and form about 20% of the population — represents the most direct continuation of ancient Levantine cooking. This is the culinary tradition of the region's millennia, and it underpins many dishes that are now claimed as "Israeli": hummus, falafel, ful medames, maqluba (upside-down rice and meat dish), knafeh.

The Arab-Israeli cities are the places to experience this most authentically: Nazareth, Akko, Haifa (especially the Wadi Nisnas neighbourhood), Jaffa, Umm al-Fahm. In these communities, Arab cooking continues in its full form — not as heritage performance but as daily life.

**Maqluba** (which means "upside down") is a layered rice, vegetable and meat dish cooked and then flipped onto a serving plate. It is the Sunday dinner dish across Arab Palestinian families. Most non-Arab Israelis encounter it only if they visit Arab-Israeli homes or specific restaurants.

**Mansaf** — lamb cooked in dried fermented yoghurt (jameed) and served over rice and flatbread — is the ceremonial feast dish of Bedouin and Palestinian heritage, served at weddings, funerals, and special occasions. For the Bedouin version with a guest experience context, see the [Bedouin Experience guide](/bedouin-experience-israel).

## Where to eat the heritage: key destinations

### Machane Yehuda Market, Jerusalem

The physical archive of Israeli culinary heritage. Open Sunday to Friday (closing Friday afternoon for Shabbat), the market sells more variety of diaspora food culture in one space than anywhere else in the country: Yemenite bread stalls, Iraqi pastry vendors, North African spice shops, Ashkenazi bakeries, Persian pickled goods, and Arab-Israeli fresh produce. Early Friday morning is the peak moment — when vendors set up Shabbat specials and the market reaches its maximum energy. After dark on weekdays, the market section transitions into bars and restaurants for a completely different atmosphere. The [Jerusalem food guide](/jerusalem-food-guide) covers the restaurant layer in more detail.

### Carmel Market (Shuk HaCarmel), Tel Aviv

Tel Aviv's main open-air market has a stronger street food focus and more overlap with tourist patterns than Machane Yehuda, but the heritage food layer is real. Key vendors: Yemenite malawach and jachnun stalls on the market's southern edge (arrive early, some sell out by noon); the produce sections that carry Persian dried goods and Moroccan spice blends alongside standard fruit and vegetables; fresh pomegranate juice pressed at market entrance carts (one of the best simple pleasures in Tel Aviv). The [Tel Aviv Carmel Market guide](/tel-aviv-carmel-market) has the navigation detail.

### Levinsky Market area, Florentin (Tel Aviv)

The most concentrated single-street representation of diaspora food culture in Israel: a narrow alley in the Florentin district where specialist spice, pickle, dried goods and pastry vendors have been operating since the 1930s. This is where you find Georgian churchkhela (walnut rolls dipped in grape juice), Persian saffron and dried limes, Bulgarian sheep's cheese, and Moroccan spice blends sold loose from open jars. The market has fewer tourist facilities than Machane Yehuda and requires some navigation — arriving mid-morning and walking the full length of Levinsky Street and its side alleys is the method.

### Nazareth

The Arab-Israeli culinary capital. Nazareth's restaurant scene has developed significantly in recent years around chefs who have trained internationally but returned to cook in their own tradition: [The Nazareth food scene](/nazareth-travel-guide) covers contemporary Arab-Israeli restaurants including Liwan (in the historic Ottoman-era Arab house of the Haddad family) and Uri Buri's extended operation in the Old City area. Nazareth also has the most intact traditional Arab market in Israel — a working souk still used by the local community.

### Wadi Nisnas, Haifa

The Arab-Christian neighbourhood of lower Haifa, where a cluster of restaurants and stalls serves Arab cooking in an environment that is far less tourist-oriented than Jaffa or the Jerusalem Old City. The [Haifa food guide](/haifa-food-guide) covers Wadi Nisnas in full — key stops include falafel stalls open since the 1950s, freshly baked ka'ak (sesame bread rings), and kanafeh made in the traditional way.

### Kerem HaTeimanim (Yemenite Quarter), Tel Aviv

The most intact ethnic neighbourhood food enclave in Tel Aviv: a cluster of family restaurants in the old Yemenite neighbourhood, south of the Carmel Market, that have been serving jachnun, malawach and Yemenite soups (lahuoh = spongy sourdough crepe; hilbeh = fenugreek-based dipping sauce) to the same community for 70 years. Primarily a Saturday morning destination, when the proper Shabbat foods are served. The [Kerem HaTeimanim guide](/kerem-hateimanim-tel-aviv) has navigation detail.

## Learning the heritage: cooking classes and experiences

Cooking classes that focus on the culinary heritage story rather than generic "Israeli food" offer the best depth. Several operators structure experiences specifically around diaspora traditions:

- **Hummus workshops** that trace the dish from its Levantine Arab and Sephardi Jewish roots, with the soaking, blending and seasoning technique taught step by step
- **Yemenite bread workshops** focused on malawach and jachnun, covering the lamination technique and the Saturday morning context
- **Moroccan-Israeli home cooking sessions** that cover preserved lemon making, couscous preparation, and the slow-cooking tradition
- **Market-to-kitchen experiences** that move from ingredient shopping at Machane Yehuda or Carmel Market to a cooking session covering three or four diaspora traditions

For the next level — professional culinary heritage education in a dedicated facility — the [Galilee Culinary Institute at Kibbutz Gonen](/galilee-culinary-institute-gonen) in the Upper Galilee runs day visitor cooking programs and immersive weeklong residencies under Chef Lior Lev Sercarz, working specifically with Israel's diaspora spice and cooking heritage.
