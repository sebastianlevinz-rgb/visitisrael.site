Jerusalem's Holy Week is the Christian liturgical calendar at its most intense. For a few days each spring, the Old City streets fill with pilgrims from every continent, the Church of the Holy Sepulchre becomes the stage for some of the oldest living Christian traditions in the world, and the walled city that was the setting of the Passion narrative absorbs the weight of that history in a way that nowhere else on earth can replicate. This guide covers every major event of Holy Week, how to attend each, and the practical details that make the difference between a transforming experience and a frustrating one.

For general Holy Sepulchre logistics year-round, see the [complete Church of the Holy Sepulchre visitor guide](/church-holy-sepulchre-guide). For the broader Christian pilgrimage circuit across Israel, see [Christian pilgrimage to the Holy Land](/christian-pilgrimage-holy-land).

## Easter dates: Western vs Orthodox

The first thing to understand about Easter in Jerusalem is that there are two Easters, and they rarely fall on the same date.

**Western Easter** (Catholic, Protestant, Anglican) follows the Gregorian calendar. **Eastern Orthodox Easter** (Greek Orthodox, Russian Orthodox, Armenian, Coptic, Ethiopian) follows the Julian calendar, and can fall anywhere from the same day to five weeks later.

| Year | Western Easter | Orthodox Easter |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| 2027 | 4 April | 2 May |
| 2028 | 16 April | 22 April |
| 2029 | 1 April | 6 May |

In 2028, the two dates are a week apart — close enough that a single trip can catch the major events of both traditions. In 2027 and 2029, they are a month or more apart, requiring two separate trips.

The Holy Fire ceremony (see below) follows the **Orthodox calendar**. If the Holy Fire is your primary goal, plan your trip around the Orthodox Holy Saturday, not the Western one.

## The Holy Week calendar (Western Easter 2027)

### Palm Sunday — 29 March 2027

The week opens with the **Palm Sunday procession** from Bethphage, on the upper slope of the Mount of Olives, down through the Lion's Gate into the Old City. The Franciscan Custos of the Holy Land leads the procession; pilgrims from every continent join, many carrying palm fronds or olive branches. The procession descends the same slope on which the Gospels place Jesus's entry into Jerusalem five days before the Crucifixion.

The procession begins around 14:30 at the Chapel of Bethphage (reached by taxi from East Jerusalem or a 30-minute walk from the Mount of Olives viewpoint) and arrives at the Old City around 16:00. It is free and open to all; arrive at Bethphage by 14:00 to get a position in the procession rather than standing watching from the side. Wear comfortable shoes — the descent is steep and cobbled.

### Holy Monday–Wednesday

These three days are relatively quieter on the public liturgical calendar, though every denomination holds services in its own churches. The **Pool of Siloam** and the **[Pilgrimage Road](/jerusalem-pilgrimage-road)** — the ancient stepped street uncovered after 13 years of excavation — offer some of the richest Passion-narrative geography in the city, and crowds are lighter than on Good Friday or Easter Sunday.

The **Mount of Olives** and **Garden of Gethsemane** are especially meaningful to walk during Holy Week. The Church of All Nations (Basilica of the Agony), built over the rock where Jesus is said to have prayed on the night of his arrest, is one of the most atmospheric places in Jerusalem at any time — during Holy Week it carries an additional charge.

### Holy Thursday (Maundy Thursday) — 1 April 2027

Two sites anchor Holy Thursday for most Christian pilgrims:

**The Cenacle (Upper Room)** on Mount Zion, traditionally identified with the room of the Last Supper, holds special meaning on Maundy Thursday. The room is administered by Israeli authorities (not a church), is open daily and free; liturgically active services are held by various Christian groups throughout the day.

**The Garden of Gethsemane**, at the base of the Mount of Olives, is the setting of Jesus's arrest in all four Gospels. The **Church of All Nations** holds its most attended services of the year on Thursday night; the walled enclosure of ancient olive trees, preserved by the Franciscans, is open for quiet prayer throughout the day.

### Good Friday — 2 April 2027

Good Friday is the most intensely observed day of Holy Week for most Christian pilgrims.

**The Franciscan Via Dolorosa procession** departs from the First Station of the Cross (near St Anne's Church and the Lion's Gate) at **12:00 noon** and walks all 14 stations to the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. The crowd is immense — thousands of pilgrims carrying wooden crosses, singing and walking together through the narrow Muslim Quarter streets. The procession arrives at the church approximately 90 minutes later. It is free and open to all; join at the Lion's Gate by 11:30 to position yourself in the procession rather than behind it.

Inside the **[Church of the Holy Sepulchre](/church-holy-sepulchre-guide)**, Good Friday services run throughout the day across the six denominations. The **Edicule (Tomb of Christ)** queue is among the longest of the year on Good Friday; if visiting the Tomb is a priority, consider arriving at the 04:00 dawn opening when the queue is short.

The **Garden Tomb**, the Protestant alternative site in East Jerusalem, holds its own Good Friday services in English, drawing large crowds of Evangelical and Anglican pilgrims in a quieter garden setting than the Holy Sepulchre.

### Holy Saturday — 3 April 2027 (Western) / 1 May 2027 (Orthodox)

**Western Holy Saturday** is a day of waiting liturgically — quieter than Good Friday and Easter Sunday, and one of the better days to visit the Sepulchre without extreme crowd pressure.

**Orthodox Holy Saturday** (this year: 1 May 2027) is when the **Holy Fire ceremony** takes place — one of the most remarkable events in the Christian calendar.

## The Holy Fire ceremony

The Holy Fire is observed by the Greek Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, Coptic, Ethiopian and Syriac Orthodox churches on Orthodox Holy Saturday. Theologically, it is understood by these churches as a miraculous event: the Patriarch of Jerusalem enters the Edicule alone, and after a period of prayer, a flame emerges and is passed out through the small windows of the Tomb to the waiting candles of the crowd.

Whether viewed as miracle or as the oldest living Christian rite in continuous practice, the ceremony is among the most extraordinary things a visitor can witness in Jerusalem. The church fills to standing capacity; thousands of pilgrims overflow into the forecourt and surrounding streets; the transmission of flame through a packed church on thousands of candles simultaneously is genuinely unlike anything else in the world.

**Practical details:**
- The ceremony is **free to attend** and open to all.
- Doors open in the morning; pilgrims begin queuing from **dawn or earlier**. By midday the church is full.
- The ceremony itself typically begins in **mid-to-late afternoon** (exact time varies; Greek Orthodox Patriarchate announces the time — check en.jerusalem-patriarchate.info).
- The crowd inside is extremely dense — not suitable for young children or anyone with severe claustrophobia or mobility limitations.
- The Israeli police coordinate crowd control outside; expect to show your passport.
- **The best positions** are inside the rotunda around the Edicule, which fill first. The arches and upper galleries (if open) offer a view over the crowd. Outside in the forecourt, you can receive the flame as it is passed through the church doors.
- Candles are passed out or sold at the entrance; bring your own thin taper candle as a backup.

## Easter Sunday — 4 April 2027 (Western) / 2 May 2027 (Orthodox)

**Western Easter Sunday** morning begins with **pre-dawn services** at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre — the most atmospheric time of the entire week to be in the church. The Franciscan Easter Sunday Mass is held in the early morning. After the liturgy, the crowd thins and a quieter morning walk through the walled city is possible.

The main **Easter Sunday Eucharist** at the Anglican Cathedral of St George (East Jerusalem) is the focal service for English-speaking Protestant pilgrims; check saintgeorgescathedral.net for current service times.

The rest of Easter Sunday in Jerusalem is genuinely festive: the outdoor restaurants of the Christian Quarter are full; the Old City market lanes carry a lighter atmosphere than Good Friday's intensity; and the afternoon provides good light for the **Mount of Olives viewpoint** and the panorama over the Kidron Valley.

## Planning essentials

### Book accommodation 9–12 months ahead

Jerusalem Holy Week accommodation is the tightest in the annual calendar. Properties within walking distance of the Old City — the Armenian Quarter guesthouses, the Mount Zion Hotel area, hotels near Jaffa Gate and the Notre Dame Centre — fill first. For 2027, searching by July 2026 is the prudent window.

Budget range: Jerusalem Holy Week prices run 30–60% above the city's standard rates. Pilgrim guesthouses (Ecce Homo, Sisters of Zion, Notre Dame, Casa Nova) offer a unique atmosphere and proximity; they book up fastest. Standard hotels in the German Colony and city centre are farther from the Old City action but easier to find.

### Getting around Old City during Holy Week

- **Light Rail Line 1** to the Central Bus Station or City Hall stops, then walk to Jaffa Gate — far faster than any taxi on Good Friday.
- **Taxis**: available, but avoid depending on them near the Old City during procession days; roads close.
- **Walking**: Jerusalem's Old City was built to be walked. Most pilgrims base near Jaffa Gate, Lion's Gate or Damascus Gate and walk everything.
- **Via Dolorosa procession day**: the Muslim Quarter is effectively closed to vehicle traffic during the procession; plan accordingly.

### Dress code

All sites in Jerusalem's Old City require covered shoulders and knees — year-round, but enforced with particular attention during Holy Week when crowds bring many visitors who have not previously visited. Carry a light scarf or wrap in your daypack. Remove hats inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. See the [Jerusalem holy sites dress code guide](/holy-sites-dress-code-etiquette).

### Combining Western and Orthodox Holy Week

In 2028, Western Easter (16 April) and Orthodox Easter (22 April) are one week apart — a trip of 10–12 days can include both Good Friday and the Orthodox Holy Fire ceremony. Plan Jerusalem for the full 10-day window: arrive Thursday before Western Palm Sunday and depart the Tuesday after Orthodox Easter Sunday.

Easter week makes a natural base for the wider Christian pilgrimage circuit:
