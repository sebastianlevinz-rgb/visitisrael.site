Lag BaOmer is one of the most visually spectacular nights of the Israeli year. As darkness falls on the 33rd day of the Omer count — the spring period between Passover and Shavuot — bonfires light up across the country: in parks, car parks, open fields, and backyards. The smell of wood smoke drifts across every Israeli city. Children who have spent weeks collecting wooden pallets and old furniture finally get to burn their piles.

For international visitors, Lag BaOmer offers a window into a distinctly Israeli experience — equal parts ancient religious tradition and national street celebration. But it also comes with a safety history that any visitor should understand before planning a trip around it.

## What is Lag BaOmer?

Lag BaOmer falls on the 33rd day of the Omer — the biblical period of counting between Passover and Shavuot. The letters *lamed* (30) and *gimel* (3) in Hebrew spell *lag*, giving the day its name.

The holiday commemorates two events in Jewish tradition: the end of a plague that killed 24,000 students of Rabbi Akiva during the Omer period in the 2nd century CE, and the yahrzeit (death anniversary) of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, the Talmudic sage traditionally credited with authoring the Zohar — the foundational text of Kabbalah. It is the yahrzeit of Rabbi Shimon that drives the pilgrimage to **Mount Meron**, where he is buried.

**2027 dates**: Lag BaOmer begins at nightfall on **Monday, 4 May 2027** and is observed through **Tuesday, 5 May 2027**. Bonfires are lit on the evening of 4 May.

## The Mount Meron pilgrimage — and its safety history

Mount Meron in the Upper Galilee, near Safed, is the site of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's tomb. In normal years, it becomes the largest annual gathering of Jews in Israel on Lag BaOmer eve — historically drawing 100,000 or more pilgrims who light bonfires, sing, pray, and mark the anniversary of the sage's passing through the night.

For observant and Kabbalistic communities worldwide, Meron on Lag BaOmer is among the most emotionally charged events in the Jewish calendar.

### A history of disruptions

Visitors planning to attend Meron should understand that access is no longer guaranteed:

**2021 — the crowd crush**: On 30 April 2021, a crush in a narrow passageway at the Meron site killed **45 people** and injured more than 150 in the deadliest civilian disaster in Israeli history. The tragedy prompted major changes to how Meron is managed, including mandatory pre-registration, sectored access, and strict capacity limits.

**2026 — the first full cancellation**: On the eve of Lag BaOmer 2026, Hezbollah rockets struck the Upper Galilee without triggering the warning sirens. Prime Minister Netanyahu ordered the complete cancellation of the Meron Hilula — the first time in modern history the pilgrimage was fully cancelled. The Transportation Ministry refused to implement the mass crowd logistics under active security conditions. Approximately 200,000 pilgrims were effectively barred; only three small official bonfires at Meron were permitted, each limited to around 200 people.

**Lesson for visitors**: Meron attendance is now **security-contingent and subject to annual government decisions**. In years when it does open, pre-registration at [mount-meron.co.il](https://mount-meron.co.il) is required — there is no walk-in access. Given the disruption history, do not plan an international trip around attending Meron specifically without checking the current-year security situation and registration status close to the date.

## National bonfires — what to expect

In most years, the bonfire tradition plays out across every neighbourhood in Israel on Lag BaOmer eve. Families and groups of children light fires in designated municipal areas — parks, car parks, and open lots — from late afternoon into the night. It is entirely informal and community-driven: groups stake out their bonfire spot in the afternoon, and by nightfall, fires are burning across the country.

For visitors staying anywhere in Israel, the experience arrives without effort: you will smell the smoke and see the fires from your hotel window or from any park you walk through.

**Practical notes**:
- **Air quality**: the massed bonfires create significant air pollution across Israeli cities. Health authorities typically advise those with respiratory conditions to stay indoors on Lag BaOmer eve. This is factual, not political — it is one of the worst air quality nights of the year in Tel Aviv and Jerusalem.
- **Traffic**: roads near popular bonfire spots and Meron-bound routes become heavily congested from mid-afternoon. Allow extra time for any journey.
- **Municipal rules change**: in 2026, a nationwide bonfire ban was issued by the Fire and Rescue Commissioner, linked to the same security situation that cancelled Meron. Local authorities set their own guidelines each year — check your municipality's rules if planning your own fire.

## Safed (Tzfat): the best experience for visitors

**Safed** is a far more manageable and accessible Lag BaOmer destination than Meron — and for most international visitors, it is the better choice.

Safed is the highest city in Israel (900m above sea level) and the historic centre of Jewish mysticism. In the 16th century, Rabbi Isaac Luria (the Ha'Ari), Rabbi Joseph Karo, and Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's Kabbalistic legacy converged here to create the spiritual tradition that still draws pilgrims from around the world. Lag BaOmer — the yahrzeit of Rabbi Shimon — is one of the most significant dates in the Kabbalistic calendar, and Safed observes it with genuine intensity.

**What to expect in Safed on Lag BaOmer**:
- Bonfires burn in the old city and its squares from nightfall on the eve
- The Ha'Ari Sephardic Synagogue and Abuhav Synagogue are active and atmospheric
- The narrow cobblestone lanes of the Artists' Quarter fill with people
- Local families and observant communities celebrate with singing and dancing around fires

Unlike Meron, Safed is **fully accessible on foot**, does not require pre-registration, and offers restaurants, accommodation, and cultural context year-round. The Old Cemetery — where the Ha'Ari's grave and the tomb of Rabbi Joseph Karo are located — is particularly moving on Lag BaOmer, when pilgrims visit through the day.

## Planning your visit

### Getting to Safed

| From | Route | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Tel Aviv | Bus or train to Haifa + bus north | ~2.5–3h |
| Jerusalem | Bus or train to Haifa + bus north | ~3–3.5h |
| By car from TLV | Route 6 + Route 65 north | ~2h 15min |
| By car from Jerusalem | Route 1 east + Route 90 north | ~2h 30min |

In years when Meron is open, special buses run from major cities on Lag BaOmer night — check Egged (egged.co.il) for the current year's schedule. The 12 km from Safed to Meron is typically served by sherut (shared taxi) from the Safed Central Bus Station.

### When to arrive

For the bonfire atmosphere, **arriving in Safed by afternoon** allows you to settle in and walk the old city before nightfall when the fires begin. If you are visiting for the day, early afternoon arrival is fine; the most intense period is from an hour before nightfall through midnight.

### Where to stay

Safed has hotels, guesthouses, and B&Bs in the old city and artists' quarter — budget one to two months ahead for Lag BaOmer, when the city fills considerably. The Dead Sea and Galilee accommodation options in Tiberias (45 minutes south) offer more availability if Safed itself is full.

### What to wear

Evenings at 900m in May can be cool — bring a layer for after dark even if the day is warm. The old city's cobblestone streets require comfortable shoes.

### Dress code at religious sites

The synagogues in Safed are active houses of worship. Covered shoulders and knees are required for both men and women; photography inside synagogues varies by site — check with staff. Do not visit during prayer services without permission.

## A note on the Omer period

Lag BaOmer interrupts a semi-mourning period in Jewish tradition — the Omer count between Passover and Shavuot — during which weddings and live music are typically restricted. On Lag BaOmer itself, these restrictions are lifted, which is why you may notice an unusual number of weddings and outdoor concerts across Israel on this one day.
