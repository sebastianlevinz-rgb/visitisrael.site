**Tel Gezer** sits on a low ridge in the Aijalon Valley, visible from Route 1 as you drive the corridor between Tel Aviv and Jerusalem — and for most of the past century, almost every tourist drove straight past it. That is changing. The site is one of the most archaeologically rich in Israel: a Solomonic fortification gate identical to those at Megiddo and Hazor, a 4,000-year-old water system you can walk down into, and the find spot of the Gezer Calendar — one of the oldest Hebrew inscriptions ever recovered. The INPA maintains a clear walking circuit through the excavations, and on a weekday the site is nearly empty.

## 26 settlement layers: why this site mattered for 5,000 years

Tel Gezer is a *tel* — an accumulation of successive settlements, each built on the levelled ruins of the last. Archaeologists have identified **26 distinct strata** spanning from around **3500 BCE to approximately 100 CE**, giving the mound one of the longest documented occupation sequences in the Levant. The site's persistence is geographical: Gezer sits at the point where the Aijalon Valley opens from the Judean highlands into the Shephelah, controlling the main ancient road between the coast and Jerusalem. Every major regional power — Egyptians, Canaanites, Israelites, Philistines, Hasmoneans, Romans — had strategic reasons to hold this ridge.

## The Solomonic Gate — Solomon's three cities confirmed in stone

The most archaeologically celebrated feature at Tel Gezer is its **six-chamber gate** from the 10th century BCE. In the 1950s, Israeli archaeologist Yigael Yadin noticed that this gate's plan — two rows of three guard chambers flanking a central passageway — was identical to gates uncovered at **Tel Megiddo** and **Tel Hazor**. The pattern matched a passage in **1 Kings 9:15**: *"This is the account of the forced labour that King Solomon conscripted to build the Lord's temple, his own palace, the terraces, the wall of Jerusalem, and Hazor, Megiddo and Gezer."* Three cities, three identical gates — the archaeological and biblical records aligned with unusual precision.

Walk the gate's stone courses and you are standing in one of the moments when the written tradition and the material record converge directly. The six-chamber plan is visible underfoot, and interpretive panels at the site explain both Yadin's discovery and the ongoing academic debate about the exact dating of the construction (some scholars place it in the Omride period of the 9th century BCE rather than Solomon's 10th century BCE; the general correspondence with 1 Kings 9:15 is not in dispute). Cross-link: [Tel Megiddo visitor guide](/megiddo-jezreel-valley-guide) — the Megiddo gate is similarly preserved and makes the architectural comparison tangible.

## The Canaanite water system — 175 steps underground

This is the site's most memorable physical experience. Around **1600 BCE**, Canaanite engineers cut a **vertical shaft and sloping tunnel** through solid limestone bedrock to reach a natural water source below the water table inside the city walls. The accessible portion descends approximately **175 rock-cut steps** into a cool, dim chamber deep below ground level. INPA maintains a lit walking path.

Practical details for the descent:
- **Bring a torch or phone light.** The INPA lighting is functional, but a personal light helps in the lower chamber and along the rough-cut walls.
- **Wear closed shoes.** The steps are hewn limestone — uneven and occasionally slippery from moisture seeping through the rock.
- **It is cool underground year-round** — welcome in summer, noticeably chilly in winter months.
- **The descent is not suitable for visitors with severe mobility limitations** — the steps are steep and not handrailed throughout.
- Allow approximately **20–25 minutes** for the full descent, exploration of the cistern chamber, and return.

Similar engineering exists at Megiddo (Ahab's Tunnel) and Hazor — Tel Gezer's system predates both by several centuries and is the least visited of the three, which means you can explore it almost entirely alone on a weekday.

## The Gezer Calendar — one of the oldest Hebrew inscriptions

In 1908, Irish archaeologist R.A.S. Macalister excavated a small limestone tablet (11 × 7 cm) inscribed in ancient Hebrew script during Ottoman-era fieldwork. The **Gezer Calendar**, as it is now universally called, records a 12-month agricultural cycle: two months of olive harvest, two months of grain planting, two months of late planting, a month of flax pulling, a month of barley harvest, and so on — an annual almanac scratched into limestone approximately **3,000 years ago**.

Its significance in the history of writing is considerable. The inscription dates to the **10th century BCE** — the period associated with the early Israelite monarchy — making it one of the earliest known examples of ancient Hebrew script. For non-specialists, the calendar brings the site's human dimension into focus: an ordinary farmer or scribe, roughly contemporary with David or Solomon, recording the rhythm of the agricultural year in a script that can be read by modern Hebrew scholars.

The original tablet is held at the **Istanbul Archaeology Museum** (removed during the Ottoman excavation period). The Israel Museum in Jerusalem holds a plaster cast. On-site interpretive panels reproduce and explain the inscription in detail.

## Combining Gezer with Latrun

Tel Gezer sits **5 minutes by car from Latrun**, making a natural half-day pairing:

- **Latrun Monastery** (Trappist monks, 1890 — active monastery with a small wine shop; beautiful grounds; free entry; no photography inside chapel): one of the most atmospheric sites in the Shephelah and a 10-minute walk from the car park.
- **Armored Corps Museum at Latrun**: Israel's largest open-air tank museum, with over 200 armoured vehicles from multiple nations and eras, tracing the history of armoured warfare from World War I through recent conflicts. An unexpected highlight for military history visitors.
- **Ayalon Valley viewpoint**: the valley below the Latrun ridge is the biblical scene of Joshua's "sun stand still" episode (Joshua 10:12–13) — the valley extends towards Gezer itself.

From central Tel Aviv, a Gezer + Latrun morning can be done and still allow an afternoon arrival in Jerusalem, making this a logical corridor stop rather than a detour.

## Practical tips

- **Download the INPA app** for self-guided audio commentary; it covers Gezer's strata and the Solomonic gate context in English.
- **Admission:** ~₪22–28 per adult without a pass; covered by the [Israel National Parks Pass](/israel-national-parks-pass). Verify at inpa.gov.il before your visit — prices and hours change seasonally.
- **Parking:** free on site; follow signs from Route 1 exit 282.
- **Water and sun:** there is no café at the site. Bring at least 1 litre of water per person in warm weather; the hilltop is exposed.
- **Photography:** permitted throughout; the Solomonic gate's orthostats photograph well in morning light.
