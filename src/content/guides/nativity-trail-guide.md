The **Nativity Trail** is one of the longest and most spiritually significant pilgrim walks in the Holy Land: a **160 km route from Nazareth to Bethlehem**, broadly following the journey that Mary and Joseph are traditionally said to have made for the Roman census described in Luke's Gospel. Where the [Jesus Trail](/jesus-trail-israel) traces Jesus's Galilean ministry northeast from Nazareth to Capernaum, the Nativity Trail turns south — through the highland villages and valleys of the West Bank — arriving finally at the [Church of the Nativity](/church-of-nativity-guide) in Bethlehem.

The trail is managed by **WalkPalestine** and the **Alternative Tourism Group (ATG)**, a licensed incoming tour operator based in Beit Sahour near Bethlehem, who coordinate the route, guesthouse network and licensed guide logistics. Most walkers complete it in **8 to 10 days**. It is not a casual undertaking: the trail passes through Palestinian Authority-administered areas, and the logistical and safety framing is different from hiking in Israel proper. A guided booking through ATG or a licensed operator is strongly recommended for anyone without prior West Bank experience.

> **Safety advisory:** The Nativity Trail passes through Palestinian Authority territory (Areas A and B). Before booking or travelling, check the current advisory for the Occupied Palestinian Territories from your home government — UK: gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice/the-occupied-palestinian-territories; USA: travel.state.gov; Australia: smartraveller.gov.au. Conditions change; what applied when this guide was written may not apply when you travel.

## How the Nativity Trail differs from the Jesus Trail

| Feature | Nativity Trail | Jesus Trail |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Start → End | Nazareth → Bethlehem | Nazareth → Capernaum |
| Distance | ~160 km | ~65 km |
| Duration | 8–10 days | 3–4 days |
| Territory | Crosses into West Bank (PA Areas A/B) | Stays within Israel (Green Line) |
| Gospel episode | Mary & Joseph's journey to Bethlehem | Jesus's Galilean ministry |
| Managed by | WalkPalestine / ATG | jesustrail.com |
| Guided recommended? | Strongly — Area A access | Optional |

The two trails are entirely compatible as a combined pilgrimage: walk the Jesus Trail first (Galilee), return to Nazareth, then begin the Nativity Trail south. This gives roughly two weeks of walking and covers both the Galilean ministry sites and the Nativity narrative.

## The route at a glance

The trail is divided into ten sections, though stage lengths are adjusted by each operator. The general corridor follows the Galilee highlands south into Samaria, continues through the hills of the central West Bank, drops to the Jordan Valley near Jericho, then climbs the Judean Hills to reach Bethlehem.

| Approx. stage | Towns passed | Distance | Character |
|---------------|--------------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Nazareth → Zababdeh | ~30 km | Galilee hills, olive terraces, Arab-Palestinian villages |
| 2–3 | Zababdeh → Jenin → Burqin | ~35 km | Fertile lowland plain, Burqin Byzantine church |
| 4–5 | Burqin → Sebastiyya → Nablus | ~40 km | Sebastiyya ruins (ancient Samaria), Mount Ebal approach, Nablus |
| 6–7 | Nablus → Ramallah corridor | ~30 km | Central West Bank highlands, villages |
| 8 | Jordan Valley descent to Jericho | ~15 km | Desert valley, 400m below sea level, dramatic terrain change |
| 9–10 | Jericho → Bethlehem via Judean Hills | ~25 km | Desert ascent, final approach to Bethlehem |

The exact stage divisions vary between operators. ATG publishes official stage maps and GPS tracks at their website and through WalkPalestine.com.

## Highlights along the route

**Burqin** — a small village near Jenin with one of the oldest continuously functioning churches in the world: the **Church of St George**, built over a cave traditionally identified with the ten lepers healed by Jesus in Luke 17. The village is largely Christian (a rarity in this part of the West Bank) and the cave-church interior is strikingly ancient.

**Sebastiyya** — the hilltop ruins of ancient **Samaria**, the capital of the northern Kingdom of Israel in the 9th century BCE. The Herodian colonnade street, Roman forum foundations and early-Israelite gate complex make this one of the most significant yet least visited archaeological sites in the Holy Land. The trail passes through the village at the hill's base.

**Nablus** — the largest city on the route and a deeply historic Palestinian urban centre. The **Jacob's Well Church** stands over the well where, according to John 4, Jesus spoke with the Samaritan woman — the conversation that crossed the deepest religious and social barriers of the era. The well itself, still drawing water, is preserved beneath the modern church and can be visited. Nablus is also known for its traditional **knafeh** (sweet cheese pastry in syrup) and its bustling Ottoman-era old city. Pilgrims typically spend one night here.

**Jordan Valley descent** — the trail's most dramatic terrain change: a descent from the West Bank plateau into the Jordan Rift Valley, arriving in the lowest continuously inhabited city on earth. **Jericho** is a natural overnight stop and offers the **Mount of Temptation Monastery** (reachable by cable car), the **archaeological mound of Tell es-Sultan** (ancient Jericho), and the site at the Jordan River where many traditions place the baptism of Jesus — distinct from the Yardenit site further north used by many tour groups.

**The Judean Hills ascent** — the final climb from Jericho (–400 m) to Bethlehem (+760 m) is the most physically demanding section of the trail, gained over roughly 25 km. It passes through the Judean Desert and the unique landscape of stone terraces and scattered Bedouin communities before reaching [Bethlehem](/bethlehem-travel-guide).

**Bethlehem** — the trail ends at **Manger Square** in Bethlehem, with the **[Church of the Nativity](/church-of-nativity-guide)** at its heart. Most pilgrims who have walked the full trail find the arrival at the Grotto of the Nativity profoundly moving: 160 km on foot, through the landscape of the story, to the place where it began.

## Practical information

**Distance and time:** ~160 km; 8–10 days for most walkers. Vehicle-supported formats are available for those who want the pilgrimage route without the full daily distances.

**Difficulty:** Moderate to challenging. No technical climbing, but multi-day distances over rolling hill terrain with full packs, combined with the heat of the Jordan Valley section, make this a serious undertaking. Good trail fitness is required.

**Best season:** Spring (mid-March to mid-May) for mild temperatures and wildflowers. Autumn (October–November) is the second-best window. December is possible and has a unique atmosphere approaching Christmas in Bethlehem, but requires careful logistics. Summer is hot and not recommended unless you start walking by 5:30 am.

**Water and food:** Guesthouses and villages supply water and food throughout most of the route. Carry 2–3 litres between villages in hotter sections. The Jordan Valley stretch requires more water than any other section — plan carefully.

**What to carry:** Light daypack if walking with baggage transfer (many operators offer this); comfortable hiking shoes or trail runners; modest clothing throughout (covered shoulders and knees in all towns and villages); respectful head covering for women in some communities.

**Checkpoints:** You will pass through Israeli military checkpoints and Palestinian Authority checkpoints at several points on the route. Always carry your passport. Licensed operators and guides know current checkpoint protocols; solo walkers need to research current conditions carefully before departure.

**Accommodation:** WalkPalestine.com publishes a network of guesthouses, family homestays and small hotels along the route. Most are in Palestinian villages and towns. Standards range from simple but clean rooms to comfortable mid-range guesthouses. Book ahead; total trail capacity is limited.

**Getting there:** The trail begins in [Nazareth](/nazareth), well connected by bus from Tel Aviv, Haifa and Jerusalem. The trail ends in [Bethlehem](/bethlehem-travel-guide), 10 km south of Jerusalem. Most walkers fly home from [Ben Gurion Airport](/ben-gurion-airport-guide) after a final night in Jerusalem.

**Language:** Arabic is the primary language throughout most of the route. Licensed guides bridge this; English is understood at most guesthouses that serve international pilgrims. Hebrew is generally not useful on this trail.

## Guided vs self-guided

**Guided walking** through ATG or a licensed operator is the approach taken by the large majority of international pilgrims walking the Nativity Trail. A guide provides: knowledge of current checkpoint protocols and Area A access; Arabic-language liaison with communities along the route; narrative depth at the biblical sites; accommodation pre-booked in a network trusted by the operator; and emergency support if needed. Many guided trips include baggage transfer between stages so walkers carry daypacks only.

**Self-guided walking** is technically possible using the WalkPalestine.com route notes, GPS tracks and guesthouse list. It requires: fluency with the political geography of the West Bank (which areas are Area A, B or C and what each means for a foreign national); no Israeli passport or citizenship (Israeli citizens cannot enter Area A); willingness to navigate checkpoint procedures without institutional support; and good experience of multi-day hiking. It is not a suitable first West Bank experience.

For most Christian pilgrims without West Bank experience, a guided booking is the right choice. Compare options on GetYourGuide and Viator using the links above.

## Combining with the Jesus Trail

The Nativity Trail and the [Jesus Trail](/jesus-trail-israel) form a natural combined pilgrimage covering two distinct arcs of the Gospel story:
- **Jesus Trail** (3–4 days, Nazareth → Capernaum): the Galilean ministry sites — Cana, the Horns of Hattin, Magdala, Capernaum.
- **Nativity Trail** (8–10 days, Nazareth → Bethlehem): the birth narrative sites — Samaria, Jacob's Well, Jericho, the Judean Desert.

Walk them back-to-back from Nazareth and you cover roughly two weeks of walking and the full physical geography of the Gospel narratives in the Holy Land. This combined format is offered by some specialist Christian pilgrimage operators as a single itinerary. See the [Christian pilgrimage Holy Land hub](/christian-pilgrimage-holy-land) for how to structure a broader holy land pilgrimage that includes both walking routes and the major Jerusalem and Galilee sites.
