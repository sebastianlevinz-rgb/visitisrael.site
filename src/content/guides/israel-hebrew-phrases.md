Learning a handful of Hebrew phrases before your Israel trip is one of the highest-return small investments you can make. English is widely spoken across the country — but arriving with 'toda raba' on the tip of your tongue earns you a disproportionately warm welcome at every [Mahane Yehuda stall](/tel-aviv-carmel-market), [Western Wall encounter](/western-wall-guide), and hotel check-in desk.

This guide gives you phonetic transliteration throughout — no need to learn the Hebrew script. All phrases are standard Israeli Hebrew (Sephardic pronunciation), which is the spoken norm across the country.

---

## Script and pronunciation basics

Hebrew is written right to left in a 22-letter alphabet with no upper/lower case distinction. For tourist use, **phonetic spelling is all you need** — restaurant menus, tourist sites, and street signs are universally available in English transliteration.

Two sounds that do not exist in English:

- **'ch' / 'kh'** — a guttural sound, like the Scottish 'loch' or German 'Bach'. Never pronounced like the 'ch' in 'cheese'. In this guide it is written as **ch**.
- **'r'** — a soft guttural sound, slightly back in the throat. Not the rolled 'r' of Spanish or the forward English 'r'. In casual speech it sounds closer to a light French 'r'.

Stress generally falls on the **final syllable**: sha-LOM, to-DA, be-va-ka-SHA.

## Practical notes for using Hebrew abroad

**Don't worry about gender errors.** Hebrew has grammatical gender — every noun and verb conjugation changes depending on whether you are male or female. Tourists consistently mix them up, and Israelis consistently find this charming rather than confusing. If you are female and say *ani mevin* instead of *ani mevina*, no communication fails.

**Slicha is your Swiss Army knife.** This single word covers: excuse me (to get past someone), sorry (to apologise), pardon? (when you didn't hear), and calling a waiter. Overuse it freely.

**'Beseder' unlocks everything.** Once you understand that *beseder* means OK in every possible sense — confirmed, understood, agreed, no problem, fine, alright — you will hear it roughly once every two minutes in Israeli conversation.

**The Latin alphabet is everywhere.** Shop signs, restaurant menus, metro and train displays, road signs on major routes, and airport signage all appear in both Hebrew and English. You will not struggle to find your way without reading the Hebrew script.
