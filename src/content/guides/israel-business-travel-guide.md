Israel has matured into one of the Middle East's most capable business travel destinations — reliable infrastructure, English fluency across professional circles, and a startup density that makes it a mandatory visit for anyone in tech, agri-tech, medtech, or cybersecurity. The practical learning curve for first-time business travellers centres on three things: Shabbat's effect on your schedule, understanding where to be in Tel Aviv versus Jerusalem, and knowing how to navigate the airport efficiently.

This guide covers the logistics that matter: where to stay, how to get around, how to run a client dinner, and how to convert a two-day trip into a richer experience with a bleisure extension.

---

## The Israeli working week

The single most important scheduling fact for business travellers: **the Israeli working week runs Sunday to Thursday**. Friday is a half-day at most organisations — many professionals are out of office by 1–2pm to prepare for Shabbat. Saturday is the full day of rest.

For international visitors arriving from Europe or North America where Monday is the start of the week, this creates a planning trap. A flight that lands Friday evening means you have missed the entire working week. A flight that lands Saturday night means Sunday is your first working day — plan accordingly. If you are attending a multi-day conference or meeting with multiple companies, aim to be in-country by Saturday evening to be fresh for an early Sunday start.

### Shabbat and the business traveller

Shabbat begins at sunset Friday and ends after nightfall Saturday. Its practical effects:

- **Israel Railways stops** for most of Shabbat (Friday sunset to Saturday night). The Ben Gurion Airport train resumes after Shabbat ends.
- **Most Jewish-owned restaurants** in Jerusalem close. In Tel Aviv's secular centre, cafes and restaurants largely stay open — Friday night in Tel Aviv is one of the week's busiest dining nights.
- **Gett taxis and private car services operate normally** through Shabbat. Transport is not the issue; planning ahead is.
- **Shopping malls and many shops** close in Jewish-majority areas during Shabbat. If you need a pharmacy or grocery, Arab-owned businesses and some 24-hour chains operate normally.

Use Shabbat as an asset rather than an obstacle: a Friday evening in Tel Aviv with no business commitments is an excellent opportunity for a seafront dinner in Jaffa, a walk through the White City, or preparation for Sunday's meetings.

## Where to stay: Tel Aviv or Jerusalem?

### Tel Aviv — Azrieli, Ramat Gan, and the corporate core

The majority of Israel's private-sector business activity — technology, venture capital, finance, diamond trading, fashion, media — is concentrated in greater Tel Aviv. The main corporate districts:

- **Azrieli / Sha'ul HaMelech** (the tower cluster near Begin Road): home to tech companies, law firms, and financial services offices. Walking distance from the Da Vinci Street and HaYarkon hotel belt.
- **Ramat Gan Diamond Exchange** (adjacent to Bnei Brak): Israel's diamond industry hub; also home to major insurance and banking headquarters.
- **HaShalom / Yigal Alon Street corridor**: increasingly dense with co-working spaces, startup offices, and VC firms.

Hotels well-positioned for Tel Aviv business:
- **Crowne Plaza TLV** (near the Azrieli towers): business facilities, frequent corporate accounts, shuttle-adjacent.
- **Hilton Tel Aviv** (seafront, central): well-known international brand, business wing, strong concierge.
- **Montefiore / Norman / David Kempinski**: luxury boutique options in the White City area — better for mixed business-social schedules than pure proximity to Azrieli.

### Jerusalem — Givat Ram, King David area, and the ICC

Government ministries, international organisations, academic institutions, and major convention events are concentrated in Jerusalem. The main conference venue — **ICC Jerusalem (International Convention Centre)** — is in the Givat Ram neighbourhood, near the Israel Museum and the Supreme Court. Hotels along King David Street and Agron Street (the King David Hotel, Leonardo Plaza, Dan Boutique) are 10–15 minutes' walk from the ICC.

If you are splitting time between Tel Aviv and Jerusalem, a car hire (DiscoverCars or Avis at Ben Gurion) for the duration of the trip gives you maximum flexibility — the highway between cities takes 45–55 minutes outside peak hours.

## Getting around: airport, city, and intercity

### Ben Gurion Airport to the city

The **Ben Gurion Airport fast train** (HaSharav Express) is the fastest and cheapest airport connection. It runs every 30 minutes, takes approximately 20 minutes to Tel Aviv HaHagana station and 24 minutes to Savidor Central. Fare: approximately ₪20. Trains operate Sunday through Thursday until midnight, and Friday until early afternoon.

On Shabbat (Friday sunset to Saturday night), no trains run. Use **Gett** (the dominant Israeli rideshare and taxi app, accepted widely by business travellers for receipt generation) or pre-booked **Welcome Pickups** for a fixed-price, name-board airport transfer.

### Within Tel Aviv

Gett is reliable for all in-city movement. The **Tel Aviv Light Rail (Red Line)** connects north Tel Aviv (University) through the city centre to the Azrieli towers and onward south — useful for commuting to the Azrieli district from northern hotels, though taxis dominate business travel.

### Tel Aviv → Jerusalem

The **Israel Railways inter-city train** from Tel Aviv Savidor to Jerusalem Yitzhak Navon runs approximately hourly and takes 30–35 minutes — notably faster than a car in daytime traffic. Navon station is in west Jerusalem, a short taxi ride from the ICC or Givat Ram hotels. Book tickets at [rail.co.il](https://www.rail.co.il/en) or via the RailWay app.

For flexibility, a hire car is worth considering for a multi-day trip involving both cities. The Route 1 and Route 443 highways between the cities are straightforward drives.

## Conference and events venues

**ICC Jerusalem (International Convention Centre):** Israel's flagship convention venue. Hosts major international medical congresses, government summits, and scientific conferences. Capacity up to 3,000 delegates across multiple halls. Located on Givat Ram opposite the Israel Museum — the neighbourhood has a cluster of hotels suitable for conference delegates.

**Expo TLV (Tel Aviv Exhibition Grounds):** The main trade fair and exhibition venue in the Tel Aviv metropolitan area, in the Neve Sha'anan district. Host to technology fairs, trade exhibitions, and large-scale industry events. Accessible by taxi from the main hotel belt.

**David Intercontinental Tel Aviv:** Seafront hotel with substantial conference and ballroom facilities, popular with international corporate events.

**Peres Center for Peace & Innovation, Jaffa:** A distinctive modern venue on the Jaffa seafront, used for smaller innovation-focused corporate events and private dinners. Useful for tech companies or organizations seeking a less conventional setting.

## Israeli business culture and etiquette

**Directness is the norm.** Israeli business culture prizes frankness and efficiency. Expect vigorous debate in meetings, direct "no" responses, and quick pivots to substance. This is not rudeness — it is how decisions get made. If you are from a culture where indirect communication is standard, prepare for the contrast.

**First names from the start.** Formal titles are rarely used in private-sector meetings. Use first names unless the context is explicitly ceremonial (government, very senior legal, religious institutions).

**Informal dress in most settings.** Smart-casual is standard across tech, startup, and creative industries. Suits are appropriate for banking, law, insurance, and government meetings. When in doubt, business casual is safe.

**Meeting dynamics.** Meetings are often longer than scheduled due to extended discussion. Build buffer time, especially before flights. A confirmed meeting does not always mean a prompt start — especially in smaller companies. If punctuality is critical for your schedule, reconfirm the meeting start time 30 minutes before.

**English is universal.** English is the working language of Israel's international business community. Hebrew fluency is not expected, needed, or helpful for most business interactions.

**Key dates to avoid.** Do not schedule business visits during the High Holy Days (Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, typically in September or October), Passover week, Sukkot, or the week of Independence Day (Yom Ha'atzmaut). These are national holidays when offices close and schedules shift dramatically. The period between Passover and Shavuot (April–June) is generally productive.

## Kosher client dining

Israel's restaurant scene has reached international quality — but the kosher framework shapes what you can order and where you can take clients. Understanding the basics prevents awkward surprises.

**Kosher classifications:**
- **Meat (fleishig):** No dairy in any course. A meat restaurant's menu has no butter, cream, cheese, or milk products.
- **Dairy (milchig):** No meat. A dairy restaurant serves fish, eggs, and vegetarian dishes with full dairy options.
- **Non-kosher (treif):** Operates freely with no restrictions — the majority of restaurants in secular Tel Aviv.

If your client is observant, ask about their kashrut level before booking — some only eat at restaurants certified by specific rabbinical authorities, and some do not eat out at restaurants at all.

**Top options for formal client entertainment:**

*Jerusalem:*
- **Shmoné** (meaning "8"): Israel's first Michelin-starred restaurant (2025–26). Modern Jewish cuisine, tasting menu approximately ₪450–600+ per person. Kosher meat. Book 4–6 weeks ahead.
- **Machneyuda**: Celebrity chef Uri Navon's flagship, open-kitchen format, famously energetic atmosphere. Kosher. Book 6–8 weeks ahead.

*Tel Aviv:*
- **OCD**: Israeli omakase-style tasting, 8 seats only, La Liste globally ranked. Kosher. Weeks-long waitlist — plan well ahead.
- **Pastel (Sarona Market)**: French-Israeli fusion, La Liste 2026 recognition. Accessible and atmospheric — a reliable choice when OCD is full.

*Non-kosher options for secular client entertainment:*
Tel Aviv's Florentin, Neve Tzedek, and Rothschild Boulevard neighbourhoods have excellent non-kosher restaurants with full wine and cocktail menus — well-suited for secular or international clients with no dietary restrictions.

## Bleisure: extending your business trip

Israel's density of world-class sites relative to its area makes it unusually well-suited to bleisure extensions. Most of the country's headline experiences are within two hours of Tel Aviv.

**Half-day from Tel Aviv:**
- **Jaffa Old Port** (30-minute taxi from the Azrieli district): 4,000-year-old port city with galleries, restaurants, and a flea market. Walk from the hilltop clock tower to the harbour and back in 2–3 hours.
- **Caesarea** (50 minutes by car): Roman harbour city and Crusader fortress on the Mediterranean — one of the best-preserved ancient sites in the region, entirely accessible without a guide in a half-day.
- **Tel Aviv beaches and the White City**: The UNESCO-listed Bauhaus architecture of Tel Aviv's White City is walkable from central hotels. The beach along the Tayelet is a 10-minute walk from the hotel belt.

**One- or two-day extension:**
- **Jerusalem**: A full day in Jerusalem (Old City, Western Wall, Mahane Yehuda market) is one of the most distinctive urban experiences available anywhere — less than 30 minutes from Tel Aviv by train.
- **Dead Sea**: 90 minutes from Tel Aviv by car or sherut taxi; the experience of floating in the lowest point on Earth takes less than two hours including the drive to a beach resort.
- **Galilee day trip**: Nazareth, the Sea of Galilee, and the Golan Heights are reachable as a one-day circuit from Tel Aviv by hire car — a full itinerary guide is at [/3-days-in-galilee](/3-days-in-galilee).

## Practical checklist for Israel business trips

- **ETA-IL**: Since January 2025, most nationalities that previously entered Israel visa-free now require an Electronic Travel Authorisation. Apply at least 72 hours before travel via the official PIBA portal. Fee: approximately ₪25. See our [full visa guide](/visa-information).
- **SIM or eSIM**: Israeli mobile data is excellent and competitively priced. An eSIM for Israel from Airalo or a local prepaid from a Partner/Cellcom/Hot Mobile counter at Ben Gurion arrivals will keep you connected from the moment you land. See [Israel eSIM guide](/israel-esim).
- **Currency**: The New Israeli Shekel (₪ / NIS). Cards are accepted virtually everywhere in business contexts, but carry some cash for cafes, market lunches, and tips. See [currency and tipping guide](/israel-tipping-currency).
- **Electrical standard**: 220V, Type H (three-pin Y-shaped) or Type C/E. Most business hotels supply universal adaptors — confirm at check-in.
- **Airport security**: Ben Gurion security questioning is more thorough than most international airports. Arrive at least **3 hours** before an international departure. Domestic and regional flights: 2 hours.
- **Gett app**: Download before you land. It is the dominant taxi and rideshare app in Israel; generates PDF receipts suitable for business expense claims.
