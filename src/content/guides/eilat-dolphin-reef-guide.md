Dolphin Reef is one of the few places in the world where you can snorkel alongside free-ranging bottlenose dolphins in natural seawater — not a tank, not a performance, and with no guarantee the dolphins will cooperate. That honest unpredictability is the point. Opened in 1990 at the southern end of Eilat's waterfront, the reef has grown into an institution: a marine reserve, a research centre, a therapy facility, and one of Eilat's most consistently recommended experiences.

This guide explains what actually happens on a visit — what each entry tier gives you, what the dolphin encounter realistically looks like, and how to fit Dolphin Reef into a broader Eilat itinerary.

---

## What Dolphin Reef is (and isn't)

Dolphin Reef is **not a dolphinarium**. There is no show, no choreographed jumping, and no trainer standing at poolside with a fish bucket. The facility encloses a section of the natural Red Sea bay with safety nets, creating a protected lagoon where a resident pod of bottlenose dolphins lives as naturally as the bay environment allows. The dolphins can — and sometimes do — swim out toward the open nets, return when they choose, and spend the day doing whatever dolphins do when nobody tells them to perform.

Visitors entering the water on snorkel or dive sessions are entering the dolphins' environment, not the other way around. The animals have been acclimated to human presence over decades, and many choose to approach with genuine curiosity. Others don't. A Dolphin Reef session where the dolphins remain at distance is still a session in open Red Sea water with free-ranging dolphins nearby — just a quieter one.

The site opened in 1990. The pod currently numbers around six to eight animals; the exact composition shifts as individual dolphins age and occasionally new animals arrive. A small research team operates out of the facility and the site has peer-reviewed academic publications on dolphin cognition and behaviour. Therapeutic programmes for adults and children with autism, developmental disabilities and post-trauma recovery run alongside the regular tourist operation — a separate track managed with clinical oversight.

## The experience tiers

### Beach and observation pier

The lowest entry level covers the beach, the floating observation pier and the café-restaurant. From the pier — a structure built directly above the lagoon — you can watch the dolphins in the water below. When the pod is active near the pier, the views are close and sustained; you can watch their behaviour without getting wet. This tier suits visitors who cannot or prefer not to snorkel, families with very young children, and anyone who wants to decide on the water after they arrive.

The pier is also a practical photography platform — the light is good in the morning, the water is clear, and the distance is short enough for zoom photography without specialist equipment.

### Snorkel session

The most popular option. A guided group enters the lagoon with snorkel gear, following an instructor into the water at specific entry points. The instructor manages the session: regulates depth, keeps the group calm and quiet, and positions participants to give the dolphins the best chance to approach of their own accord. Sessions last roughly an hour in the water; the dolphins are free to engage or not.

Snorkel equipment is available to hire on site. Wetsuits are also available — the Red Sea is warm but a wetsuit extends comfort during a longer session and provides minor protection on any coral-adjacent entry. Minimum swimming ability and age requirements apply; check the current policy at dolphinreef.co.il before booking, particularly for children.

### Dive session

PADI-certified divers can access a deeper-water dive session in the lagoon. The underwater perspective changes the encounter significantly — the dolphins move at depth, and seeing the pod from below is a different experience from surface snorkeling. Dive gear is available to hire. The dive session typically has a smaller group size than the snorkel session.

If you want to combine Dolphin Reef with serious Red Sea diving at other Eilat sites — Coral Beach, the walls and pinnacles further south — see the [Eilat diving and snorkeling guide](/eilat-diving-snorkeling) for a full site comparison.

### Encounter programmes

A separate track of small-group therapeutic sessions operates alongside the tourist programme. These are not available as walk-in tourist products — they are organised in advance with clinical and educational institutions working with specific participant groups. If you are travelling with a child who participates in dolphin-assisted therapy, contact the facility directly at dolphinreef.co.il well in advance of your trip.

## Practical logistics

**Location.** The entrance is on the coastal road approximately 3 km south of the North Beach hotel strip, past the central part of the promenade. The address is on Route 90 on the waterfront; a navigation app searching "Dolphin Reef Eilat" takes you directly.

**Getting there.** Short taxi or rideshare from the hotel strip (5–8 minutes). Walking takes 30–35 minutes south along the promenade — comfortable in October through April, very exposed in June through September when daytime heat regularly exceeds 38°C. Bus 15 runs along the coastal road; ask at your hotel for the current timetable.

**Hours and entry.** The site opens at 09:00 daily. Current hours and session timetables — including Shabbat arrangements — appear on dolphinreef.co.il. Eilat is a secular city and Dolphin Reef typically operates on Shabbat, but confirm directly rather than assume.

**Prices.** Entry fees vary by tier (beach/pier, snorkel, dive) and change periodically. The backlog research figure of approximately ₪100–150 for snorkel sessions provides a rough orientation, but check the official site for current pricing before you visit. Equipment hire is charged separately.

**Booking.** Book snorkel and dive sessions in advance during July–August, Passover, Sukkot and any Israeli school holiday period — capacity is capped and the popular 09:00–10:30 slot fills earliest. Off-peak, walk-in access for beach and pier entry is usually possible without advance booking.

**Onsite facilities.** There is a café and restaurant on the floating pier — useful if you are making a half-day visit. The kitchen overlooks the lagoon. Parking is available.

## Photography at Dolphin Reef

Dolphin Reef permits personal cameras and action cameras (GoPro, etc.) in the water. Flash photography is prohibited in the dolphin interaction zones — flash disorients the animals. Underwater camera housings and rentable waterproof cameras are available at the site if you don't have your own.

From the observation pier, a standard zoom lens (70–200mm equivalent) gives tight framing of dolphins in the lagoon below. Morning light — particularly in the first two hours after opening — falls well on the water surface and gives better contrast for surface photography than the high midday sun.

## Combining Dolphin Reef with the rest of Eilat

Dolphin Reef works best as a half-day anchor, usually the morning of a day that continues south to other coastal sites.

**[Coral Beach Nature Reserve](/eilat-beach-guide)** is 3 km south of Dolphin Reef along the coastal road — a 5-minute drive or a pleasant 35-minute walk. The reserve protects the most northerly coral reef in the world and allows unguided snorkeling directly from the beach. Entry includes snorkel gear hire. The combination of Dolphin Reef in the morning and Coral Beach in the afternoon fills a day entirely on the water.

The **[Eilat snorkeling guide](/eilat-snorkeling-guide)** covers Coral Beach and other snorkeling sites in detail if you want to compare options before committing.

**Underwater Observatory Marine Park** is 7 km south of the city centre — a family-oriented above- and below-water marine museum with a chamber descending 6 metres below sea level surrounded by Red Sea fish. It is the logical add-on for visitors who want reef life without getting wet. See [Eilat travel guide](/eilat-travel-guide) for the full overview of these southern coastal sites.

## Honest summary

Dolphin Reef is one of the most defensible marine-mammal tourism experiences available anywhere — the dolphins are free-ranging and not performance-trained, the environment is as close to natural as a managed bay allows, and the research and therapy programmes add legitimate institutional substance to the commercial operation.

The honest downside is that you cannot control the dolphins. A session where the pod stays at distance is entirely possible. Most visitors find the experience worth it; a few find a quiet session underwhelming compared to the price point. Going in with accurate expectations — an encounter in natural water with animals that are not obliged to engage — generally produces a satisfying visit.
