The **Sea of Galilee** — the Kinneret, in Hebrew — is a freshwater lake of about 166 square kilometres in the Jordan Valley, 215 metres below sea level. It is the heart of the Israeli Galilee, one of Christianity's most sacred landscapes, and the country's top nature and freshwater destination. Where you stay at the lake determines what circuit is feasible in a day, how far you walk to reach a sunrise on the water, and whether dinner means a kibbutz dining room or a Tiberias restaurant strip.

The accommodation landscape extends well beyond Tiberias city. The western shore has lakeside kibbutz guesthouses at Migdal and Ginosar; the northern shore has pilgrim retreat centres within reach of Capernaum; the eastern shore sits below the Golan Heights plateau; and the southern tip connects to the Jordan River and Yardenit baptism site. This guide covers the full lake picture — all four shores — and where to stay by purpose.

For city-specific hotel picks in Tiberias (Scots Hotel, U Boutique Kinneret, Leonardo Club), see the [best hotels in Tiberias guide](/best-hotels-tiberias).

## Understanding the four shores

Before choosing a hotel, it helps to understand how the Kinneret's four shorelines function for tourists:

**Western shore (Tiberias → Migdal → Nof Ginosar → Capernaum):** The main tourist spine. Route 90 runs the length of the western shore from Tiberias north to the Jordan River at the top of the lake. All the major Christian heritage sites — Magdala, the Mount of Beatitudes, Tabgha, Capernaum — are on this corridor. Tiberias city is at the south end; Capernaum is approximately 20 kilometres north. This shore has the highest concentration of hotels, kibbutz guesthouses, and pilgrim centres.

**Northern shore (Capernaum → Jordan River → Yardenit approach):** The Jordan River enters the lake from the north. The northern-shore road (Route 87) connects the western and eastern shores and passes near the Daughters of Galilee monastery and several pilgrim facilities. The crossing point is where the ferry from Tiberias docks at Kibbutz Ein Gev.

**Eastern shore (Kibbutz Ein Gev → Hamat Gader hot springs → Route 92 south):** The least-visited and most scenic shore for those who want Golan panoramas and seclusion. Kibbutz Ein Gev is the main accommodation node. Route 92 runs the eastern shore and connects to Hamat Gader (Roman hot springs complex) at the confluence of the Yarmouk and Jordan rivers in the south-east corner.

**Southern shore (Tzemach → Yardenit → Kibbutz Kinneret area):** The Yardenit baptism site on the Jordan River is at the south end of the lake. Kibbutz Kinneret — one of Israel's oldest kibbutzim, founded in 1910 — has guesthouses in a peaceful agricultural setting.

## Tiberias city — transport hub, restaurant access, lake views

Tiberias is where most first-time visitors to the Sea of Galilee base, and for practical reasons: it has the most hotels at every price tier, the lake's main bus station (connections to Jerusalem and Tel Aviv), the largest restaurant strip, the Tiberias jetty for boat tours, and Hamat Tiberias hot springs 4 kilometres south.

The standout properties in the city are the **Scots Hotel** (5-star, converted 1885 Scottish mission hospital, Church of Scotland-owned, wine cellar and lake terrace) and the **U Boutique Kinneret Hotel** (4-star, contemporary design, rooftop bar, lakefront rooms). At the family and all-inclusive tier, the **Leonardo Club Hotel Tiberias** has a private beach and pool on the southern waterfront.

For all city-specific detail — per-property profiles, booking timing, Shabbat notes, and the budget hostel tier — see the [best hotels in Tiberias guide](/best-hotels-tiberias). The rest of this guide covers the lake's other zones.

## Kibbutz Ginosar — private lake beach + the Jesus Boat

**Nof Ginosar Kibbutz Hotel** (Kibbutz Ginosar; approximately 10 kilometres north of Tiberias on the western shore) is the most recommended accommodation on the lake for Christian pilgrim visitors and for anyone who prioritises direct lake access over city-centre proximity.

The kibbutz has a **private freshwater beach** on the Kinneret — one of the best swimming spots on the lake, with clear water and a grassy lawn edge. It also has a large outdoor pool, kayak and rowing boat access from its own jetty, and sports facilities spread across the grounds.

What makes Nof Ginosar genuinely distinctive is the **Yigal Allon Museum** on the same grounds, which houses the **Ancient Boat** — a 2,000-year-old wooden fishing vessel excavated from the lake in 1986. The boat dates to the 1st century CE, contemporary with the Gospel accounts of fishermen on the Kinneret; for Christian pilgrim visitors, arriving at a hotel where this boat sits in a climate-controlled pavilion a few minutes' walk away is a significant experience. Museum admission is included in the room rate for staying guests.

**Location advantage:** Capernaum is 12 kilometres north; Magdala and the Duc In Altum church are 4 kilometres south; the Mount of Beatitudes and the Church of the Multiplication of the Loaves and Fishes are 15–18 kilometres north. This makes Nof Ginosar the most efficient base for the northern-shore circuit without needing to drive from Tiberias each morning.

**Who it suits:** Christian pilgrim groups and independent pilgrim visitors; families who want a lake beach and pool base; travellers who want a quieter pastoral setting than Tiberias city; birdwatching visitors (the kibbutz grounds attract resident species year-round). **The trade-off:** Tiberias restaurants are 10–15 minutes by car, so this is not a city walkable base. ## Migdal and Magdala — pilgrim centre on an archaeological site

The village of **Migdal** (ancient Magdala, the town of Mary Magdalene) sits on the western shore approximately 5 kilometres north of Tiberias. In 2009, during the construction of the Magdala Center pilgrim facility, workers uncovered a **first-century CE synagogue** — one of only seven synagogues from the time of Jesus ever found in Israel. The discovery paused and then reshaped the construction project; the centre that was built around the archaeological find is now known as **Duc In Altum** (Latin for "Put Out Into the Deep," from Luke 5:4).

The **Magdala Center** is a pilgrim retreat operated by the Legionaries of Christ. It has a chapel of considerable architectural quality — designed specifically so that women pilgrims visiting the site can symbolically encounter Mary Magdalene as a figure in her home town — along with the uncovered synagogue floor, a small museum of finds from the excavation, and guesthouse accommodation primarily designed for pilgrim groups. Individual rooms are sometimes available for self-organising pilgrim travellers; contact the centre directly at magdala.org for current availability and rates. Rates are not listed here as they are subject to change and depend on group size and programme.

**Who it suits:** Catholic and Christian pilgrim visitors for whom the Magdala connection and the on-site synagogue are primary draws; visitors wanting a spiritually-oriented stay rather than a resort-format hotel; organised church groups. **Note:** The Magdala Center is not a standard hotel and is primarily oriented toward pilgrim groups — individual bookings may have limited availability in peak pilgrimage season.

## Northern shore — pilgrim retreat centres near Capernaum

The area around **Capernaum** and the **Mount of Beatitudes** has a small cluster of pilgrim retreat centres that are distinct from standard hotels. These are primarily used by Catholic and Christian pilgrimage groups but increasingly available for individual bookings in quieter periods.

**Domus Galilaeae** is a pilgrim retreat centre on a hillside above the northern shore, near Korazim, with panoramic views across the Kinneret toward the Golan Heights. The centre was built under the auspices of the Neocatechumenal Way and serves as a major pilgrimage formation centre; Pope John Paul II visited in 2000 and Pope Francis in 2014. It has residential facilities for larger groups. Availability for independent travellers varies; book via the centre's direct website.

Several Franciscan-managed guesthouses near the **Capernaum complex** offer basic pilgrim accommodation focused on proximity to the site rather than resort facilities. These are valued by pilgrims who want to arrive at Capernaum before the day-tour groups for a quieter early-morning visit.

**Who suits this zone:** Pilgrim visitors on a structured retreat programme; groups attending formation weeks or spiritual conferences; travellers who specifically want to be within walking distance of Capernaum and the northern biblical sites.

## Eastern shore — Kibbutz Ein Gev and Golan panoramas

**Ein Gev Holiday Resort** (Kibbutz Ein Gev; eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee) offers the most distinctive position on the lake: sitting directly below the Golan Heights plateau, facing west across the open Kinneret to the Tiberias ridge. Sunsets from the east shore — watching the light leave the lake from the Galilee side — are among the most photographed views in the region.

The resort has room options across a modest range of grades, set within the kibbutz grounds among date palms and lawned gardens. It has a lake beach and modest facilities. The **Ein Gev Fish Restaurant** at the kibbutz is a long-standing regional institution — known for St Peter's fish (Galilee tilapia) fresh from the lake.

**Access logistics (important):** There is no direct road connection south from Tiberias along the western and then eastern shores. Getting from Tiberias to Ein Gev requires either:
- **By boat:** Regular tourist ferry crossings from the Tiberias jetty take approximately 30–40 minutes; several operators run the route.
- **By road:** Drive north from Tiberias on Route 90, cross the Jordan River at the northern tip of the lake, then south on Route 92 along the eastern shore — approximately 45–50 minutes by car.

**Who it suits:** Visitors who want genuine lakeside quietude and Golan panoramas; those already routing through the Golan Heights (Ein Gev works well as a final-night stop before heading south); birdwatching visitors (the eastern shore and Hula Valley to the north form a major flyway corridor); travellers who have already done the western-shore circuit and want a different perspective. **Not ideal as a logistics base** for multiple-day Galilee touring — the ferry or road detour adds time to every excursion. ## Southern shore — Kibbutz Kinneret area

The **Kibbutz Kinneret** area at the southern tip of the lake is the least tourist-developed part of the shoreline and the most tranquil. Kibbutz Kinneret — established in 1910 as one of the world's first kibbutzim — has a small guesthouse operation and cemetery that is a pilgrimage site for secular Zionist visitors (the poets Rachel Bluwstein and Naomi Shemer are buried here).

This area suits visitors who want a genuinely quiet lakeside stop, are interested in early Zionist pioneering history, or are continuing south toward the Yardenit baptism site (where the Jordan River exits the lake, 3 kilometres from Kibbutz Kinneret) and the Beit She'an area. It is not the right base for day-tripping the northern Christian sites — the distances add significantly to the daily drive.

## Booking patterns and seasonal notes

The Sea of Galilee has pronounced **pilgrimage seasons** that affect availability more sharply than most Israeli destinations:

**Easter and Passover (March–April):** These usually fall in the same week or consecutive weeks. The western-shore Christian circuit is at maximum capacity: Nof Ginosar, Magdala Center, and northern-shore pilgrim centres are block-booked by tour operators months in advance. Even Tiberias city hotels fill. Book 4–6 months ahead for Easter if your dates are fixed.

**Christmas and New Year:** The pilgrimage traffic peaks again at Christmas. The Tiberias hotels fill from mid-December; Nof Ginosar and Magdala are typically booked solid across the Christmas–New Year period by organised groups.

**Israeli summer school holidays (July–August):** Domestic Israeli tourism drives up occupancy at the lake beaches — Nof Ginosar's private beach and the Leonardo Club are particularly popular with Israeli families. Prices rise and the kibbutz beaches become crowded. Early morning swims are still possible before the crowds arrive.

**Best value seasons:** November, March (non-Easter years), and October offer pleasant weather, lower prices, and significantly lighter crowds. The Kinneret in autumn light — warm days, cool evenings, birdwatching season approaching in the Hula Valley — is consistently rated by repeat visitors as the ideal time.

**Shabbat:** All hotels remain open. Kibbutz dining rooms operate on Shabbat (Friday dinner and Saturday lunch are typically included in the room rate). Friday-afternoon traffic on Route 90 can be heavy as Israeli families head to lake areas — factor this into arrival timing.
