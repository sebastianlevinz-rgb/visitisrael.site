**Beit Alfa** holds what many archaeologists consider the finest ancient synagogue mosaic in Israel — a complete 6th-century CE floor in three distinct panels that have survived in extraordinary condition since their discovery during kibbutz ploughing in 1928. The site is small and the visit is short, but the mosaic is genuinely exceptional: a rare case where an ancient synagogue floor is almost entirely intact, still in place on the ground where it was laid nearly 1,500 years ago.

The synagogue belonged to a Jewish farming community that lived in the Jezreel Valley during the late Byzantine period. The mosaic was commissioned from two named artists — Marianos and his son Hanina — whose signature inscription survives in the northernmost panel. For a mosaic of this age and quality, the signed commission is an extraordinary documentary survival.

---

## The three mosaic panels

The floor is divided into three panels running north to south, each with a distinct subject. The logic of the sequence corresponds to the liturgical layout of the synagogue: worshippers entered from the south and faced north toward the Torah Shrine.

### Panel 1 — Akeidat Yitzhak (Binding of Isaac)

The southernmost panel shows the **Binding of Isaac** (Genesis 22) — one of the most frequently depicted narratives in ancient Jewish art. The composition follows the standard Byzantine rendering: Abraham stands on the left holding a curved knife above his head; Isaac, bound, appears on the sacrificial altar; a ram is caught by its horns in a thicket to the right; two servants wait with a donkey; a divine hand emerges from above to stay Abraham's arm.

The figures are stylised in the manner of Byzantine folk art — frontal faces, schematic drapery, proportions that prioritise narrative clarity over naturalism. The hand of God descending from a cloud in the upper corner is among the most vivid elements; the Hebrew inscription adjacent to it reads *"and Abraham stretched forth his hand"* (Genesis 22:10).

### Panel 2 — The zodiac wheel

The central and dominant panel is a **full zodiac wheel** — the most complex and most debated element of the floor. The wheel contains all twelve zodiac signs with their Hebrew month names written in each segment: Nisan (Aries), Iyyar (Taurus), Sivan (Gemini), and so on around the circle. In the centre, **Helios** drives a four-horse chariot (quadriga) across the sky; personified seasons occupy the four corners of the square frame (Spring as a woman with flowers, Summer with sheaves of wheat, Autumn with grapes, Winter wrapped in a cloak).

The Greek sun-god figure in a Jewish house of worship has been discussed since the mosaic's discovery. The scholarly consensus treats it as an artistic convention of the late-antique eastern Mediterranean — Byzantine Jewish artists used the same decorative vocabulary as their Christian neighbours, adapting inherited motifs to Jewish liturgical purposes. Several other Galilean synagogues of the 4th–6th centuries (Hammat Tiberias, Zippori/Sepphoris, Na'aran) share this iconographic programme. The zodiac's primary function was calendrical: it marked the Jewish festival year against the astronomical seasons. The artists' names in the dedicatory inscription — Greek-influenced "Marianos" and Hebrew "Hanina" — reflect the same cultural bilingualism as the mosaic's content.

### Panel 3 — The Torah Shrine

The northernmost panel, closest to the apse where the Torah Ark was kept, depicts the **sacred furniture of the synagogue and the Jerusalem Temple**. At the centre is a stylised Torah Ark (the ark containing the Torah scrolls, shown with doors ajar and a flame inside); flanking it are two seven-branched **menorahs** on their traditional tripod bases; additional ritual objects fill the composition — a shofar (ram's horn), a lulav (palm branch), an etrog (citron), an incense shovel, and birds perched on the menorah branches.

This panel makes explicit what the zodiac panel implies: the zodiac calibrates the festival calendar; the Torah Ark panel depicts the sacred objects associated with those festivals and the Temple cult. The three panels together form a coherent theological programme moving from narrative history (Akeidat Yitzhak) through cosmic time (zodiac) to sacred space (Torah Shrine).

## The builders: Marianos and Hanina

In the dedicatory inscription at the base of the Torah Shrine panel, the mosaic artists signed their work: *"Done by the hand of Marianos the craftsman and his son Hanina."* This inscription — in Aramaic, the everyday language of Jewish Palestine in the Byzantine period — is one of the few surviving examples of a Byzantine-era craftsman signing a Jewish sacred mosaic. Whether Marianos and Hanina worked on commission or donated their labour as a pious act is unknown.

A second inscription in the southernmost panel records the donors who paid for the mosaic: the community members whose names were set in stone alongside their contributions. The combination of donor inscriptions and artist signatures gives Beit Alfa an unusual documentary richness for a 6th-century site.

## Visiting the site

The mosaic is protected inside a purpose-built **shelter structure** that shields it from direct sunlight and rain while keeping it accessible and visible. The floor is displayed at the original level, protected by a transparent covering; lighting is adequate for detailed examination, and the colour of the tesserae — cream, ochre, blue-grey, black — is well-preserved.

Information panels in English and Hebrew explain each panel and the broader context of Byzantine synagogue mosaic art. The site is fully flat and covered, making it accessible in summer heat and winter rain alike.

**Allow 30–45 minutes.** After the mosaic, the rest of the site is minimal — a small garden with ancient olive trees and a ticket office. The value is concentrated in the shelter.

## The Jezreel Valley circuit {#jezreel-circuit}

Beit Alfa is most rewarding when combined with neighbouring sites into a **full Jezreel Valley day**. All four INPA sites below accept the annual parks pass:

| Site | Distance from Beit Alfa | Highlight | INPA Pass |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gan HaShlosha / Sachne** | 4 km east | Spring-fed pools at 28°C — best natural swimming in northern Israel | Yes |
| **Beit She'an National Park** | 10 km east | Most complete Roman city ruin in Israel; 7,000-seat theater; earthquake-frozen cardo | Yes |
| **Tel Megiddo (Armageddon)** | 20 km west | UNESCO-listed 26-layer tel; Ahab's water tunnel; panoramic Jezreel Valley views | Yes |
| **Beit She'arim UNESCO necropolis** | 45 km north-west | Largest Jewish catacomb system outside Rome; UNESCO World Heritage Site | Yes |

A practical full-day circuit from Haifa or Nazareth: **Beit She'arim** (morning, 90 min) → **Beit Alfa** (10:30–11:15) → **Gan HaShlosha** (lunch swim, 11:30–14:00) → **Beit She'an** (afternoon, 14:30–17:00). Total driving: ~110 km.

A circuit from Tiberias: **Beit She'an** (morning, 08:00–10:30) → **Beit Alfa** (10:45–11:30) → **Gan HaShlosha** (swim, 12:00–15:00) → **Tel Megiddo** optional (15:30–17:30). Total driving: ~90 km.

## Getting there

Beit Alfa has no regular public bus service. A **rental car** is strongly recommended.

**By car from Tiberias:** Route 90 south (40 min to Beit She'an), then Route 669 west for 10 km to Kibbutz Heftziba.

**By car from Haifa:** Route 75 east toward Afula, then Route 669 east — approximately 50 km, 45–50 minutes.

**By car from Nazareth:** Route 60 south to Afula, then Route 669 east — approximately 40 km, 35–40 minutes.

**By car from Tel Aviv:** Route 6 north, Junction 19, Route 90 south to Beit She'an junction, then Route 669 west — approximately 130 km, 1.5–2 hours.
