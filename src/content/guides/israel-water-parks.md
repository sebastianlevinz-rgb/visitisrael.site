Israel's family travel scene goes well beyond beaches and history sites. The country has several dedicated water parks, amusement parks, and interactive family attractions that work well as standalone days out or as breaks between sightseeing-heavy itineraries. Most are reachable within an hour from Tel Aviv or Jerusalem, and Israeli families treat them as core summer entertainment — which means they fill fast on weekends and holidays.

This guide covers the main options region by region, with practical logistics and honest notes on what each park does well and for whom.

## Shefayim Water Park — Israel's flagship

**Shefayim Water Park** (Afarsek Beach, Kibbutz Shefayim) is the country's largest and most popular water park, and consistently cited as the best single-day family attraction in the centre of the country. It sits directly on the Mediterranean coast, so the water slides and the sea are combined in the same facility.

The park has 25+ slides across different speed and difficulty grades — from enclosed tube slides and open drop slides for teenagers and adults, to gentler family raft rides and a dedicated toddler splash zone. The wave pool generates controlled swells that Israeli families use for bodyboarding. There is also a lazy river circuit, a heated indoor pool and slide complex that extends the season into cooler months, and direct access to a supervised Mediterranean beach.

**What works particularly well at Shefayim:**
- The adjacent Kibbutz Shefayim hotel offers an overnight package that includes park entry — a genuinely good deal for families not wanting to drive back to Tel Aviv at the end of a full day.
- Weekday mornings (arrive before 10am) before the crowds build are significantly better than weekend afternoons.
- The Mediterranean beach access is a free extension of the day — the park proper closes in the early evening but the beach remains open.

**Who it suits:** Families with children aged 3 and upward through to adults. The spread of slide types means different ages in a family group can all find something appropriate. Teenagers using the high-speed slides and toddlers in the splash zone can operate simultaneously without adults having to choose a single area.

**Getting there:** Approximately 30 minutes by car from central Tel Aviv via Route 2 (coastal motorway); 25 minutes from Netanya. By train: Binyamina or Netanya station, then taxi (10–15 min). A free shuttle sometimes operates from Netanya station on peak days — check the park's website before travelling.

**Tickets and booking:** Book in advance online, especially for weekends and holidays. Prices are in the ₪100–200+/person range depending on season and whether indoor sections are included — check the park's official site for current rates. No exact figures are hardcoded here as rates adjust seasonally.

## Superland — thrill rides for older children and teens

**Superland** in Rishon LeZion (about 20 minutes south of Tel Aviv by car) is Israel's largest dedicated amusement park, structured around roller coasters, drop towers, and themed ride zones rather than water.

The park has around 50 attractions covering different speed and intensity levels. The headline rides include a wooden roller coaster (one of only two in Israel), a large drop tower, spinning rides, and a water-ride element in the form of a log flume. Many of the larger rides have minimum height requirements (typically 120–140 cm) which effectively makes this a park better suited to ages 6 and above than to toddlers, who have limited options.

**Who it suits:** School-age children through to adults who like fairground rides. Teens in particular tend to prefer Superland over Shefayim because the focus on thrill attractions holds their attention longer than a water park. Families with a wide age spread may find it harder to satisfy toddlers and teenagers simultaneously here.

**Getting there:** By car, 20 minutes from central Tel Aviv via Route 44. By light rail: the Tel Aviv–Rishon LeZion Red Line (opened in phases 2024–2026) connects central Tel Aviv to Rishon LeZion, with the park accessible from the Rishon LeZion Newe Hadar or Rishon LeZion HaRishonim stations plus a short taxi or walk. Check the light rail map for current station positions — extensions were in progress in 2026.

**Tickets:** The park operates a single-entry/ride wristband system. Book online for discount rates; peak-day queues for the popular rides can be significant, so arriving before 11am gives better access.

## Luna Park Tel Aviv — classic fairground for younger children

**Luna Park Tel Aviv** occupies a corner of Yarkon Park in north Tel Aviv and offers a traditional funfair-style experience: carousel, bumper cars, small roller coasters, spinning teacups, and a Ferris wheel with city views. The scale is smaller than Superland and the rides are gentler — it is best suited to children aged 3–10 rather than teenagers seeking adrenaline.

The setting inside Yarkon Park makes it easy to combine with a picnic on the park's lawns, a paddle boat on the Yarkon River, or access to the park's other facilities including skate park and climbing wall. The park operates mainly on weekends and during school holidays rather than every day — verify current operating hours before visiting.

Luna Park is also notable for being one of the more affordable day-out options in Tel Aviv: individual ride tickets rather than an all-day wristband means families can calibrate how much they spend.

## Gal-Gil — lakeside water park on the Sea of Galilee

**Gal-Gil** (Sapir Beach, Sea of Galilee) is the main water park option for families spending time in the Galilee region. Located on the southern shore of the Kinneret (Sea of Galilee), it combines water slides with direct access to the lake itself.

The park operates in the summer season (roughly May through October) and includes a set of water slides, children's pools, a beach volleyball court, and the attraction of swimming directly in the Sea of Galilee — one of Israel's most famous bodies of water and a significant site in Christian pilgrimage tradition. The lake water is warm from June through September, reaching 26–28°C at peak summer.

**Who it suits:** Families already basing themselves in Tiberias, Kibbutz Ein Gev, or the Sea of Galilee area. It is not worth driving from Tel Aviv specifically to Gal-Gil when Shefayim is closer, but as part of a Galilee itinerary it is a very natural fit — the lake is beautiful and the park itself is well-run and family-friendly.

**Getting there:** By car, approximately 30 minutes from Tiberias. Public buses serve the southern Kinneret shore; check Moovit for current routes from Tiberias.

## Mini Israel — scale models at Latrun

**Mini Israel** (Sha'ar HaGai / Latrun, midway between Tel Aviv and Jerusalem on Route 1) is not a water park or amusement park in the conventional sense — it is a large outdoor walking attraction where around 385 scale models recreate Israel's iconic landmarks at 1:25 scale.

The models include the Western Wall and Old City of Jerusalem, the Dome of the Rock, Masada, the Bahá'í Gardens in Haifa, Ben Gurion Airport with moving aircraft, the Knesset, Tel Aviv's skyline including the Azrieli Towers, and dozens more. The grounds are fully paved and stroller-accessible. Audio stations provide commentary at key models.

**Why it works for families:**
- For children who have visited Masada or Jerusalem, seeing a scale replica is a genuinely enjoyable game of recognition.
- For families who cannot get to all the sites (a common constraint on short trips), Mini Israel provides a condensed visual overview.
- The models are extremely detailed and adults find them interesting as well.
- The on-site snack facilities and shaded seating areas make it a comfortable 2–3 hour stop.

**Getting there:** Directly off Route 1 (Tel Aviv–Jerusalem highway) at the Latrun interchange. By car from Tel Aviv, approximately 30 minutes; from Jerusalem, 20 minutes. The adjacent Latrun Armoured Corps Museum (free outdoor display of tanks and military vehicles) appeals to older children and can be combined in the same half-day.

## Eilat for water-loving families

Eilat does not have a traditional water park format, but the Red Sea coast offers family-friendly water experiences that are in some ways better:

**Coral Beach Nature Reserve** is a shallow-entry snorkelling zone where children aged 6 and up can see live coral and tropical fish with just a mask and snorkel. The water is crystal-clear and extremely calm — no waves, no current — making it safe for older children. The reserve has changing facilities, equipment rental, and supervised entry. [See the full Eilat diving and snorkelling guide](/eilat-diving-snorkeling) for details.

**Hotel pool complexes on North Beach:** The large Eilat resort hotels (Royal Beach, Dan Panorama, Isrotel Royal Garden, Herods) operate large pool complexes open to hotel guests. Many offer day-pass access to non-residents during weekdays — worth calling ahead to confirm. The Red Sea water temperature is 24–28°C year-round, making Eilat the only place in Israel where outdoor water activities are genuinely comfortable from November to March.

**Glass-bottom boat tours** depart from the Eilat marina and provide a sea-life viewing experience accessible to children who are not yet comfortable snorkelling. The 45-minute cruises are family-standard and require no swimming.

## Planning logistics

**Best season:** July and August are peak season for water parks — warmest water, school holidays, and maximum park operations. The trade-off is maximum crowds and advance booking is essential. **June and September** offer the best balance: warm enough for water parks, fewer domestic crowds, and more availability at family hotels. April–May (spring) are excellent for Mini Israel and the Sea of Galilee but some water parks are not yet at full summer operation.

**How to get around:** Most of these attractions are easiest to reach by car. Ben Gurion Airport car rentals from major operators are straightforward; a standard family estate car handles most of the country well. [See the car rental guide](/car-rental-israel) for operators and pricing. For families without a car, GetYourGuide and Viator list family day-tour operators that include transport.

**Booking tickets:** Book all major park tickets online in advance, especially for summer weekends. Israeli domestic tourism fills parks fast on Fridays and Saturdays (Jewish weekend). For Shefayim specifically, purchasing a combined hotel + park package through Kibbutz Shefayim's own booking channel often gives better value than buying separately.

**Budget:** Water park entry is roughly ₪100–200/person depending on the park and season. Superland and Mini Israel are in a similar range. A family of four should budget ₪500–900 for park entry alone, plus food (park food is expensive — packing lunch in a cooler bag saves money, and most Israeli parks permit this). See the [Israel travel cost guide](/israel-cost-budget) for broader budget context.

## For younger children: splash pads and free alternatives

Beyond the paid parks, most Israeli cities have free or low-cost family water options:

- **Yarkon Park, Tel Aviv** has splash pads and water play zones that are free and well-maintained — a practical option for toddlers who will enjoy 30 minutes of water play without a full park admission.
- **Gan HaShlosha (Sahne)** in the Jezreel Valley is a national park with natural spring-fed pools at 28°C year-round — one of the most beautiful swimming sites in the country and accessible on the INPA National Parks pass. The pools are safe for children and the site is popular with Israeli families. [See the Israel National Parks Pass guide](/israel-national-parks-pass).
- **Kibbutz beach access** at many Sea of Galilee and Mediterranean kibbutz hotels is available to day visitors for a small fee — often ₪30–60/person for access to beach and pool facilities that are well-maintained and rarely crowded compared to public parks.
