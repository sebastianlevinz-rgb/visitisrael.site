Tel Aviv Pride is the largest Pride event in the Middle East — and by almost any measure one of the most remarkable urban celebrations on the planet. Around 250,000 people attend the parade each year, turning central Tel Aviv into a days-long festival. This guide covers everything you need to plan the trip: dates, route, accommodation timing, the beach scene, the parties, and the practical logistics.

For year-round LGBTQ+ travel guidance across all of Israel, see the [LGBTQ travel in Israel guide](/lgbtq-travel-israel).

## When is Tel Aviv Pride?

The parade falls on the **second Friday of June** each year — typically the second full week of the month, so the date shifts between approximately 7 and 14 June depending on the year.

| Year | Expected parade date |
| --- | --- |
| 2026 | Friday, 12 June (passed) |
| 2027 | Friday, ~11 June |
| 2028 | Friday, ~9 June |

These dates are confirmed by the Tel Aviv Pride organisation several months before the event and can move slightly. **Always verify at the official Tel Aviv Pride website before booking.** The week of surrounding events — parties, film screenings, art exhibitions, beach days, bar specials — runs from roughly Thursday through Sunday around the central Friday parade.

## The parade route

The parade starts at **Gan Meir Park** on King George Street, a few blocks east of Dizengoff Square. This is the gathering point from mid-morning: food stalls, music, and the pre-parade atmosphere build here before the official start.

From Gan Meir Park the parade moves through central Tel Aviv's main streets toward the **beachfront**, typically ending near the Hilton area. The route is announced in the weeks before Pride; it has varied slightly from year to year. Allow two to three hours from start to beach if you march the full length.

After the parade reaches the beach, the free **Ganei Yehoshua Park after-party** takes over — an outdoor event with a family-friendly afternoon wave and a separate evening programme. The park sits just north of the beach near the area where the Yarkon River meets the sea.

**Practical tip:** position yourself at the Gan Meir Park start for the best atmosphere before the crowds compress onto the parade route. If you are more interested in the arrival spectacle than the march, head directly to the beach an hour before the estimated arrival time.

## Accommodation: where to stay and when to book

**Book accommodation three to six months before Pride week at minimum.** Pride week is Tel Aviv's single busiest hotel week — rooms in the best-positioned neighbourhoods sell out months ahead and prices typically reach two to three times normal rates during the five days around the parade.

### Best neighbourhoods for Pride week

**Dizengoff and city centre** is the optimal base: walking distance from Gan Meir Park (parade start), close to the beach and the nightlife strips. Hotels here are the first to fill.

**Hilton Beach area (Old North)** puts you within a five-minute walk of the beach and Gay Beach. The promenade strip has several hotels directly on or adjacent to the beach. Also fills very quickly.

**Florentin** (south Tel Aviv) is a popular choice when central options are full — an authentic neighbourhood with independent cafés and bars, connected to the centre by bus and a 25-minute walk to Gan Meir Park.

**Jaffa** is roughly 5 km south of the parade start, which means transport reliance during an event when roads close and taxis are slow. It is a beautiful neighbourhood — factor in the logistics.

## Hilton Beach and the beach scene

**Hilton Beach** (also known locally as Gay Beach) is the de facto home base of Tel Aviv's LGBTQ+ community on the seafront. Positioned north of the Hilton Hotel, it has operated as an informal community gathering point since the 1990s — not by official designation, but by long social tradition.

During Pride week, arrive before **9am** for a good spot on the sand. The beach runs a full day: coffee and breakfast from nearby cafés, volleyball, swimming, building crowds through the afternoon. Chair hire is available from beach operators (not a fixed price — negotiate on the day).

The beach is public, free to access, and genuinely welcoming to everyone. Metzitzim Beach, slightly further north, has a mixed but similarly welcoming atmosphere.

## The circuit parties and events

The evening programme around Pride week centres on large-scale club events, sometimes called circuit parties, held across Tel Aviv's clubs and outdoor venues. The specific organisers and venues rotate each year — do not assume the same event will be in the same place as the year before.

What remains consistent:
- Major parties run **Thursday through Saturday** evening, with Sunday brunch events and quieter Monday recovery options
- **Tickets sell out weeks or months before Pride** — buy before you travel
- Prices for headline parties typically range from a few hundred shekels upward for the larger events

For the current year's confirmed lineup:
- **Tel Aviv Gay Center** (gaycenter.org.il) is the most reliable Israeli source
- **Time Out Tel Aviv** publishes the full Pride programme in the weeks before
- Platforms like Resident Advisor and GayPartyNetwork list international-circuit events

**HONESTY note:** party organiser lineups, venues, and pricing change significantly year to year. What is listed in any travel article, including this one, may be out of date. Verify with primary sources before purchasing anything.

Beyond the parties, Pride week also includes **film screenings** (TLV International LGBTQ Film Festival often overlaps), **art exhibitions**, and **bar specials** throughout central Tel Aviv.

## Practical logistics for Pride week

### Heat
June in Tel Aviv is warm: typically **30–32°C** during the parade, often humid. The route is fully exposed. Bring:
- Reusable water bottle (refill at kiosks; drink early and often)
- SPF 50 sunscreen + hat
- Light, breathable clothing

### Transport
Road closures during the parade affect central Tel Aviv significantly. **Do not rely on cars or taxis to reach the parade start** — they cannot reach Gan Meir Park once the route is closed. Walk or take public transport to a point outside the closure zone. The Dan bus network reroutes around Pride; check the Dan app on the morning.

### Safety
Tel Aviv Pride is a safe, well-stewarded event with a significant police and volunteer presence. Bag checks operate at large evening events. Follow standard crowd-safety practices at any large outdoor event: identify exit points, agree a meeting point with your group if you separate.

### Language
English is widely spoken in Tel Aviv and by Pride organisers. Event communications are typically in Hebrew and English.

## Pride beyond Tel Aviv

**Jerusalem** holds its own annual Pride march, typically in August. The Jerusalem march has a different character — smaller, more politically charged, with a significant security presence and a history of protest from ultra-Orthodox communities. It is a genuine and important event but operates in a different register than Tel Aviv.

**Haifa** and **Eilat** have growing LGBTQ+ calendars and events around Pride season.

Tel Aviv remains the undisputed centre of gravity.
