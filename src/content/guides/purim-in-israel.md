**Purim** is the Jewish holiday that transforms Israel each spring — and in no city more dramatically than Tel Aviv. On Purim day, Dizengoff Square fills with 200,000–250,000 costumed revellers across multiple music stages, making it one of the world's largest annual costume street parties. Jerusalem observes the holiday a day later (Shushan Purim) with the Adloyada parade and the distinct atmosphere of a city where tradition runs deeper.

For international visitors, Purim offers an accessible, joyful entry point into Israeli Jewish culture — requiring no religious background, no advance tickets, and nothing more than a willingness to join a genuinely celebratory crowd. This guide covers Purim 2027 dates, what to expect in Tel Aviv and Jerusalem, how to attend a megillah reading, and everything else you need to know.

## Purim 2027 dates

| Event | Date | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Fast of Esther | Thursday, 18 March 2027 | Dawn-to-nightfall fast; most tourist activity unaffected |
| Megillah reading (eve) — Tel Aviv, rest of Israel | Saturday night, 21 March | Synagogues + Chabad centres open to visitors |
| Purim day — Tel Aviv, rest of Israel | Sunday, 22 March 2027 | Dizengoff Square street party; main costume celebrations |
| Shushan Purim eve — Jerusalem | Sunday night, 22 March | Jerusalem megillah reading |
| Shushan Purim — Jerusalem | Monday, 23 March 2027 | Jerusalem Adloyada parade; celebrations in walled city |

**Why Jerusalem celebrates one day later**: Under Jewish law, cities that were walled in the time of Joshua observe Purim on the 15th of Adar (Shushan Purim) rather than the 14th. Jerusalem qualifies; Tel Aviv and most other Israeli cities do not. In practice, this means you can attend Purim celebrations in Tel Aviv on Sunday and then catch Jerusalem's Adloyada on Monday.

## Tel Aviv: Dizengoff Square and the city-wide costume party

Tel Aviv's Purim is the defining Israeli street celebration — a city-wide costume party with Dizengoff Square (Kikar Dizengoff) as its heart.

### Dizengoff Square

The raised circular plaza at Dizengoff Circle becomes the main stage for the Tel Aviv Purim party. Multiple live-music stages set up around the circle and on the surrounding streets; DJs and bands run from mid-morning through late evening. The crowd is multigenerational — parents with young children in the morning, a more adult crowd developing through the afternoon and evening.

**What to expect on the day:**
- Streets around Dizengoff are closed to traffic from the morning; approach on foot or by Light Rail
- The crowd peaks between 2–6 pm; earlier (10–11 am) is better for families with children
- Multiple stages playing simultaneously; the atmosphere is similar to a major open-air festival
- Costume vendors and street food carts set up throughout the area
- Alcohol is widely available and heavily consumed — be aware of this in the crowd

**Nearby LGBTQ+ celebrations**: The LGBTQ+ community traditionally marks Purim with a Hilton Beach gathering on Purim day, with costumed crowds from mid-morning. Combined with the Dizengoff party, this gives Tel Aviv two simultaneous focal points for celebration.

### Tel Aviv neighbourhood events

Beyond Dizengoff, Purim spreads across the entire city:

- **Florentin**: alternative, creative crowd; basement parties and street gatherings in the area's industrial spaces
- **Rothschild Boulevard**: Bauhaus backdrop for street parties and outdoor bar events
- **Jaffa/Old Jaffa**: Arab-Jewish mixed character means a different atmosphere; the flea market area has its own Purim character
- **Carmel Market and Nahalat Binyamin**: vendors in costume; the market takes on a festive, chaotic energy

**Tel Aviv circuit parties**: Major club events (Kuli Alma, Dogstar, and the annual Haoman 17 Purim special) sell out weeks before Purim. If you want tickets to the flagship club events, buy before you arrive in Israel — they rarely have tickets at the door on Purim night.

## Jerusalem: Shushan Purim and the Adloyada

Jerusalem celebrates **Shushan Purim** on Monday, 23 March 2027 — one day after the rest of Israel. This one-day offset means Jerusalem has its own distinct Purim atmosphere, combining family parades with religious observance in one of the world's most historically layered cities.

### The Adloyada parade

The Jerusalem Adloyada is the centrepiece of Shushan Purim in Jerusalem — a parade of giant puppet floats, community performances, and costumed processions through one of Jerusalem's neighbourhoods (the German Colony / Emek Refaim area is a common route). The word "adloyada" (עַד דְּלֹא יָדַע) refers to the Talmudic instruction to drink until you cannot tell the difference between "blessed is Mordechai" and "cursed is Haman."

The Jerusalem Adloyada is notably more family-oriented than the Tel Aviv street party — expect children's performances, community dance groups, and a parade atmosphere rather than a music festival. The Jerusalem Municipality publishes the confirmed route and programme in the weeks before Purim; check jerusalem.muni.il for 2027 details.

### Megillah readings in Jerusalem

Jerusalem's synagogues — from the Great Synagogue on King George Street to the historic synagogues of the Jewish Quarter — hold elaborate megillah readings on Shushan Purim eve (Sunday night, March 22) and morning (Monday, March 23). The **Hurva Synagogue** in the Jewish Quarter holds a particularly atmospheric reading given its setting.

Chabad of Jerusalem maintains multiple locations across the city offering open megillah readings. The Western Wall plaza holds readings throughout the evening and morning.

### Old City character during Shushan Purim

The Jewish Quarter of the Old City takes on a festive character during Shushan Purim — costumes are common, bakeries sell hamentashen (triangular pastries), and the Cardo fills with families. The experience is markedly different from Tel Aviv: more contained, more community-based, and set against ancient stone rather than modernist architecture.

## Megillah readings: for visitors of any background

The **megillah** (the Book of Esther) is read aloud twice on Purim — once on the evening before and once on the morning of the holiday. The reading is the ritual heart of Purim: congregants use noisemakers (graggers) to drown out every mention of the villain Haman's name, making the service unexpectedly participatory and loud.

**How to find an open reading:**

- **Chabad**: The easiest option for visitors. Chabad centres in every major Israeli city hold open megillah readings welcoming guests of any background. Find your nearest Chabad reading at [chabad.org/israel](https://www.chabad.org). No advance booking required; arrive before the reading starts (typically at nightfall and again at 9–10 am)
- **Abraham Hostels**: Both the Jerusalem and Tel Aviv branches typically host community Purim events with megillah readings, costume competitions and parties — well-suited to independent travellers
- **Hotel events**: Many hotels with significant Jewish clientele hold in-house megillah readings and Purim parties; ask your hotel concierge in advance
- **Western Wall**: The Kotel plaza hosts megillah readings throughout Purim eve and morning for observant visitors

**What to expect**: the megillah reading takes 30–40 minutes. Hebrew texts with English translation are usually available. No religious knowledge is required. Children are both welcome and encouraged — Purim is explicitly a children's holiday in Jewish tradition. Dress respectfully (covered shoulders and knees at the Western Wall and in synagogues).

## Purim traditions explained for visitors

Understanding the key Purim customs makes the holiday more meaningful to observe:

**Mishloach manot** (gift baskets): on Purim day, Jewish communities exchange food and drink gift packages — typically containing at least two ready-to-eat food items. Walking through Tel Aviv or Jerusalem on Purim, you will see people carrying baskets and decorated packages. Hotels and restaurants often offer guests small Purim packages.

**Matanot la'evyonim** (gifts to the poor): Purim has a strong charitable component. Many organisations collect on Purim day; street collecting is common.

**Costumes**: the Purim costume tradition predates Halloween by centuries. The custom is variously explained as concealing identity (mirroring God's hidden presence in the Esther story) or symbolising the reversal of fortunes at the heart of the Purim narrative.

**Hamentashen**: triangular pastries filled with poppy seed, jam, chocolate or halva, shaped to represent Haman's three-cornered hat. Every bakery in Israel sells them for weeks before and during Purim.

**Drinking**: the Talmudic instruction to drink "ad d'lo yada" (until you cannot tell Mordechai from Haman) is taken seriously by many Israelis. Public drinking is common on Purim day in Tel Aviv particularly. Be aware of this in crowds.

## Practical information

### Tel Aviv versus Jerusalem: which is better for Purim?

| | Tel Aviv | Jerusalem |
|---|---|---|
| Date (2027) | 22 March (Sunday) | 23 March (Monday, Shushan Purim) |
| Atmosphere | Massive street party, secular, festive | Family parade, more religious character |
| Main event | Dizengoff Square party (200,000+) | Adloyada parade + megillah readings |
| LGBTQ+ scene | Yes, Hilton Beach + main party | Smaller presence |
| Accommodation cost | Significantly elevated Purim week | Elevated but less than TLV |
| Best for | Nightlife, street-party, LGBTQ+ travellers | Cultural depth, families, religious experience |
| Can you do both? | Yes — visit TLV on Sunday, Jerusalem Monday | Yes — 45 minutes by train |

Many visitors do exactly this: celebrate Tel Aviv Purim on Sunday and catch the Jerusalem Adloyada on Monday.

### Getting there and around

**Train**: Tel Aviv to Jerusalem takes 30 minutes on the fast express train (Jerusalem-Navon station). Trains run on Sunday (March 22) and Monday (March 23) — Purim is not a public holiday affecting transport. Book the Sunday evening or late-afternoon return in advance as trains fill quickly on Purim night.

**Within Tel Aviv on Purim day**: expect road closures near Dizengoff Square from morning. Walk or take the Light Rail. Taxis and Gett operate but prices surge and drop-off points are blocks away from the closed streets.

**Accommodation**: Tel Aviv hotels during Purim week (especially the weekend of March 20–25, 2027) are significantly more expensive than surrounding weeks and fill early. Book three to four months ahead for the best selection. Jerusalem accommodation also rises but less dramatically.

### Costume sourcing in Israel

Costume shops appear across Tel Aviv in the weeks before Purim, stocking everything from simple accessories (capes, masks, hats) to elaborate character costumes. The Carmel Market and Nahalat Binyamin craft market have Purim accessories from early March. If you want a specific costume, plan it in advance or bring elements from home — popular costumes sell out in the days before Purim.
