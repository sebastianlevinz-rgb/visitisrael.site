Ashkelon is the kind of place that experienced Israel travellers talk about in hushed tones: a major coastal city of 140,000 people with an archaeological record that runs from Canaanite Bronze Age through Philistine Iron Age, Herodian, Byzantine, Crusader, Ottoman and into the modern era — yet almost completely absent from the standard tourist itinerary. This guide covers what's worth your time, how to get there and how to plan a realistic visit.

---

## Ashkelon National Park

The park is the reason most visitors come, and it justifies the trip entirely. It occupies the northwestern edge of the city where the ancient settlement sat on the coast — an oval-shaped tell surrounded by earthworks built in the Middle Bronze Age and still clearly visible as a ridge around the site.

**The Canaanite arched gateway** (circa 1800 BCE) is the centrepiece and arguably the most significant single object in the park. Two mudbrick towers flanking an arch of fired bricks have survived almost 4,000 years in near-perfect condition. When excavated, it was found to be the world's oldest intact arched gateway — predating the Greek and Roman arch tradition by more than a millennium. Standing in front of it has genuine impact.

**The dog cemetery** is less visually dramatic but archaeologically fascinating: more than 1,000 dogs were carefully buried here during the Persian period (5th century BCE), each laid on its side with tails tucked. The scholarly consensus from excavation reports (including a 2011 paper in the Journal of Archaeological Science) points to a healing cult; the dogs were not sacrificed but apparently tended and honoured in connection with medicinal or ritual practices at the site. Interpretation panels explain the context.

**Roman layers** are extensive: a forum, basilica, colonnaded streets and inscribed column bases indicate that Ashkelon was a prosperous coastal city in the Roman period. Fragments of a Roman marble statue have been recovered and are displayed at the site.

**Practical:** the park is managed by the INPA (Israel Nature and Parks Authority) and the Israel National Parks Pass is valid here. Check parks.org.il for current opening hours and entry fees. A walking tour of the main excavation area takes 1.5–2 hours. The site is coastal and exposed — bring water and a hat in warm months.

## The beaches

Ashkelon has roughly 3 km of public Mediterranean sand running along the city's western edge. Two beaches are particularly well-organised:

**Barnea Beach** is the main city beach — wide, sandy, with lifeguards in season (June–September), beach-volleyball courts and a row of café and food stalls along the promenade. Free entry. It is considerably less crowded than Tel Aviv beaches in July and August, making it an alternative for visitors who want a quieter beach day.

**Delilah's Lagoon** (Laguna Delilah) is a sheltered sea-pool formation to the south of Barnea, popular with families because the lagoon creates calmer water for children. The Philistine naming is a local tradition; Ashkelon is mentioned in the Samson and Delilah narrative in the Book of Judges, though the lagoon itself is a modern facility. Free entry.

The **coastal promenade** connecting the beaches offers pleasant walking at any time of year — views across the Mediterranean, occasional archaeological interpretation panels, and a café strip.

## Afridar — West Africa in Israel

One of Ashkelon's most distinctive neighbourhoods is Afridar, settled in the 1950s by immigrants who came predominantly from Nigeria and other West African countries, many of them part of Hebrew-faith communities (the Bayudaya and Igbo-Jewish diaspora communities). Their descendants form a recognisable community in Ashkelon today, and Afridar retains a character visibly distinct from the rest of the city — architecture, street patterns and community institutions that reflect a different founding history. It is not a tourist neighbourhood in the conventional sense (there are no guided tours or heritage centres), but walking through Afridar offers a glimpse of a part of Israeli demographic history that appears in no other city.

## City centre and Ottoman old city

Ashkelon's city centre is a functioning Israeli commercial district rather than a preserved historic zone. The Ottoman-era town (Majdal) was substantially rebuilt after 1948, but fragments survive: an Ottoman mosque (now the city museum), some limestone-built houses and the old market area. The municipal museum has exhibits on the city's history across its many layers. For most visitors, the national park and beaches will take priority over the city centre, but it is worth a brief walk if you have time.

## Yad Mordechai — nearby

Twenty kilometres south of Ashkelon on Route 4 is [Kibbutz Yad Mordechai](/israel-military-heritage), site of a pivotal 1948 battle in which a small number of defenders delayed an Egyptian armoured advance northward for six days. The kibbutz was founded by survivors of the Warsaw Ghetto Uprising and is named for its commander, Mordechai Anielewicz. A preserved battlefield, outdoor military display and museum make it a natural combination with an Ashkelon day trip. See the [Israel military heritage guide](/israel-military-heritage) for the full context.

## Getting to Ashkelon

**By train:** Direct services from Tel Aviv Savidor Central run roughly every hour to Ashkelon (55–65 minutes). From Jerusalem, change at Tel Aviv or Beer-Sheva. From the Ashkelon train station, the national park is 3 km north by taxi or bus.

**By car:** Route 4 (Kvish HaHof, coastal highway) from Tel Aviv takes about 45 minutes without traffic. Parking at the national park is signposted from Route 4. A car significantly improves access: it allows you to combine the park (north of the city), the beach strip (west), and Yad Mordechai (south) in a single day.

**By tour:** Organised day trips from Tel Aviv and Jerusalem cover Ashkelon National Park with a licensed guide and return transport. These are particularly good value for visitors who want archaeological context without the logistics of a self-drive.

**For all visits:** check [is-israel-safe](/is-israel-safe) and your home government's travel advisory for current conditions before travel, particularly given Ashkelon's proximity (20 km) to the Gaza border area.

## When to visit

The best months are **October through April**: temperatures of 15–24°C, comfortable for park walking and beach strolling, with the site calm and uncrowded. Summer (June–September) is hot and sunny — good for beach days, more tiring for the archaeological site. Winter brings brief rain showers (November–March) that occasionally make the coastal tell muddy; check forecasts before a specifically archaeology-focused visit.

Ashkelon is well suited as a [day trip from Tel Aviv](/day-trips-from-tel-aviv) — see that guide for the broader range of southern and coastal options from the city.
