**Keshet Cave** in the Western Galilee hills is one of Israel's most unusual geological formations — a natural limestone arch that visitors can walk through, entering through one cave portal and emerging on the other side beneath open sky. The name means "arch" in Hebrew, and the site delivers: a 48-metre limestone span stands above a 30-metre walkable tunnel, the sole surviving ceiling of a sea cave that formed when this coastline was underwater millions of years ago.

The cave sits within **Goren Park**, an Israel Nature and Parks Authority reserve approximately 25 minutes east of Nahariya. The same park trails connect directly to the **Nahal Kziv canyon** and the atmospheric ruins of **Montfort Fortress** — making Keshet Cave the natural starting point for one of the best combined nature and history half-days in northern Israel.

---

## The geology of Keshet Cave {#geology}

The story of Keshet Cave begins when the western Galilee coastline was submerged under the ancient Mediterranean. Sea water penetrated the limestone bedrock, dissolving it along cracks and weaknesses over millions of years. Where the dissolution was most intense, caves formed. As sea levels fell and the land rose, these caves were exposed to weathering and gravity.

Most of the Keshet cave system collapsed over time. What remains is the final section of intact ceiling — now a free-standing arch spanning 48 metres. This is what makes Keshet unusual: most natural arches are formed by erosion working from outside inward. Keshet is an **inverted cave** — erosion worked from inside outward, leaving the ceiling as the arch rather than a side wall.

The practical result for visitors is that you enter through a proper cave portal on the east side — 5 metres wide, 4 metres high, completely natural limestone — walk through a 30-metre tunnel section, and emerge into the open air on the far side, under the arch, with sky framed above by the curved limestone span. Looking back, you can see through the tunnel to the forest on the other side. The viewpoint from under the arch looks west over the western Galilee hills.

This walk-through configuration sets Keshet apart from other notable Israeli arches, where you stand on one side and admire the structure from a distance. Here you are inside the geological event.

## The zip-line and adventure activities {#adventure}

**Keshet Cave Adventure Park** operates a commercial concession within the nature reserve, offering:

### Zip-line

A 200-metre zip-line launches from a platform above the canyon and passes over and around the natural arch. The flight takes approximately 30–45 seconds and offers views of the limestone span, the forested canyon and the Western Galilee hills. The experience is not extreme — this is not a speed zip-line — but the combination of the arch below and the canyon setting makes it visually striking.

**Practical notes for the zip-line:**
- Advance booking is **essential**, particularly on weekends, Israeli school holidays, and throughout spring and summer. The zip-line frequently sells out on busy days. Book via [keshet-cave.co.il](https://www.keshet-cave.co.il) before your visit.
- Suitable for most visitors from approximately age 8 upward; the operator publishes weight and health restrictions — verify before booking.
- The zip-line ticket is a **separate fee** from Goren Park entrance; the Israel National Parks Pass does not cover it.

### Rappelling

Rappelling is available for those who prefer a vertical descent rather than a horizontal flight. The rappel face is at the canyon wall adjacent to the arch. Equipment and briefing are provided; suitable for beginners with no prior experience.

### The cave walk (no booking required)

The walk-through tunnel is accessible independently, without booking or additional fee beyond the standard Goren Park entry. Most visitors who are not using the zip-line spend 30–45 minutes at the cave: walking through the tunnel, standing under the arch, and taking in the viewpoint from the west-facing side.

## Nahal Kziv — the water hike below the cave {#nahal-kziv}

The **Nahal Kziv** is a spring-fed stream that runs through the same canyon system as Keshet Cave. Below Goren Park, the stream flows through forest pools, natural rock slides and swimming holes before eventually reaching the Mediterranean coast near Kibbutz Yas'ur — a total distance of approximately 12 kilometres one-way.

The Nahal Kziv is consistently ranked among Israel's finest water hikes when in season.

### Season and conditions

The stream flows from **October through May** after the winter rains fill the aquifers. It is typically at its best in **spring (March–May)**: water levels are good for swimming and wading, temperatures are comfortable, and the canyon forest — Tabor oak, eastern strawberry tree, and Mediterranean maquis — is at its most lush.

From June through September, most upstream sections run dry. The canyon walk remains pleasant but the swimming pools are largely absent.

### What to expect

Below Goren Park, a trail descends into the canyon and follows the stream. Depending on the section and season:
- Knee-to-thigh-deep wading through stream channels
- Natural rock pools suitable for swimming (depths vary; some are very shallow)
- Smooth limestone boulders requiring some scrambling
- Dense forest canopy providing shade

The most popular day-hike section is 2–3 kilometres out-and-back from the Goren Park car park, sufficient to experience the pools and canyon character.

**Flash flood safety (essential reading):** Never enter any wadi — including Nahal Kziv — during rainfall or after heavy rain upstream. Flash floods occur with little warning, even under clear skies locally, because rain may be falling kilometres away in the upper catchment. The INPA advises checking [parks.org.il](https://www.parks.org.il) or the trailmates.org.il application for current conditions before hiking.

**Gear:** Water shoes are essential for the stream sections. Bring a change of clothes, a waterproof bag for electronics, and at least 1.5 litres of water per person.

## Montfort Fortress — 1 kilometre downstream {#montfort}

From Goren Park, a marked trail descends 1 kilometre along the Nahal Kziv canyon to the ruins of **Montfort Fortress** — the most isolated and atmospherically impressive Crusader castle in Israel.

Montfort was built by the **Teutonic Knights** (the German military-religious order) in the early 13th century as their administrative centre in the Kingdom of Jerusalem. Unlike better-known Crusader sites such as Caesarea or Belvoir, Montfort can only be reached by hiking into the canyon — there is no road access — which means the ruins remain genuinely quiet and unspoiled.

### What remains at Montfort

- The **donjon** (main keep) on the western ridge, with walls up to two metres thick
- A **great hall** with pointed Gothic arches in pale limestone — the largest standing room reaches 8 metres in height
- Vaulted storage chambers and administrative halls
- Rock-cut cisterns and the remains of the gatehouse

The castle was besieged and captured by Mamluk Sultan Baybars in 1271. The Teutonic Knights surrendered after seven days, removed their archives and treasury to Acre, and never returned. The ruins have remained essentially unaltered since 1271.

### Combined itinerary: Keshet Cave + Montfort

Allow approximately **4 hours** for the full combination:

| Segment | Duration |
|---|---|
| Keshet Cave walk-through + viewpoint | 30–45 min |
| Hike to Montfort Fortress (descent, 1 km) | 40–50 min |
| Montfort ruins exploration | 60–90 min |
| Return hike to Goren Park | 40–50 min |

This is a **moderate half-day** — the trail is rocky and involves a 100-metre descent. Appropriate footwear (trail shoes or sturdy trainers) is essential. The trail is waymarked and clearly defined.

## Practical information {#practical}

### Getting there

**By car:** The most practical option. From Nahariya: Route 89 east toward Peqi'in, then turn onto Route 8833 toward Kibbutz Goren. Goren Park is approximately 20–25 minutes from the Nahariya centre. From Haifa: approximately 50 minutes north via Route 4 to Nahariya, then Route 89 east. From Akko: approximately 30 minutes via Route 89.

There is no practical direct bus service to Goren Park on timings useful for a day visitor. See the [Israel car rental guide](/car-rental-israel) for hiring options in Haifa or Nahariya.

### Entry and fees

- **Goren Park entry:** standard INPA fee, approximately ₪28–30 per adult; covered by the [Israel National Parks Pass](/israel-national-parks-pass). Parking is free.
- **Zip-line / rappelling:** separate commercial fee; book in advance at keshet-cave.co.il.
- **Montfort Fortress:** included in Goren Park; no additional fee.

Verify current INPA entry pricing at [parks.org.il](https://www.parks.org.il) before your visit.

### Opening hours

Goren Park is typically open approximately 08:00–17:00. Hours may vary seasonally and around Jewish holidays — confirm at parks.org.il before travelling. The zip-line concession operates on its own schedule; check keshet-cave.co.il.

### What to bring

- Trail shoes or sturdy trainers (essential for the Montfort descent)
- Water shoes (if planning to hike Nahal Kziv stream sections)
- Change of clothes and waterproof bag (for stream sections)
- At least 1.5 litres of water per person
- Sun protection — the cave itself is shaded but the trails above the canyon are exposed
- Head-torch (not essential, but useful in the darker sections of the cave tunnel)

### Best season

**Year-round for Keshet Cave.** March–May for the full combination of cave, zip-line, Nahal Kziv water and Montfort. Avoid midday hours in summer (June–August) — arrive by 09:00 and complete the canyon hike before 12:00.
