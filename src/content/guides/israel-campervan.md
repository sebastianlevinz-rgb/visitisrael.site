A campervan is one of the most liberating ways to travel Israel. Public transport covers the cities well, but the Galilee shoreline at dawn, the Golan Heights without a tour schedule, the Negev desert with your own kitchen and bed — none of those experiences are possible at the same pace without a vehicle you can sleep in. Israel is also compact enough that a well-planned campervan loop covers a remarkable amount of ground in seven days.

This guide covers renting a campervan in Israel, where you can legally sleep overnight, the best vanlife route, and the practical details that will actually affect your trip.

---

## Renting a campervan in Israel

Israel's campervan rental market is small but functional. Two main operators stand out.

**TerraVan** (terravan.co.il) is the established player, with a fleet of fully equipped 4-berth campervans available for pick-up in Haifa and central Tel Aviv. Their vehicles include:

- LPG cooker (2-burner)
- 12V compressor fridge
- Bed linens and pillow
- Satellite GPS unit
- Awning for shade (most vehicles)
- Hookup cable for campsite electric points

Nightly rates range from approximately ₪400–800 depending on season, van model and how far ahead you book. Summer (July–August) and school holidays (Sukkot, Passover, Hanukkah) are peak pricing periods; spring and autumn midweek bookings typically sit at the lower end.

**Campervans-israel.com** offers a second option with a slightly different fleet profile, including some smaller 2-berth vans suited to couples who want lower fuel consumption and easier parking.

**Booking in advance**: supply is tight. For summer and public holidays, book 2–3 months ahead. Spring (March–May) is the most popular season with international visitors — book at least 6–8 weeks out. Autumn is often easier to book at short notice.

## Legal camping spots: where to sleep overnight

### INPA-designated campsites

The Israel Nature and Parks Authority (INPA) operates a network of official campsites that welcome vehicles, including campervans. These are the legal and fully serviced option.

**Sea of Galilee (Kinneret) shore camps**
Multiple INPA campsites ring the Sea of Galilee — at Gofra, Susita/Hippos, and Kursi among others. They offer vehicle pitches, basic shower and toilet blocks, and access to the shore for swimming. Booking through the INPA website (parks.org.il) is required in high season. The Kinneret camps are a natural overnight for the northern leg of the campervan route.

**Ein Gedi / Nahal Arugot cluster (Dead Sea)**
The INPA campsite near Ein Gedi is the logical base for the Dead Sea and Masada section. You are within walking distance of the Ein Gedi Nature Reserve (spring pools and ibex), the Ein Gedi beach on the Dead Sea (free access for campers), and 25 km from Masada. An early morning Masada ascent via the Snake Path before the heat arrives is one of the highlights of the route.

**Mitzpe Ramon (Ramon Crater)**
The INPA Mitzpe Ramon campsite sits on the crater rim of Makhtesh Ramon — the world's largest erosion crater. This is the undisputed centrepiece of any Negev campervan trip. IDA (International Dark-Sky Association) certification means the skies above Mitzpe Ramon are among the darkest in the Middle East. A moonless clear night on the crater rim with your own kitchen and a reclining chair outside is as good as Israeli travel gets.

### Informal free-camp spots in the Negev

The Negev is large and population-sparse, and a handful of spots are used by the vanlife community as informal overnight stops. These are not officially sanctioned, but are tolerated in practice:

- **Ma'ale Akrabim (Scorpion Pass) overlook area** — a high desert pass on Route 227 with panoramic views over the Arava Valley. No facilities; strict leave-no-trace.
- **Uvda Valley** — a wide flat valley in the southern Negev between Mitzpe Ramon and Eilat, used for overnight desert camping by 4x4 and campervan travellers. Again: no facilities, pack out everything.

If you use informal spots, never light open fires (strictly prohibited), take all rubbish with you, and respect the desert ecology. The Israel Nature and Parks Authority does patrol and will issue fines for illegal camping inside park boundaries.

## The classic 7-day campervan route

This clockwise loop starts and ends in Tel Aviv and covers approximately 900 km. It works with a 5- to 10-day rental; 7 days is the comfortable minimum that doesn't feel rushed.

### Day 1 — Tel Aviv → Rosh Hanikra (approx. 120 km, 1.5 h)

Pick up the van in Tel Aviv and head north on the coastal Route 2. Stop at **Caesarea** (Roman harbour city, 45 km north) for 2–3 hours if you're not planning a full Caesarea day later. Continue north past Haifa to the **Rosh Hanikra sea grottoes** at the Lebanese border — white chalk cliffs and sea caves accessible via cable car. Overnight at a campsite near Nahariya or the Rosh Hanikra area.

### Day 2 — Rosh Hanikra → Akko → Sea of Galilee (approx. 80 km, 1.5 h driving)

Morning in **Akko (Acre)** — Crusader tunnels, the Arab market and the best hummus in northern Israel (Abu Christo restaurant near the port). Drive east along Route 85 into the Galilee, dropping to the Sea of Galilee at Tiberias. Camp at one of the Kinneret shore camps. Walk to the waterfront at golden hour.

### Day 3 — Sea of Galilee circuit (local driving)

A full day around the Kinneret. **Capernaum** (Jesus's base of ministry; free to park nearby). **Mount of Beatitudes** (short walk from the car park). **Sea of Galilee boat** (optional; the ancient boat in the museum at Kibbutz Ginosar is worth the 30 minutes). Evening back at the Kinneret camp — swimming from the shore at dusk is outstanding.

### Day 4 — Golan Heights (approx. 60 km loop from camp)

Drive north and east from the Sea of Galilee into the **Golan Heights**. The drive up from the Kinneret to the plateau is one of the most dramatic ascents in Israel. Key stops: **Nimrod Fortress** (Crusader/Ayyubid castle with sweeping views), **Gamla Nature Reserve** (ruins of a first-century Jewish city; griffon vulture colony), **Majdal Shams** (Druze town near the Syrian cenotaph). Return to camp at the Kinneret or push east to an informal pitch in the Golan.

### Day 5 — Jordan Valley → Dead Sea (approx. 130 km, 2 h)

Drive south on **Route 90**, the Jordan Valley highway — one of the longest and most hypnotic straight roads in Israel, running at up to 400m below sea level through the Jordan Rift Valley. Stop at **Beit She'an** for its extensive Roman theatre complex. Continue south to **Ein Gedi** and the INPA campsite. Afternoon swim in the Dead Sea — the Ein Gedi beach stretch is accessible on foot from camp. The water really does hold you up.

### Day 6 — Masada → Negev (approx. 100 km, 1.5 h)

Set the alarm for 4am. Drive the 25 km to **Masada** and start the Snake Path ascent before dawn — reaching the summit at sunrise is one of the great experiences in Israel. Return to camp by 10am before the Dead Sea heat becomes oppressive. Afternoon drive south through the Arava Valley to **Mitzpe Ramon**. Camp on the crater rim for the night.

### Day 7 — Mitzpe Ramon → Tel Aviv (approx. 230 km, 2.5 h)

Morning walk along the **Ramon Crater rim** before the heat arrives. The crater floor trail (2–3 h with a proper descent) reveals multicoloured rock formations, fossils and stunning scale. Drive north on Route 40 through the Negev heartland, connecting to Route 6 (toll motorway) north of Be'er Sheva for the quickest return to Tel Aviv. Van return by late afternoon.

## Practical details

### LPG and fuel

Fill LPG at major **Paz** and **Delek** petrol stations. The connector type for Israeli campervans is typically the ACME fitting — confirm with your rental operator before departure. Top up LPG whenever you see it in the Negev; petrol stations are 60–80 km apart in the south. Most vehicles run diesel — fill that at any petrol station on the network.

### Parking and driving in cities

- **Tel Aviv**: most campervans are too tall for underground car parks. Use the large open-air lots in the Tel Aviv port area (Namal) or north of the city. Driving a 4-berth van on Dizengoff Street or through Florentin is miserable — park on the edge and walk or use a bike.
- **Jerusalem**: Old City parking structures typically have 1.9–2.1m clearance. Most 4-berth campervans stand 2.2–2.6m tall — they will not fit. Park at Ammunition Hill (north Jerusalem) or Malha Mall (south) and take the light rail or a taxi.
- **Highway driving**: Israeli highway driving is fast and assertive. Keep to the right lane unless overtaking and use Waze — it is universally trusted by Israeli drivers and accounts for real-time police speed checks.

### Internet and navigation

An Israeli SIM card is essential — offline Waze is unreliable without data. Gett Telecom, Cellcom and Hot Mobile all sell tourist data SIMs at Ben Gurion Airport on arrival. TerraVan vehicles include a satellite GPS but Waze on your phone with a local SIM is more current.

### Shabbat logistics

Your campervan is your best Shabbat asset. Stock groceries (supermarkets: Rami Levy, Shufersal) by Thursday evening or Friday morning. Fill fuel and LPG by 2pm Friday. INPA campsites stay open. Roads on Shabbat are unusually quiet — Masada on Saturday morning has half the usual queues.

### Van height clearance

Check your van's exact height with the rental operator. Most 4-berth Israeli campervans stand 2.2–2.6m. Plan all overnight parking accordingly — open-air lots and designated campsite pitches will always clear this; most underground garages will not.

## Campervan vs rental car: which is right for you?

A campervan makes the most sense if:

- You are comfortable with self-catering (cooking breakfast at a Kinneret shore camp is genuinely excellent)
- You want the flexibility to stay somewhere unexpected without checking hotel availability
- You are travelling as a couple, family or group of three to four
- You plan at least 5–7 nights (shorter trips rarely justify the rental premium over a regular car + hotels)

A regular rental car is better if:

- You want to stay in hotels and restaurants every night
- Your route is urban-focused (Tel Aviv → Jerusalem → Tel Aviv does not need a campervan)
- You are travelling solo with a tight budget

For hybrid approaches — drive the Negev section in a campervan, use hotels in Jerusalem and Tel Aviv — the rental operators will sometimes accommodate split logistics, but confirm this in advance.
