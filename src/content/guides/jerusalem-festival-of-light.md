Each June, for roughly ten consecutive nights, Jerusalem's Old City becomes something it is not at any other time of year: a gallery of illuminated contemporary art set inside a 2,000-year-old stone labyrinth. The **Jerusalem Festival of Light** (also called the Jerusalem Light Festival or Festival of Lights) brings international artists to project onto the city walls, alleyways, churches, mosques, synagogues, and minarets of the Old City — transforming sites you may have visited in daylight into entirely different visual experiences after dark.

The centrepiece is a free walking route of roughly three kilometres that winds through all four quarters of the Old City. No ticket is required for the main route. The combination of world-class contemporary light art, the physical beauty of the Old City at night, and the scale of the event — typically 200,000–250,000 visitors over the festival's run — makes it one of the most significant annual events in the country.

---

## What Is the Festival of Light?

The Jerusalem Festival of Light is an international urban light-art festival held annually in the Old City of Jerusalem. Artists from France, Germany, the United States, the United Kingdom, Israel, and beyond use projection mapping, LED sculpture, and light installations to animate the Old City's stone surfaces, arches, and monuments.

It takes place each year for approximately 10 consecutive nights in early-to-mid June — exact dates vary and are confirmed by the organiser a few months in advance. Check [festivaloflight.co.il](https://www.festivaloflight.co.il/en) for the current year's dates and map before planning your trip.

The festival is organised by the Jerusalem Development Authority in cooperation with the Jerusalem Municipality. It has run annually (with rare pandemic interruptions) since 2009, and has grown from a small-scale event to one of the largest light festivals in the Middle East.

## The Walking Route

The free walking route is approximately 3 kilometres long and passes through all four quarters of the Old City: the **Jewish Quarter**, **Christian Quarter**, **Muslim Quarter**, and **Armenian Quarter**, as well as the Tower of David and Jaffa Gate area.

Key sections of the route:

- **Jaffa Gate and the City Walls** — The approach from Jaffa Gate is typically the most dramatic entry: the illuminated Ottoman walls, now animated by large-scale projection, serve as a visual statement of what the next two hours will contain.
- **Christian Quarter** — The Church of the Holy Sepulchre complex and the surrounding lanes receive some of the most technically sophisticated projections. Stone arches and ancient facades lend themselves naturally to mapping art.
- **Muslim Quarter** — The covered markets (the *souks* of the Old City) and the lanes leading toward the Damascus Gate receive installations that play with the interplay of light and the market's ornate vaulted ceilings. The area is busy but safe; festival crowds and local residents mix freely.
- **Jewish Quarter** — The Cardo (Roman-era colonnaded street, now excavated), Hurva Square, and the rooftop terraces overlooking the Western Wall Plaza offer some of the best elevated viewing points for distant installations.
- **Armenian Quarter** — Often the least crowded section; the installations here tend to be quieter and more contemplative in character.
- **Tower of David** — The Citadel's massive stone towers are the most visually striking single canvas in the Old City. A separate ticketed premium light show uses the Tower as its backdrop; the free route passes the exterior and the show is visible (if partially) from outside.

The official festival map, updated each year, marks installations and their locations; pick one up at the Jaffa Gate entrance or download it from the festival website before you arrive.

## Free Route vs. Premium Experiences

**The main walking route is free.** No ticket is needed to enter the Old City during the festival hours and walk the illuminated route at your own pace. This has been the case since the festival's inception and is one of the reasons the event draws such large crowds.

**Premium experiences** are ticketed separately and change from year to year. They typically include:

- **Tower of David Night Spectacular** — A large-scale immersive light-and-sound show projected onto the interior of the Citadel, viewed from the courtyard. This is widely considered the most technically impressive individual show in the festival; it runs over multiple screenings per evening and tickets sell out well in advance. Check towerofdavid.org.il for the current year's show dates, times, and pricing.
- **Guided premium routes** — Some years include a curated guided walk with a local expert, covering additional private courtyards and indoor installations not open to free-route visitors. Available through the festival website and platforms like GetYourGuide.
- **Indoor venue shows** — Specific synagogues, churches, or cultural centres occasionally host independent ticketed performances or exhibitions during the festival week.

**Honesty note:** Premium offerings change each year. Do not book based on what was available in a prior year — always verify the current year's programme at [festivaloflight.co.il](https://www.festivaloflight.co.il/en).

## Best Timing and Avoiding Crowds

The festival operates nightly from approximately **9pm to midnight** (closing times may vary slightly; confirm on the festival website for your specific visit).

**Weekday evenings** (Sunday through Thursday) are significantly less crowded than weekends. If you have flexibility, a Monday or Tuesday visit gives you a much more relaxed experience of the same installations that will be packed on Friday and Saturday nights.

**Within the evening itself:**
- The first hour (9pm–10pm) tends to be the busiest as most visitors arrive together.
- Arriving at **10pm or later** often results in shorter queues at popular installations and more space to stop and photograph.
- The final hour (11pm–midnight) is the quietest, though some visitors leave before the end if they have late-night transport to catch.

**First vs. last nights:** The opening night typically has an intense buzz and slightly higher crowds. The penultimate and final nights attract "last chance" visitors. The quietest period is usually nights 3–7.

## Photography Guide

The Festival of Light is one of the most photogenic events in Israel — and one of the most challenging environments to photograph well.

**Key tips:**

- **Use Night Mode** on a modern smartphone (iPhone 12 and above, Pixel 4 and above, Samsung S20 and above). The computational photography in night mode is optimised exactly for this kind of illuminated-stone scene.
- **Long exposure** on a DSLR or mirrorless camera (ISO 800–1600, f/4–f/8, 2–8 second exposure on a tripod or stable surface) produces striking results on the large wall projections.
- **The light on stone** is the distinctive visual element — the Old City's Jerusalem limestone absorbs and reflects coloured light in a way that looks quite different from contemporary surfaces. Frame tightly on the lit stone to capture this.
- **Arrive early at the Tower of David exterior** for photographs before crowds build — the illuminated Citadel from the alley approaching David's Tower gives one of the most recognisable images.
- **Avoid flash** — it overpowers the installation light and reduces the quality of results. Almost all modern phone cameras handle low-light scenes better without flash.
- A **small lightweight tripod or gorilla pod** is useful if you want sharper long-exposure shots, but note that the cobblestone surfaces are uneven and a stable setup takes practice.

## Practical Information

**Getting there:**
- **Light rail:** Jerusalem's Red Line runs to City Hall / Safra Square, a five-minute walk from Jaffa Gate. Running late into the evening during the festival, this is the most stress-free option from within Jerusalem.
- **By car:** The Mamilla underground car park (near Jaffa Gate, below the Mamilla Mall) is the most convenient. Arrive before 8pm — it fills quickly on festival evenings. Alternative parking near the municipality buildings on Safra Square.
- **From Tel Aviv:** Train to Jerusalem Yitzhak Navon station (90 minutes), then a short bus or taxi ride to the Old City. Late return trains run until approximately 1am — check rail.co.il/en for the exact last departure during the festival period.
- **Taxi / Gett:** Works well for getting to and from the Old City, but expect surge pricing and longer wait times during peak festival hours.

**What to wear and bring:**
- Jerusalem in June evenings typically ranges from 18–22°C — pleasant but can feel cool after midnight. A light layer is recommended for the post-11pm period.
- **Flat, sturdy shoes**: the Old City's cobblestones are uneven and some alleys are on significant slopes. Sandals work, but sole grip matters.
- A **refillable water bottle** — hydration stalls are present but queues form. The evenings are warm enough that dehydration is a real consideration, especially for children.
- **No glass bottles** — the Old City restricts glass containers during large events. Plastic or metal bottles are fine.
- **No wide pushchairs or prams** in the narrow alleys — the Old City's medieval-width streets cannot accommodate wide buggies during crowd events. A baby carrier is practical for infants.

**Safety and etiquette:**
- The Old City is heavily policed during the festival and is considered a safe event environment. Israeli security presence is significant but low-profile.
- The Muslim Quarter remains a functioning neighbourhood during the festival; residents live here. Walk respectfully and avoid loud conversations directly in front of residential doorways.
- The Church of the Holy Sepulchre and the Western Wall remain religious sites even during the festival — dress modestly (covered shoulders and knees) in these areas.

## Combining the Festival with Daytime Jerusalem

The most satisfying approach is to **spend the day in Jerusalem** exploring the Old City, then stay for the festival in the evening. The Old City in daylight and the Old City after dark are such different experiences that seeing both in the same visit gives a remarkable double perspective.

Suggested same-day combination:
- **Morning:** Western Wall and Western Wall Tunnels (book the tunnel tour in advance at thekotel.org)
- **Midday:** [Yad Vashem](/yad-vashem-visitor-guide) or the [Israel Museum](/jerusalem-museums) (in West Jerusalem, about 20 minutes from the Old City)
- **Late afternoon:** Lunch or dinner in the [Mahane Yehuda market area](/jerusalem-food-guide) — the market transforms into a bar district after dark, and eating there before heading to the Old City for the festival is a classic Jerusalem evening sequence
- **Evening (9pm onwards):** Festival of Light walking route

If you have two nights in Jerusalem, [Jerusalem's museums](/jerusalem-museums) — including the Israel Museum and Bible Lands Museum on the Givat Ram campus — deserve a full separate day.

## Booking Accommodation

Jerusalem hotel rates rise significantly during the Festival of Light period, with increases of 40–60% on peak evenings (first few and last nights, and any weekend nights) compared with non-festival weeks in June. The central Jerusalem options — near Jaffa Gate, in the German Colony, and on the Mamilla/City Center boundary — sell out fastest.

**Book as early as possible** — ideally three to four months before the festival, once dates are confirmed. If you book early and dates shift (uncommon but not impossible), most hotels allow free cancellation up to 48–72 hours before arrival.

The hotel area between Jaffa Gate and King David Street is optimal for walking to the festival and back without needing transport. The German Colony (Emek Refaim area) is a 20-minute walk or short taxi.

## Planning a Day Trip from Tel Aviv

Many visitors arrive from Tel Aviv for the festival and return the same night. This works logistically but requires planning:

- **Train:** The frequent train service from Tel Aviv HaHagana or Tel Aviv HaMerkaz (Savidor Center) to Jerusalem Yitzhak Navon takes 80–90 minutes. Late-night trains return until approximately 1am — check rail.co.il for the exact schedule during the festival week.
- **Arrival window:** Aim to arrive in Jerusalem by 6pm, giving time for dinner before the festival starts at 9pm.
- **The constraint:** If you miss the last train, you need a private taxi or car — which can cost ₪250–400 for the Jerusalem–Tel Aviv run at midnight. Building in a buffer or booking one night in Jerusalem removes this entirely.
