The Czech Republic has one of the oldest and most distinctive connections to Israel of any European country — rooted in the history of Bohemian and Moravian Jewish communities stretching back centuries, and cemented by a strong post-war Czech Israeli community that keeps cultural ties alive today. El Al operates direct flights from Prague, and Czech travellers typically experience minimal jet lag given the small time difference. This guide covers the practical information specific to Czech visitors: the MZV advisory, the ETA-IL requirement, flights from Prague, the public health insurance gap, power adapters, banking and Czech Embassy contacts.

For the broader trip-planning picture, see the [complete first-time visitor guide](/first-time-in-israel) and the [safety guide](/is-israel-safe).

## MZV travel advisory

The Czech Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Ministerstvo zahraničních věcí, **MZV**) publishes live, regularly updated travel safety information for Israel at **mzv.gov.cz**. The advisory is tiered by region and updated in real time as the security situation develops.

Key points from the current framing:

- **Gaza Strip** — Travel not advised; the border is closed to civilians.
- **Parts of the West Bank** — Caution advised; specific roads and areas carry restrictions.
- **Northern border area** — Elevated caution near the Lebanese border; Haifa, Tiberias and the Sea of Galilee are outside the highest-caution zone.
- **Main tourist areas** — Jerusalem, Tel Aviv, the Dead Sea, Galilee (south of the border zone), the Negev and Eilat are the principal destinations visited by Czech tourists.

**Always read the live MZV advisory** before booking, before departing, and periodically during your trip. Do not rely on third-party summaries — wording and area classifications can change quickly in response to events.

**Register with DROZD.** The Czech Foreign Ministry's DROZD system (drozd.mzv.gov.cz) lets you register your stay abroad so the Czech Embassy in Tel Aviv can contact you in an emergency. Registration is free and recommended for any extended trip.

## Flying from the Czech Republic

### Direct and connecting routes

El Al and several other carriers connect Prague (PRG) with Ben Gurion Airport (TLV):

| Route | Carriers |
|---|---|
| Prague PRG direct | El Al (several times weekly), Wizz Air (seasonal) |
| Via Vienna VIE | Austrian Airlines + connecting flight |
| Via Amsterdam AMS | KLM + connecting flight |
| Via Frankfurt FRA | Lufthansa + connecting flight |
| Via Warsaw WAW | LOT Polish Airlines + connecting flight |

**El Al** operates the most convenient option — a direct Prague–Tel Aviv service (approximately **4 hours 30 minutes**). El Al is Israel's national carrier and flies year-round between PRG and TLV; flight frequency varies by season. **Wizz Air** offers seasonal low-cost routes from Prague, typically in the summer months.

For connecting itineraries, Vienna (1 hour from Prague by train or short flight) is the most convenient European hub, with Austrian Airlines offering daily connections to Tel Aviv. Warsaw and Frankfurt also provide reliable one-stop options.

**When to book.** Return fares from Prague to Tel Aviv typically range from **3,000–7,000 CZK** for economy, depending on season and how far in advance you book. The most expensive periods are the Jewish High Holidays (September–October), summer peak (July–August), Passover (March–April) and Christmas–New Year. Booking 10–20 weeks in advance usually produces competitive fares. See the [cheap flights to Israel guide](/cheap-flights-to-israel) for fare tools and a full airline breakdown.

## Entry requirements: ETA-IL

Since January 2025, Czech citizens must hold an **Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA-IL)** before boarding any flight to Israel. The Czech Republic is on the Israeli visa-exempt list — the ETA-IL replaces a full visa but is a mandatory pre-travel step.

Key facts:

- **Cost:** ₪25 per application (approximately 175 CZK or €7 at recent exchange rates; priced in Israeli shekels)
- **Validity:** Up to 2 years, or until your passport expires — whichever is earlier; valid for multiple entries
- **Processing time:** Usually 72 hours; can take several days during peak season — apply at least one week before departure
- **Apply at:** iaa.gov.il (the Israel Airports Authority official portal; third-party sites charging higher fees are not official)
- **Length of stay:** Up to 90 days per visit as a tourist

The ETA-IL is linked electronically to your passport — you do not receive a physical stamp or document to carry. Keep a record of your application reference number. See the [full ETA-IL and visa guide](/visa-information) for the application steps in detail.

**Israeli entry stamp policy.** Ben Gurion Airport does not issue passport stamps to tourists — instead, you receive a small paper entry slip. Keep this slip; hotels and some internal checkpoints may ask to see it.

## Time zone

Israel Standard Time (IST) is **UTC+3 in summer** (approximately late March to late October) and **UTC+2 in winter** (late October to late March).

| Czech time | Summer (CEST, UTC+2) | Winter (CET, UTC+1) |
|---|---|---|
| Difference to Israel | Israel is **+1 hour** ahead | Israel is **+1 hour** ahead |

Czech travellers experience minimal jet lag — the time shift is consistently just one hour in both summer and winter. A direct flight from Prague departing at 14:00 CEST arrives in Tel Aviv around 19:30–20:00 IDT.

## Health insurance — the public insurance gap

**Standard Czech public health insurance (veřejné zdravotní pojištění) does not cover medical treatment outside the European Union.** Israel is not an EU member state, so your Czech health card provides no coverage there. This differs from travel within the EU/EEA, where the European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) covers emergency treatment.

Medical care in Israel for tourists without insurance can be expensive — a short hospital admission, emergency surgery, or medical evacuation home can run into tens of thousands of CZK.

**What to do:**

- **Buy a cestovní pojištění** (travel health insurance) before you travel. Czech insurers including Allianz, AXA, Generali, Kooperativa and UNIQA all offer single-trip and annual international travel policies. International providers such as SafetyWing are also widely used by Czech travellers.
- **Check existing supplementary cover.** Some Czech credit card policies include limited international medical cover — check the specific terms and coverage limits carefully.
- **Conflict-zone exclusions.** Some travel insurance policies contain conflict-zone exclusions. Read the policy wording before buying, and confirm that Israel is covered under your specific policy given current conditions. See the [Israel travel insurance guide](/israel-travel-insurance) for what to look for.

## Banking and cards

Israel's currency is the **New Israeli Shekel (₪, ILS)**. Card payments are broadly accepted in Tel Aviv, Jerusalem, Eilat and most tourist-facing businesses.

**Czech CZK vs Israeli ₪.** The Czech Republic uses the Czech Koruna (CZK), not the euro. Be aware when planning your budget that Israeli ATMs display amounts in shekels; exchange comparisons will involve a CZK-to-ILS conversion (currently approximately 1 CZK ≈ 0.14–0.15 ILS; check a live rate before travel). Cash in euros or USD is more easily exchanged at Israeli exchange bureaux than CZK.

**Recommended cards for Czech travellers:**

- **Revolut** — no FX fees up to a monthly limit; real interbank exchange rate; app-based spending control; widely used in Israel
- **Wise (formerly TransferWise)** — competitive mid-market exchange rates; Mastercard debit card accepted at Israeli ATMs
- **Czech Visa or Mastercard debit/credit cards** (Komerční banka, Česká spořitelna, ČSOB, Raiffeisenbank) — broadly accepted at Israeli ATMs and terminals; check your card's international transaction fee (poplatek za zahraniční transakce) before travelling, as it varies significantly by bank

**ATMs.** Bank Leumi, Bank Hapoalim and Israel Post Bank ATMs are widely distributed in cities and tourist areas. When prompted, always choose to be charged **in shekels** (not CZK or EUR) to avoid the ATM operator's own exchange markup.

**Cash.** Carry some cash (₪200–400) for smaller vendors, markets, shared taxis and rural areas. Jerusalem's Old City bazaars and Tel Aviv's Carmel Market are cash-preferred. Airport exchange desks carry poor rates — use a city-centre exchange bureau or ATM on arrival. See the [Israel money guide](/israel-money-guide) for current rate benchmarks and cash-vs-card guidance.

## Power adapter and voltage

Israel uses **Type H sockets** — three flat pins in a Y-shape (230V, 50Hz), unique to Israel. Czech sockets are **Type E** (230V, 50Hz, two round pins with a socket-hole for an earth pin).

**Good news for Czech travellers:** the voltage (230V) and frequency (50Hz) are identical in the Czech Republic and Israel. You do **not** need a voltage converter — only a physical plug adapter to change the pin shape.

A **Type E–to–Type H adapter** (or a universal travel adapter with Type H support) is all you need. These are available online or in electronics shops (Alza.cz, Datart) — buy before you leave, as adapters at Ben Gurion Airport arrivals are significantly more expensive.

Most modern Czech electronics — smartphones, laptops, tablets, shavers and camera chargers — are marked "100–240V" and work worldwide with just the plug adapter. Check the label on any older appliances or hairdryers before packing.

## Mobile phones and connectivity

Israeli mobile networks operate on GSM/LTE/5G — Czech phones work without any hardware modification.

**EU roaming rules do not apply in Israel.** Unlike travel within the EU/EEA, Israeli operators are outside the EU roaming framework. Your Czech T-Mobile, O2 or Vodafone contract will charge international rates:

- Most Czech providers offer an international day-pass option — check your specific tariff at your provider's website before travelling.
- For stays longer than a few days, a **local Israeli SIM** is the most cost-effective option: Golan Telecom, Hot Mobile and Pelephone sell tourist SIM cards at Ben Gurion Airport arrivals (open 24 hours) and in phone shops across Tel Aviv and Jerusalem. A 30-day unlimited-data SIM typically costs ₪50–90 (approximately 330–600 CZK) and includes calls within Israel.
- An **eSIM** from Airalo or Nomad can be activated before departure — convenient if your phone supports eSIM (most recent iPhones and Android flagships do).

Wi-Fi is widely available in hotels, restaurants and cafés across Israel's tourist areas. See the [Israel eSIM and SIM guide](/israel-esim) for a full provider comparison.

## Czech–Israeli historical connections

Few European countries have as layered a connection to Israel as the Czech Republic. Bohemia and Moravia were home to one of Central Europe's oldest and most distinguished Jewish communities — Prague's **Jewish Quarter (Josefov)** is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the best-preserved medieval Jewish urban districts in the world, with six historic synagogues still standing.

The 20th century brought profound tragedy: the Nazi occupation resulted in the near-total destruction of Czech Jewry, with **Theresienstadt (Terezín)**, 60 km north of Prague, serving as a concentration and transit camp from which thousands of Czech Jews were deported to Auschwitz. Yad Vashem's Czech section and the Pinkas Synagogue in Prague — whose walls bear the names of 77,297 Czech Jewish Holocaust victims — are among the most moving memorial sites in Europe.

After 1948, significant numbers of Czech Jews emigrated to Israel, forming a close-knit community that has maintained cultural ties across generations. Czechoslovakia was among the first countries to recognise the State of Israel and was a key supplier of arms during Israel's 1948 War of Independence — a fact of enduring historical significance. President Tomáš Masaryk was also a known supporter of Zionist aspirations well before statehood.

Today the Czech Republic is considered one of Israel's strongest diplomatic allies within the European Union. Many Czech visitors choose to include a visit to **Yad Vashem** — the World Holocaust Remembrance Center in Jerusalem — as part of their Israel trip. The [Yad Vashem visitor guide](/yad-vashem-visitor-guide) has full details on opening times, tours and the weight of the experience.

## Czech Embassy and emergency contacts

**Czech Embassy Tel Aviv**
Current address and consular hours: **mzv.gov.cz/telaviv** (official Czech Embassy Israel page)
Emergency consular assistance for Czech citizens abroad: call the MZV duty line or check the Embassy website for the out-of-hours emergency number.

**Emergency numbers in Israel:**
- Police: **100**
- Ambulance (Magen David Adom): **101**
- Fire: **102**
- National emergency operator: **112** (works from all mobile phones including Czech SIMs)

Register your stay on **DROZD** (drozd.mzv.gov.cz) before departure so the Embassy can contact you in an emergency.
