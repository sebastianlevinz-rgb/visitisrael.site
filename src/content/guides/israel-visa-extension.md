Israel grants most Western visitors a 90-day tourist authorization on arrival — usually enough for a comprehensive trip, but sometimes not quite enough. Whether you've fallen in love with the country, you're studying Hebrew, visiting family for longer than expected, or you're recovering from an illness, the Population and Immigration Authority (Misrad HaPnim) can grant a **tourist visa extension** that lets you stay legally for a further period.

This guide explains the process, the documents you'll need, timing, and the alternatives — including the "border run" option and what the risks actually are.

## Who can apply for an extension

Any tourist who entered Israel legally and holds a valid ETA-IL authorization (or a B/2 visitor visa) can **apply** for an extension. Approval is at the discretion of the officer, but applications are routinely granted for visitors who can demonstrate:

- A legitimate reason for the extended stay (family visit, enrolled Hebrew ulpan, medical treatment, ongoing pilgrimage or religious study)
- Sufficient funds to support themselves without working illegally
- A clear intention to leave Israel when the extended period ends (onward booking, home ties)
- A valid passport with at least six months' validity remaining

There is no strict list of acceptable reasons — the officer assesses each application individually. Cultural and educational purposes (language study, volunteer archaeology placements with recognized programs, religious community participation) are generally looked on favorably.

## The extension process, step by step

### 1. Book your appointment

The **Population and Immigration Authority (Misrad HaPnim)** handles all tourist extension applications. There are major offices in **Jerusalem** (Kanfei Nesharim Street), **Tel Aviv** (Shalom Tower), **Haifa** (Haneviim Street) and **Beer Sheva**. Walk-in queues at busy offices can stretch to several hours; **book an appointment in advance** via the official portal at piba.gov.il.

### 2. Apply before your current authorization expires

Time this carefully. Your application must be submitted **before** your current authorized stay ends — you can apply up to 30 days before the expiry. Don't wait until the last day: if the office is busy or there are document issues, you may run out of time. Aim to apply at least 2–3 weeks before your authorization expires.

### 3. Gather your documents

Bring everything listed below to your appointment:

| Document | Notes |
|---|---|
| Passport | Valid ≥ 6 months beyond planned departure |
| ETA-IL approval | Email or printout from before your flight |
| Paper entry slip | Issued at Ben Gurion on arrival — keep this safe |
| Bank statements | Last 2–3 months showing sufficient funds |
| Onward flight booking | Shows intention to leave Israel |
| Supporting letter / enrollment | For study, medical, or family-visit cases |

The officer may not ask for all of these, but it's better to have them and not need them.

### 4. Pay the fee and wait

The current fee is in the range of **₪200–₪300** (approximately $55–$80). Bring a credit card; some offices also accept cash. In most straightforward cases the decision is made at the appointment itself. If the officer needs to refer your case for further review, you'll be told a timeline.

### 5. Extension granted

If approved, you receive a new authorized stay stamped into your passport or noted on an updated entry document, typically granting an additional **90 days**. Your total stay can reach approximately six months in practice.

## How long before my authorization expires should I apply?

Apply at least **two to three weeks before** your current period ends. The practical reasons:

- Office appointments can book out by days or even a week in busy periods
- If there's a document issue you need time to fix it and re-apply
- Applying late (within a few days of expiry) leaves no margin for error
- The application itself must be submitted while you still have legal status

If you are genuinely running out of time and are at risk of overstaying, go to the nearest Population Authority office immediately, even without an appointment. Explain the situation. Officers prefer applicants who proactively seek compliance over those who simply overstay.

## The "border run" — what you should know

Some long-term visitors to Israel exit to Jordan (via the **Allenby/King Hussein Bridge** crossing near Jericho or the **Yitzhak Rabin crossing** near Eilat) and re-enter to reset their 90-day clock. Each new entry gets a new authorized stay. This is sometimes called a border run.

**What Israeli law actually says**: there is no explicit legal prohibition on re-entry after a short absence. However:

- Israeli immigration officers have broad **discretion** to deny re-entry if they believe a visitor is manipulating the system to circumvent the authorized stay rules
- Travelers making multiple repeated short entries and exits have been refused re-entry at the airport or border
- A refusal to re-entry does not come with an automatic right of appeal or legal remedy at the border
- The strategy offers no benefit over a legitimate extension in terms of total time — and carries meaningful risk

The formal extension process through the Population Authority is the lower-risk, compliant approach. If you want certainty that you can remain in the country legally, use the extension process rather than a border run.

## Longer-term options in Israel

If 90 days plus one extension (roughly six months) still isn't enough, there are legitimate pathways to a longer legal stay:

### Ulpan (Hebrew language study)

Intensive Hebrew language programs — **ulpanim** — are Israel's most accessible pathway to a longer authorized stay for foreign nationals with Jewish connections. The most well-known program is **Ulpan Etzion** in Jerusalem. Recognized ulpan enrollment can support a student-category extension at the Population Authority. Programs run for 5 months full-time or longer part-time.

### MASA Israel programs

**MASA Israel** (masaisrael.org) is a partnership of the Jewish Agency and the Israeli government offering programs from 5 months to a year for Jewish young adults from the diaspora. Programs include internships, volunteer placements, university study, and gap-year experiences. MASA participants receive a dedicated visa status.

### Aliyah (immigration to Israel)

If you are Jewish or have a Jewish grandparent, you may be eligible to make **Aliyah** — formal immigration to Israel under the Law of Return. The process is handled by the Jewish Agency (jewishagency.org) and involves a different visa category entirely. Aliyah is a significant life decision, but if you are researching it, the Population Authority immigration officers can refer you to the appropriate desk.

### Long-term volunteer programs

Recognized volunteer placements with established organizations (WIZO, Meir Panim, American Jewish World Service grantees, archaeological dig programs affiliated with universities) can in some cases support an extended authorized stay. Check with the specific organization and the Population Authority about the relevant visa category.

## If you're visiting after Birthright

If you came to Israel on a Birthright Israel (Taglit) trip and want to stay on independently, you entered as a tourist and your 90-day clock started on your arrival date. The extension process above applies exactly the same way.
