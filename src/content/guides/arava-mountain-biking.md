The southern Arava Valley is Israel's least-visited desert — a long, flat-bottomed rift between the Negev mountains to the west and the Jordanian highlands to the east, stretching from the Dead Sea to the Red Sea. In late 2025 and early 2026, the Eilot Regional Economic Development Corporation invested ₪20 million to turn this empty corridor into one of the most ambitious mountain biking trail networks in the Middle East: **the Yahel–Timna singletrack**.

For riders who know how to handle technical desert terrain, this is genuinely new ground. The trail connects Kibbutz Yahel, 55 km north of Eilat, to Timna Park at the southern end — a 100+ km arc through volcanic basalt ridges, limestone wadi systems and the ochre sandstone formations of the Timna Valley. No other travel publisher in English has a complete guide to this trail. This is it.

---

## What the Yahel–Timna trail is

The singletrack is not a loop. It runs broadly south-southwest from Kibbutz Yahel to Timna Park, threading through terrain that most visitors never see from the highway. The Arava looks flat from Highway 90; from inside it, at trail level, it is anything but.

**The volcanic section** (northern half, around the Yahel area) crosses fields of black basalt — cooling lava from ancient volcanic activity that left a landscape of jagged, angular rock. The singletrack here is narrow and technical: off-camber crossings over basalt slabs, short rocky drops into dry riverbeds, and boulder-strewn climbs onto low ridges with long views east into Jordan. Riders unfamiliar with rocky desert riding will find this section demanding. Take it slowly until you know the surface.

**The limestone and wadi section** (middle segment) transitions to pale limestone and chalky sandstone as the trail descends into and out of several significant wadi (dry riverbed) systems. Wadi crossings are smooth and fast when dry — wide gravel beds with good flow. After rain anywhere in the watershed (including the Eilat Mountains to the west, which are not always visible from the wadi floor), these crossings flood rapidly. **Never enter a wadi crossing during or after rain.** Check the Israel Meteorological Service (ims.gov.il) forecast before leaving.

**The Timna approach** (southern section) enters the characteristic colour palette of Timna Park — sandstone formations in ochre, sienna and white, shaped by millennia of erosion into arches, pillars and canyon walls. The trail descends to the park perimeter with views of Solomon's Pillars ahead.

## Getting to the start: Kibbutz Yahel

Kibbutz Yahel sits directly on Highway 90, 55 km north of central Eilat. The kibbutz was founded in the 1970s as part of the regional settlement programme; it remains a working agricultural community growing date palms and melons in the desert with drip irrigation.

**There is no public bus to Kibbutz Yahel.** The highway sees relatively little scheduled transport, and the kibbutz has no transit hub. Practical options:

- **Rent a car from Eilat** (city centre or Ramon Airport ETM, 18 km north) and drive to Yahel in 40–50 minutes on Highway 90. The road is well-maintained dual carriageway with minimal traffic outside Jewish holidays and weekends.
- **Self-shuttle with two cars**: park one car at Timna Park, drive the other to Kibbutz Yahel, and ride point-to-point south. This is the cleanest option for groups.
- **Arrange a private shuttle**: some Eilat outdoor operators and guesthouse owners in Eilat and Kibbutz Yotvata (18 km south of Yahel) offer shuttle services for trail riders. Ask when booking accommodation.

## Bike hire

There is no bike hire at Kibbutz Yahel. Mountain bike rental is available in **Eilat city**, from shops near the North Beach hotel strip and the city centre. Stock varies by season; hardtail MTBs are more commonly available than full-suspension. Book ahead if you are visiting in peak season (March–April and October–November weekends).

**Bike type**: the Yahel–Timna singletrack is suitable for hardtail MTBs on most sections; full-suspension adds comfort on the basalt rock sections and rough wadi terrain. Tyre pressure should be lower than you would run on smoother trails — wide rubber (2.2" minimum) handles the sharp volcanic rock better than narrow tyres. Road bikes and gravel bikes are not suitable for the singletrack. A gravel bike is viable on the wider gravel sections of the broader Eilot network (the 200+ km of gravel loops) but not on the technical volcanic singletrack.

## Trail logistics and one-way ride planning

The Yahel–Timna trail is designed primarily as a **one-way point-to-point** from north to south. The southern terminus is Timna Park; the northern start is Kibbutz Yahel.

**Water**: bring a minimum of 3 litres per rider. There are no reliable water sources on the trail between Yahel and Timna Park. Desert dehydration is fast and serious even in winter when temperatures are comfortable; in spring (April–May), water needs increase sharply. Do not rely on finding water.

**Emergency access**: most of the trail is within mobile phone coverage (Hot Mobile and Cellcom have reasonable signal on the Arava plain; ravines may drop to one bar). The trail passes through no populated areas between Kibbutz Yahel and Timna Park — if you have a mechanical issue that cannot be fixed with a trail kit (tube, tyre lever, pump, multi-tool, chain link), you are relying on calling for vehicle pickup. Tell someone your itinerary before riding.

**Route navigation**: download the Eilot Council trail app or a GPX file before departing — paper maps are not adequate for singletrack navigation through the wadi system. The trail is not uniformly signposted.

## Combining with Timna Park

The southern end of the singletrack connects to the Timna Park perimeter. After the descent into the Timna Valley, the trail reaches the park's bike-accessible entry point. A post-ride visit to the park gives you:

- **Solomon's Pillars**: 50-metre sandstone columns, the most photographed feature in the park
- **Mushroom Rock**: an isolated sandstone dome balanced on an eroded pedestal column
- **Copper Mine Trail**: 3,200-year-old mining shafts and tunnels worked by Egyptians under Ramesses II
- **Timna Lake and Tabernacle replica**: a desert lake in the park's eastern section with a full-scale reconstruction of the Exodus-era Mishkan (Tabernacle)

Timna Park is independently managed (not an INPA site) and the Israel National Parks Pass does not apply. Entry is approximately ₪35–45 per adult; current rates and opening hours at [parktimna.co.il](https://www.parktimna.co.il). The park is spread out — a car is needed to cover all the main sites in a half-day. If you have ridden point-to-point from Yahel, arrange your vehicle pickup at the Timna entrance.

For a full guide to every site in the park, including the stargazing evenings and the best photography positions at dawn, see the [Timna Park visitor guide](/timna-park-guide).

## The broader Eilot trail network

The Yahel–Timna singletrack is the flagship route of a **350+ km regional mountain bike network** that the Eilot Regional Council has been building since the early 2020s. The full network includes:

- The Israel Bike Trail (IBT) southern section, which passes through the Eilat Mountains
- The Yahel–Timna singletrack (the new addition)
- 200+ km of gravel and asphalt loops connecting Eilat, Kibbutz Yotvata, and the surrounding wadi systems

Riders who want to explore the complete network over multiple days can use Eilat as a base, with different sections accessible by car within 30–60 minutes. The Eilot Council tourist app provides GPX files, route ratings and condition updates.

For the Eilat Mountains routes — including the terrain near the [Red Canyon](/red-canyon-eilat) — those are a separate network accessed directly from Eilat city and are suitable for shorter half-day rides closer to the city.

## Getting there from Eilat

**By rental car**: pick up from Eilat city centre or Ramon Airport (ETM). Drive north on Highway 90 — Kibbutz Yahel is 55 km, approximately 45 minutes. For a one-way ride to Timna Park, a second car at the Timna exit simplifies the return; alternatively arrange a shuttle from Eilat. **From Tel Aviv or Jerusalem**: fly to Ramon Airport (ETM) and collect a rental car there. Ramon Airport is 18 km north of Eilat centre — the drive to Kibbutz Yahel from the airport is approximately 35 minutes on Highway 90.
