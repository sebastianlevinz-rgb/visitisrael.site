Tel Aviv is best known for beaches, restaurants, and nightlife — but its museum scene is genuinely world-class. The city holds one of the Middle East's finest fine-art collections, the world's largest Jewish diaspora museum, a multi-pavilion archaeology complex, and the building where the State of Israel was declared in 1948. This guide covers the six institutions worth making time for, with honest advice on what each delivers, how to get there, and what to expect.

---

## Tel Aviv Museum of Art

**Where:** 27 Shaul Hamelech Boulevard, Ramat Aviv (also signed as King Saul Boulevard), adjacent to the Hayarkon Park entrance.

The Tel Aviv Museum of Art is Israel's flagship fine-art institution — a major collection spanning Impressionism, Modernism, and contemporary Israeli art across five interconnected pavilions. The 2011 addition by Preston Scott Cohen (the Herta and Paul Amir Building) resolved the challenge of grafting a large new wing onto the original 1932 building; the atrium's spiralling hyperboloid is worth a look in its own right.

**What's inside:** Old Masters (a modest but representative selection), Impressionist and Post-Impressionist works, a strong Modern section (Chagall, Modigliani, Israeli greats like Reuven Rubin and Nahum Gutman), and rotating contemporary shows that draw international artists. The design collection on the upper floors is often overlooked; it rewards visitors who continue past the painting galleries.

**Practical notes:**
- Admission prices change — always check tamuseum.org.il for current rates before visiting
- Closed Yom Kippur; reduced hours on many Jewish holidays — verify before going
- Thursday evenings typically run extended hours (check the website)
- The café on the ground floor is reliable for a mid-visit break
- Budget at least two to three hours; serious art visitors often spend a full morning

## ANU — Museum of the Jewish People

**Where:** Gate 2, Tel Aviv University campus, Ramat Aviv, north Tel Aviv.

ANU (formerly Beit Hatfutsot / Beth Hatefutsoth) reopened in 2021 after a major renovation and expansion, and it is now a genuinely impressive institution — one of the few Israeli museums that merits the superlatives it gets. The name means "we" in Hebrew: the museum's premise is to tell the story of every Jewish community in the world, across 4,000 years.

**What's inside:** Permanent galleries cover Jewish communities from Ethiopia to India to Ashkenaz and Sephardic communities; the Hall of Names contains a database of over five million Jewish individuals from the Holocaust; a genealogy research centre allows visitors to trace Jewish family lineages worldwide; temporary exhibitions bring contemporary Jewish art and culture. The building has been dramatically upgraded — the entrance hall and permanent collection spaces are museum-quality.

**The genealogy database** is a unique resource that sets ANU apart from any comparable institution. Visitors can search for family names and origins at database terminals; the staff can assist with more complex searches. Some research services require advance appointment — check anu.org.il before visiting if genealogy research is your primary purpose.

**Getting there:** Red Line light rail to Tel Aviv University station, or bus routes to Ramat Aviv. Parking on campus. Budget three to four hours minimum; the building rewards unhurried exploration.

For the full ANU experience — including the Codex Sassoon display and genealogy database — see our [dedicated ANU Museum visitor guide](/anu-museum-guide).

## Eretz Israel Museum

**Where:** 2 Haim Levanon Street, Ramat Aviv, north Tel Aviv (walking distance from ANU).

The Eretz Israel Museum (Land of Israel Museum) is a multi-pavilion complex rather than a single building — a collection of specialised museums and exhibition halls spread across an archaeological site. It rewards visitors who commit to the full complex rather than dropping in on a single pavilion.

**What's inside:**
- **Tel Qasile** — an active archaeological mound with excavated Philistine levels visible in situ; one of the few places in Israel where you can stand on Bronze Age/Iron Age layers without a guided tour
- **Glass Pavilion** — a world-class collection of ancient glass from excavations across Israel; one of the finest glass antiquity collections anywhere
- **Coins Pavilion** — thousands of coins spanning Phoenician, Hellenistic, Roman, Byzantine, Ottoman, and modern Israeli periods
- **Ethnography and Folklore Pavilion** — reconstructed interiors from Jewish communities across the Middle East, North Africa, and Europe
- **Planetarium** — scheduled sky shows; check times at eretzmuseum.org.il

**Practical notes:** Allow a minimum of three hours; half a day is better for the full complex. Combined with ANU (a 10-minute walk), this makes a complete Ramat Aviv museum day.

## Ben-Gurion House

**Where:** 17 Ben-Gurion Boulevard, central Tel Aviv (Rothschild area).

The modest two-storey house at 17 Ben-Gurion Boulevard is where David Ben-Gurion lived with his wife Paula from the 1930s until his death in 1973. Entry is free. The interior is preserved as it was during his lifetime — most notably his personal library of approximately 20,000 books covering philosophy, military strategy, Greek classics, and Buddhism, filling every wall from floor to ceiling.

Ben-Gurion's bedroom, study, and the room where he received foreign dignitaries are all accessible. The house communicates something that biographical accounts sometimes miss: the austerity of a man who shaped a state but lived simply, surrounded entirely by books.

**Guided tours in English** run on scheduled days (historically Tuesdays and Thursdays at 2 pm, Sundays at 11 am) but these times are subject to change. Call ahead or check the Tel Aviv municipality culture listings before building your visit around an English-language tour. The visit without a guide is also worthwhile — allow 45 minutes.

**The house sits one block from the seafront promenade** — easy to combine with a morning walk along the Tayelet before continuing to the Democracy Pavilion and the White City walk along Rothschild Boulevard (Independence Hall, a 10-minute walk south, is currently closed for renovation but the exterior is viewable).

## Independence Hall (Beit Ha'atzmaut) — Currently Closed for Renovation

**Where:** 16 Rothschild Boulevard, central Tel Aviv.

**Note (updated July 2026):** Independence Hall has been closed for renovation since 2024. No official reopening date has been announced. The exterior of the building on the Rothschild Boulevard promenade is still visible and accessible — it is worth pausing here on any White City walk. Call ahead or check the Tel Aviv municipality listings before travelling specifically to visit the interior.

This is the room where the State of Israel was declared: on the evening of 14 May 1948, David Ben-Gurion stood beneath a portrait of Theodor Herzl and read the Israeli Declaration of Independence to an invited audience of 250 people. When it reopens, the hall is expected to be restored to its 1948 appearance, and guided tours have historically been free.

**While the interior is closed:** Visit the Democracy Pavilion, a free interactive installation on Rothschild Boulevard where visitors can explore the founding of Israel and digitally sign the Declaration of Independence. It serves as the accessible alternative for anyone interested in Israeli statehood history on the Rothschild promenade.

**Practical notes:** The building (also called Balfour House, after the original 1910 structure) sits at the southern end of the Rothschild café strip. Ben-Gurion House is a 10-minute walk north along the boulevard. Even with Independence Hall closed, a Ben-Gurion House → Democracy Pavilion → White City walk makes a worthwhile half-morning circuit.

## Design Museum Holon

**Where:** 8 Pinkhas Eilon Street, Holon (not Tel Aviv — a separate city to the south).

The Design Museum Holon is the most architecturally distinctive museum building in the greater Tel Aviv area. Designed by British-Israeli architect Ron Arad and opened in 2010, the structure — five interlocking bands of weathering Corten steel that arc and tilt around a central courtyard — is itself a significant work of design.

The museum focuses on contemporary design, fashion, visual communication, and applied arts. It does not have a large permanent collection in the traditional sense; instead, it runs a rotating programme of major temporary exhibitions that have included retrospectives on Israeli fashion designers, international design biennales, and cross-disciplinary shows combining product design with architecture and film.

**Before visiting:** Check the current exhibition at dmh.org.il. The museum is not worth a separate trip to Holon if the current show doesn't interest you; it is strongly recommended for design, fashion, or architecture enthusiasts when the programme aligns.

**Getting there:** Holon is roughly 20 minutes from central Tel Aviv by car or taxi. There is no direct light rail connection; the most practical option is a taxi (Gett/inDrive) or rideshare. Alternatively, combine with a visit to Old Jaffa (which is en route) and make it a south-of-Tel-Aviv afternoon.

## Planning your museum day

### Rothschild / city-centre half-day
**Ben-Gurion House → Democracy Pavilion → White City walk** — Ben-Gurion House is free; the Democracy Pavilion (free interactive installation on the Declaration of Independence) is nearby on Rothschild Boulevard; the promenade connects them for a pleasant walk. Note: Independence Hall is currently closed for renovation — the exterior is viewable. Add the Tel Aviv Museum of Art (15 minutes by car or light rail) for a full day.

### Ramat Aviv full day
**ANU Museum in the morning → Eretz Israel Museum in the afternoon** — these two sit 10 minutes' walk apart on the Tel Aviv University campus side of Ramat Aviv. This is the most museum-dense day possible in Tel Aviv; budget the full day.

### Design + Jaffa afternoon
**Old Jaffa → Design Museum Holon** — head south from central Tel Aviv; walk Jaffa's flea market and ancient port area in the morning, then continue by taxi to Holon for the Design Museum. Check the current exhibition before committing.

### What to skip if time is short
If you only have one museum slot, the choice between the Tel Aviv Museum of Art (for art) and ANU (for Jewish history and genealogy) depends entirely on your interests. Both are genuinely excellent. Eretz Israel Museum is the most rewarding for archaeology and material culture enthusiasts. The Design Museum is for design-specific visitors.

## Practical information

**Shabbat and holidays:** Most Tel Aviv museums are closed on Friday evenings and Saturdays (Shabbat). Several reopen on Saturday nights. Jewish holidays bring additional closures — always check the specific museum's website for the week you plan to visit. Yom Kippur is a universal closure.

**Admission:** Ben-Gurion House is free. The Democracy Pavilion (an alternative statehood-history experience on Rothschild Boulevard while Independence Hall remains closed) is also free. The Tel Aviv Museum of Art, ANU, Eretz Israel Museum, and Design Museum Holon all charge admission; prices are not reproduced here as they change — check each institution's website directly. Independence Hall is currently closed for renovation; check current status at Tel Aviv municipality listings.

**Guided tours:** All four paid museums offer Hebrew and English guided tours; some require advance booking. ANU's genealogy services sometimes require an appointment. The Tel Aviv Museum of Art's audio guide covers the permanent collection well.

**Israel National Parks Pass:** Does not cover these museums (they are not INPA sites). See the [Israel National Parks Pass guide](/israel-national-parks-pass) for park entry.

**Combining with food:** After Eretz Israel Museum or ANU, the Ramat Aviv mall area has good lunch options. After Rothschild-area museums, Rothschild Boulevard cafés and the nearby Levinsky Market are excellent. After the Design Museum Holon, return via Jaffa for the flea market food stalls and restaurants.
