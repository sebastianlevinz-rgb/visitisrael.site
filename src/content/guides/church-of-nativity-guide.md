The **Church of the Nativity** in [Bethlehem](/bethlehem-travel-guide) is the oldest continuously used Christian church in the world and one of the most-visited pilgrimage sites in the Holy Land. Built by Emperor Constantine around 330 CE over the cave traditionally identified as the birthplace of Jesus — and largely rebuilt by Emperor Justinian in the 6th century — the basilica is jointly administered by the Greek Orthodox, Roman Catholic and Armenian Apostolic churches under the Status Quo arrangement that has governed Christian holy sites in the region since the Ottoman period.

This guide covers everything you need to visit the church itself: the Grotto of the Nativity, how each denomination's space is arranged, the Christmas visit, practical logistics and how to reach Bethlehem from Jerusalem. For the wider city, see the [Bethlehem travel guide](/bethlehem-travel-guide). For a comparison of tour formats, see [Bethlehem tours compared](/bethlehem-tours-compared).

> **West Bank note.** Bethlehem is a Palestinian city in Area A of the West Bank under Palestinian Authority control. Tourists visit freely and daily. Always check your home government's current travel advisory before visiting and confirm your travel insurance covers the West Bank.

## The Door of Humility

The main entrance to the basilica is the **Door of Humility** — a doorway reduced to roughly 1.2 metres high that requires every visitor to bow as they enter. Originally a standard arched opening, it was narrowed in Ottoman times (and possibly earlier during Crusader modifications) to prevent horsemen from riding inside and to make the space harder to fortify. The forced bow became a feature rather than an inconvenience: the humility of entering a sacred site made physical.

## The Main Basilica

Inside, the basilica shows its 6th-century Justinianic construction: 44 original columns of red Bethlehem limestone running the length of the nave, faded Crusader-era paintings visible on several shafts, and wooden roof timbers replaced at various points over the centuries. Trapdoors cut into the later mosaic floor reveal sections of the original 4th-century Constantinian mosaic beneath.

The atmosphere is genuinely ancient and deliberately un-restored compared to most major Christian sites — no renovation has tried to make it look pristine. The Greek Orthodox church holds primary rights to the main altar area; the space is an active place of worship, not a museum.

## The Grotto of the Nativity

The **Grotto** is the cave chamber beneath the basilica's main altar, reached by marble stairs on either side of the chancel. It is the heart of any visit to the Church of the Nativity.

### The Birth Star

At the centre of the Grotto floor, a **14-pointed silver star** set into marble marks the site traditionally identified as the birthplace of Jesus. The Latin inscription around it reads: *Hic de Virgine Maria Jesus Christus natus est* — "Here Jesus Christ was born of the Virgin Mary."

The star has been an object of pilgrimage for centuries. The original star was removed in 1847 (its disappearance contributed to the diplomatic dispute that preceded the Crimean War); the current silver star was installed in 1853 under pressure from France on behalf of the Latin church. It is a shared object — Greek Orthodox priests, Franciscan friars and Armenian clergy all have custodial roles in the Grotto.

### The Manger

A separate niche a few steps from the birth star, slightly lower and to one side, marks the manger. Different denominations have rights to this niche on a rotating schedule.

### Queue management

The Grotto is small — 6 to 8 people enter at a time — which creates queues when the church is busy. The wait is typically 20–40 minutes during mid-morning peak periods and significantly longer during Easter or Christmas:

- **Arrive before 9 am or after 3 pm on a weekday** for the shortest waits.
- **Weekends and pilgrimage days** see the heaviest traffic regardless of hour.
- During **Christmas and Easter**, plan at least 60–90 minutes for the Grotto alone.

Photography inside the Grotto: cameras and phones are permitted. **No flash** — the cave is dimly lit and the flash is prohibited out of respect. Behave as you would in any active place of worship; Orthodox priests may be conducting prayers.

## Denomination map

Understanding which denomination controls which part of the church prevents confusion and accidental disruption of services.

| Area | Primary custodian |
|---|---|
| Main nave | Greek Orthodox |
| Grotto of the Nativity (birth star) | Shared: Greek Orthodox, Latin, Armenian |
| Grotto (manger niche) | Rotating schedule |
| Church of St Catherine | Roman Catholic (Franciscan) |
| Altar of the Kings | Armenian Apostolic |
| South transept | Armenian Apostolic |
| North transept | Greek Orthodox |

The **Status Quo** — an Ottoman-era legal arrangement frozen in 1852 and still operative today — determines who may clean which floor, who hangs which lamp, and who may conduct services when. Disputes over its terms have occasionally become international incidents; the arrangement is managed through a combination of clergy agreements, Israeli legal oversight and Jordanian custodial authority. Visitors encounter it as an interesting reality rather than a problem: the Grotto is genuinely shared, and the three traditions coexist in an ancient cave with remarkable practicality.

## Church of St Catherine (the Catholic church)

Connected to the Nativity basilica via a **Franciscan cloister** — a quiet courtyard with a statue of St Jerome at its centre — the **Church of St Catherine** is the Roman Catholic parish church of Bethlehem. It was built by the Franciscan Custody of the Holy Land in 1881, incorporating earlier Crusader-era remains.

The cloister honours **St Jerome** (347–420 CE), who lived in a cave complex directly beneath and adjacent to the Grotto for over thirty years and here completed his translation of the Bible into Latin — the Vulgate, which became the standard text of Western Christianity for more than a millennium. His tomb, along with those of St Paula and her daughter Eustochium, is in the cave complex accessible from St Catherine's.

### Christmas Eve Mass

The **Latin Christmas Eve Mass** on 24 December is celebrated by the Franciscan Custos of the Holy Land at midnight in St Catherine's. Seats are limited and are allocated through the **Latin Patriarchate of Jerusalem** — apply several months in advance at latinpatriarcha.org. The outdoor ceremony in Manger Square, broadcast on large screens, is free and open to all but arrives well before midnight for a good position.

| Denomination | Christmas date |
|---|---|
| Roman Catholic | 24 December |
| Greek Orthodox | 6 January (Gregorian) |
| Armenian Orthodox | 18 January (Gregorian) |

## Dress code and etiquette

- **Covered shoulders and knees** required for entry — for both men and women. Scarves are sometimes available at the entrance. See the [holy sites dress code guide](/holy-sites-dress-code-etiquette) for the full detail.
- Photography is permitted throughout the basilica and in the Grotto (no flash).
- **Do not enter while a service is in progress** unless you have been invited by the officiating clergy to observe quietly.
- The Grotto has a designated flow — enter from one side, exit from the other. Follow the direction indicated by the attendant or clergy present.

Entry to the Church of the Nativity is **free**. Donations in side chapels are customary but not required.

---

## Jerome's cave and the adjacent grottoes

Below and to the north of the Nativity Grotto, accessible from St Catherine's Church, is a separate cave system associated with Jerome, St Paula, and — in Catholic tradition — the cave where the Innocents slaughtered by Herod were interred. A visit to St Catherine's is worth combining with the main basilica visit; the cave complex adds a quieter, less-crowded dimension to the experience.

## Getting there from Jerusalem

The Church of the Nativity is in central Bethlehem, 10 km south of Jerusalem across the West Bank separation barrier.

**By guided tour** (recommended for first-time visitors): Organised tours from Jerusalem handle Checkpoint 300, provide transport and include a licensed guide. Half-day tours depart mid-morning and return by early afternoon. See [Bethlehem tours compared](/bethlehem-tours-compared).

**Independently**: Arab bus **231** from Damascus Gate runs to the checkpoint approach. Cross Checkpoint 300 on foot with your passport — typically 2–5 minutes for tourists. Take a local taxi or shared service into Manger Square (5 minutes; agree a price first). Return the same way.

**Do not drive your hire car**: Israeli rental car insurance typically excludes West Bank Area A. See the [driving in Israel guide](/driving-in-israel) for the insurance detail.

Bethlehem sits within Area A of the Palestinian Authority — no separate visa or permit is required for tourists holding Israeli entry (ETA-IL or visa). For full entry context, see the [visa and ETA-IL guide](/visa-information).

## Combining with other sites

A half-day from Jerusalem covers the church, Manger Square, and a quick walk along the separation barrier art. A full day adds Shepherds' Field, the old city, lunch and time for craft shopping.

The most popular extensions:
- **Shepherds' Field** (Beit Sahour, 2 km by taxi) — where shepherds received the nativity news in Luke's Gospel; the Franciscan chapel includes a Byzantine cave church
- **Manger Square and the Mosque of Omar** — the square directly in front of the basilica
- **Separation barrier and Banksy artworks** — 5 minutes by taxi from Manger Square
- **Jericho** — a 40-minute drive down through the Judean Desert adds 3,500 years of continuous settlement history; many full-day tours combine both
