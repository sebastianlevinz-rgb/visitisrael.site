Jerusalem's coffee scene is smaller and less written-about than Tel Aviv's, but it holds its own. A cluster of specialty roasters around Mahane Yehuda market, a strip of neighbourhood cafés along Emek Refaim, and a collection of kosher institutions in the city's stone-building residential quarters form a circuit well worth an extended morning.

The character is entirely different from Tel Aviv. Stone architecture, slower pace, heavier Shabbat observance, and a denser intersection with religious life give Jerusalem café culture something that has no equivalent anywhere else in the country.

## The specialty roasters

Jerusalem's specialty scene has two main anchors, with a ring of smaller neighbourhood cafés around them. All details below reflect 2026 research — verify current hours and locations via Google Maps before visiting.

### Power CoffeeWorks — Mahane Yehuda

The flagship specialty roaster of Jerusalem, Power CoffeeWorks operates in and near Mahane Yehuda market and is consistently cited as the city's most serious coffee address. Direct-trade sourcing, a strong filter and espresso programme, and a location inside the most energetic market in the country make it both the obvious first stop and one of the most memorable café experiences in Jerusalem. The combination of specialty technique and shuk energy — coffee alongside the noise, colour and smell of a working produce market — is specific to this place.

### Birma — Emek Refaim

Birma, on the Emek Refaim strip in the German Colony, is the neighbourhood anchor of the southern café cluster. Known for precise espresso and a calm, regulars-heavy atmosphere, Birma is what you visit when you want quality coffee without the market theatrics — a serious neighbourhood café that holds its own against anything in Tel Aviv. The German Colony surroundings (tree-lined streets, stone buildings, families walking on Shabbat evening) give the experience a gentler texture than the shuk.

### Tmol Shilshom — Nahalat Shiva

One of the most characterful café spaces in Israel: a kosher literary café installed in a 19th-century stone building near Nahalat Shiva, lined with books and frequented by writers, academics and tourists who have discovered it. Tmol Shilshom is not purely a specialty-coffee destination — it is a place — but the coffee is good, the menu is serious (full lunch and dinner service, kosher), and an hour spent here is unlike anything in the Tel Aviv café scene. It closes on Shabbat and Jewish holidays in line with its kosher certification.

### Sybaris — Rehavia

Sybaris is a shorter walk from Emek Refaim, in the Rehavia residential neighbourhood, with a focus on cold brew and filter coffee. An option for those who find the shuk scene too hectic or want a more focused specialty experience away from the tourist circuit.

### Cafelix — city centre branch

Cafelix — also found across Tel Aviv — operates a Jerusalem branch with the same single-origin espresso approach and French-press service the brand is known for. A reliable quality option if you are already in the city centre.

### Coffee Mill — German Colony

Coffee Mill is one of the older café institutions of the German Colony and Emek Refaim area — an established neighbourhood café that predates the third-wave scene. Reliable, unpretentious, popular with local families and with a Shabbat closure policy in line with the predominantly kosher character of its neighbourhood.

## The neighbourhood café circuit

Jerusalem's café geography is less continuous than Tel Aviv's — the specialty spots are concentrated rather than strung along a single walking corridor. The most practical circuit combines two clusters:

**Mahane Yehuda → Nachlaot → Emek Refaim → Rehavia**

Start at **[Mahane Yehuda market](/mahane-yehuda-market-guide)** in the morning, when the shuk is at its liveliest and Power CoffeeWorks is running its full café operation. The market's produce and spice stalls, the smell of fresh bread from the bakeries, and the noise of the market traders provide a backdrop for the first coffee stop that no other Israeli city can replicate. Allow time to walk the market before or after the coffee.

From the market, cut south through **Nachlaot** — one of Jerusalem's most photogenic old residential neighbourhoods, a dense warren of stone alleys between the shuk and Emek Refaim. Smaller cafés and neighbourhood spots appear on the alleyway corners; Nahalat Shiva (adjacent) is where Tmol Shilshom sits.

Continue south to **Emek Refaim** and the German Colony — the main tree-lined street with Birma and Coffee Mill — for a second café stop. This is the quieter, more residential option, suited to a longer sit. See the [Jerusalem neighborhoods guide](/jerusalem-neighborhoods-guide) for the wider German Colony context.

A short detour into **Rehavia** adds Sybaris if you want a cold-brew-focused stop or an afternoon second coffee.

The full circuit covers roughly 4–5 km on foot and works well as a full morning, particularly on weekdays (avoid planning the shuk stop for Saturday if you want Power CoffeeWorks specifically — check their Shabbat policy).

## Shabbat and kosher: the practical reality

The single most important practical difference between Jerusalem and Tel Aviv café culture is Shabbat observance.

In Tel Aviv, most specialty cafés — being non-kosher — open on Saturday as normal. In Jerusalem, a much higher proportion of cafés are kosher-certified, which means they close from before Shabbat starts (Friday afternoon, time varies by season) until after Shabbat ends (Saturday night, roughly 90 minutes after sunset).

**On Friday afternoons:** plan for shorter café hours — many close by 14:00–15:00. This is not a problem if you know in advance; it can be frustrating if you arrive at 16:00 expecting an open café.

**On Saturday:** check each café individually via Google Maps. Non-kosher cafés (less common in the western Jewish neighbourhoods) may open; kosher cafés will not. The German Colony has a mix. The Muslim Quarter and East Jerusalem have cafés and restaurants that operate normally on Saturday.

**On Saturday night (Motzei Shabbat):** the city visibly reopens. Cafés that closed on Friday come back to life, and the neighbourhood streets fill up relatively quickly. A coffee stop on Saturday night is one of the city's underrated pleasures.

For the full system of kosher certification and what it means for visitors, see the [kosher food guide](/kosher-food-guide).

## Coffee and the Old City

The Old City has almost no specialty café infrastructure — the Muslim Quarter and Christian Quarter have traditional Arab coffee shops serving botz-style coffee and Arabic coffee (cardamom-spiced, light roast) rather than espresso. If you are spending time in the Old City and the Arab coffee tradition is of interest, look for the small coffee stalls along the market lanes and in the Arab Quarter; the experience is completely distinct from the western specialty scene and worth trying on its own terms.

## Practical tips

**Coffee style.** Jerusalem specialty cafés follow the same espresso-plus-filter pattern as Tel Aviv: flat whites, cortados, V60 and aeropress are standard at third-wave venues. Arabic coffee (cardamom-spiced, light roast) is available in the Old City and East Jerusalem. Traditional botz (unfiltered Turkish-style) appears at older Israeli cafés and market kiosks.

**Languages.** Hebrew is dominant; English is widely understood at specialty cafés. In the Old City and East Jerusalem, Arabic and English are the main languages.

**Payment.** Cards are accepted at virtually all specialty cafés. Cash is preferable at older market kiosks and Arab coffee stalls.

**When to visit.** The optimal Jerusalem café morning is Sunday–Thursday, when the full range of options is open. If visiting on Saturday, plan around East Jerusalem or non-kosher venues and check opening hours in advance.
