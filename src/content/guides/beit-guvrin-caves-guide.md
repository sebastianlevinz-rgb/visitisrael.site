**Beit Guvrin** (Hebrew: **בֵּית גֻּבְרִין**; Arabic: بيت جبرين, Bayt Jibrīn) sits in the Judean Lowlands about 60 km southwest of Jerusalem, where 2,500 years of continuous underground activity have produced one of the most extraordinary subterranean landscapes on earth. The national park — jointly designated with ancient Tel Maresha — holds more than 3,500 catalogued cave chambers: Hellenistic burial vaults painted with hunting scenes and exotic animals, vast Ottoman chalk quarries shaped like inverted bells, columbaria packed with dove niches, Roman-era oil presses, and Byzantine cisterns. UNESCO recognised the site in 2014 as a microcosm of "the land of the caves."

This guide covers the Bell Caves, the Sidonian Burial Caves, the columbarium complex, the Roman amphitheatre remains, and how to plan a full Judean Lowlands day.

---

## The Bell Caves

### How they were made

The Bell Caves are the most visually striking feature of the park and the easiest to understand once you see the quarrying logic. Crusader and Ottoman builders needed chalk for mortar, lime, and construction stone. To extract it, they located surface outcrops, broke through a narrow opening at ground level — no wider than a person — and then widened the cavity progressively downward in all directions. Because the quarrymen removed more material as they descended, each cavity is narrowest at the top and widest at the base: the classic inverted-bell shape that gives the caves their name.

The result is around 800 bell-shaped chambers distributed across the park, ranging from modest 3-metre closets to cathedral-scale voids 10–20 metres high and 15 metres across. Natural light pours in through the circular openings at the top, throwing theatrical beams across chalk-pale walls. On the chamber floors, quarry marks and footholds chipped into the walls trace exactly where the Ottoman workers descended into the growing void beneath them.

### What to see

The main Bell Cave complex has a boardwalk and visitor path that connects several of the largest chambers. Key features to look for:

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Central bell chamber | The largest chamber open to visitors; natural-light beam from top opening |
| Stacked chambers | Several bells carved close together, separated by thin chalk walls that survived the quarrying |
| Tool marks | Parallel chisel grooves on chamber walls; footstep niches cut into the sides |
| Cross carvings | Byzantine and Crusader pilgrims carved crosses into the chalk in multiple chambers |
| Connecting passages | Low horizontal tunnels linking adjacent bells; some require ducking |

The Bell Caves require natural light from the openings to see well — a visit on a bright morning between 9am and 12pm gives the most dramatic illumination. On overcast days the chambers are dimmer. A torch is useful in the connecting passages.

## The Sidonian Burial Caves

### Hellenistic Marisa

Ancient Maresha (biblical Marisha) was a major city in the Judean Lowlands from at least the 8th century BCE. In the Hellenistic period (3rd–2nd centuries BCE), a large community of Phoenicians from the city of Sidon settled there, and they carved an elaborate necropolis into the soft chalk outside the city walls. These burial caves are among the best-preserved painted burial chambers from the Hellenistic world.

### The painted murals

The Sidonian Burial Caves are named for the community that made them, and their murals are unlike anything else in Israel. The paintings — rendered in red, black, yellow, and blue on white plaster — include:

- **A frieze of animals**: elephant, rhinoceros, giraffe, eagle, and domesticated animals that would have been exotic to the ancient Mediterranean world, possibly drawn from descriptions of Ptolemaic royal menageries in Egypt
- **Hunting scenes**: armed figures pursuing game, with Greek inscriptions naming the hunters
- **Inscriptions in Greek**: names, dates, dedications, and epitaphs identifying the buried individuals
- **Musicians and dancers**: figures at a symposium-style feast

The colours are faded but still clearly legible in the major friezes. The cave complex has multiple chambers connected by carved doorways; the largest chamber holds the most complete painted scenes.

**Practical note on access**: The Sidonian Burial Caves within the park sometimes operate on restricted timed entry or require advance booking with INPA, separate from the main park entrance. Confirm current access arrangements when planning — the caves are not always open for independent entry and a guided slot may be required. Check [parks.org.il](https://www.parks.org.il) or call the INPA visitor centre before your visit.

## The columbarium caves

Columbaria (Latin: *columbarium*, dovecote) are caves fitted with thousands of small carved niches — approximately 30 × 30 cm each — that were used to keep domesticated doves. Beit Guvrin has dozens of columbarium caves dating from the Second Temple period (roughly 2nd century BCE–70 CE). The doves served as food, as animals for religious sacrifice in the Jerusalem Temple, and possibly for messaging.

The niches in the Beit Guvrin columbaria are carved with remarkable regularity — rows upon rows covering every wall and ceiling, sometimes running 20–30 niches high. A large columbarium cave can hold 2,000–3,000 niches. With a torch, the carved geometry of the niches becomes fully visible. These are genuinely impressive at close range: the scale of the dove operation implied by the niches gives a visceral sense of the agricultural industry surrounding the ancient city.

## The Roman amphitheatre and Tel Maresha

Beit Guvrin contains the only visible Roman amphitheatre in Israel open to the public. The structure is substantially ruined — only the lower seating banks and arena outline survive — but the footprint is clear, and informational panels contextualise how the Roman city of Eleutheropolis (Beit Guvrin's name under Roman rule from the 2nd century CE) would have used it for gladiatorial contests and animal fights. At its peak, Eleutheropolis was one of the most important cities in the province of Judea Palaestina.

Adjacent to the amphitheatre is Tel Maresha — the mound of the ancient city itself, built up over millennia of occupation from the Canaanite period through the Hellenistic era. Archaeological excavations have uncovered streets, houses, workshops, and a Phoenician marketplace beneath the tel. The tel surface is walkable; views from the top extend across the Judean foothills.

## Planning your visit

### Admission and hours

Beit Guvrin–Maresha National Park is managed by INPA. The **Israel National Parks Pass is valid** for entry. Without a pass, admission is charged at the gate. Current prices are published at [parks.org.il](https://www.parks.org.il) — always verify before visiting as prices are updated periodically. Adult admission has historically been in the ₪29–35 range; children (5–18) approximately half-price; under-5 free.

**Operating hours** (verify at parks.org.il before travelling — hours change seasonally):
- Sunday–Thursday and Saturday: 8:00am to 5:00pm (last entry 4:00pm)
- Friday: 8:00am to 4:00pm (last entry 3:00pm)
- Closed: Yom Kippur

The park is open on Shabbat (Saturday), unlike some Jewish-owned sites — one of the practical advantages of an INPA national park. See the [Shabbat guide](/shabbat-guide) for context on what stays open.

### Getting there by car

Beit Guvrin is only practically reachable by car. There is no regular bus service to the national park entrance.

**From Jerusalem (60 km, ~60 min):** Head west on Highway 1 toward Tel Aviv, take the Latrun interchange exit, then drive south on Route 38 (signposted Beit Shemesh and Kiryat Gat). After approximately 25 km, turn onto Route 35 west — follow INPA signs to Beit Guvrin national park. Parking at the entrance is free.

**From Tel Aviv (80 km, ~75 min):** Take Highway 1 east to the Latrun interchange, then south on Route 38 as above.

**From Beer Sheva (50 km, ~50 min):** Route 35 east through the Judean foothills directly to the park.

### Combining Beit Guvrin with a Judean day

Beit Guvrin works well as part of a longer Judean Lowlands circuit:

**The Elah Valley loop (half-day add-on):** The drive from Jerusalem to Beit Guvrin on Route 38 passes through the Elah Valley — where the biblical account places David and Goliath's encounter. A short stop at the stream bed, now signposted as a tourist site near the town of Khirbet Qeiyafa, adds 30–45 minutes to the journey with no backtracking.

**Ashkelon National Park (40 km west):** Roman ruins, Canaanite city gate, Crusader fortifications, and beach on the Mediterranean coast. A good afternoon addition if you've done Beit Guvrin in the morning.

**Tel Lachish (15 km south):** The ancient Canaanite-Israelite-Assyrian city whose siege is depicted in the Lachish Reliefs now displayed in the British Museum. The tel is openly accessible (no fee) and connects directly to the Beit Guvrin area via Route 35.

For a Jerusalem-based day-trip context, see [Jerusalem day trips](/day-trips-from-jerusalem).

### Practical checklist

- **Torch / flashlight**: essential for columbarium niches and connecting passages; phone torch is sufficient
- **Good walking shoes**: cave paths are unpaved chalk; can be slippery after rain
- **Water**: bring 1–1.5 litres per person; the caves are cool but walks between clusters are exposed
- **INPA pass**: if you have one, bring it — saves the gate fee and is valid here
- **Sidonian Burial Caves**: call INPA or check parks.org.il for current access — may require advance slot booking
- **Camera**: the Bell Caves in morning light reward patience; long exposures not needed as ambient light is strong
- **Children**: the caves are excellent for families; the columbaria and the scale of the Bell Caves hold children's attention well; some passages require ducking (not appropriate for very young children in pushchairs)

## Nearby sites

**Tel Lachish (15 km south)** — The major Israelite fortress besieged by Sennacherib in 701 BCE and Nebuchadnezzar in 588 BCE; siege ramp still visible. Open access, no fee. The Lachish Letters — ancient Hebrew ostraca discovered here — are displayed in the Israel Museum.

**Ashkelon National Park (40 km west)** — Mediterranean coast archaeological park with Canaanite gate (oldest arched city gate in the world), Crusader cathedral ruins, and Roman basilica. INPA pass valid. See [Ashkelon](/israel-hidden-gems) for context.

**Jerusalem (60 km northeast)** — The natural base city for a Beit Guvrin visit. See the [Jerusalem travel guide](/jerusalem) for the full Old City and day-trip orientation.

**Masada and the Dead Sea (90–100 km southeast)** — A second major Judean day from Jerusalem; not practical to combine with Beit Guvrin in a single day but part of the same regional circuit. See [Masada visitor guide](/masada-visitor-guide) and the [Dead Sea guide](/dead-sea-guide).

**Sorek Cave (50 km northeast)** — Israel's largest stalactite cave, near Beit Shemesh; guided tour required, INPA pass not valid there. See [Sorek Cave guide](/soreq-cave-guide) for the contrast between the two cave experiences — Beit Guvrin is largely human-made chalk quarrying; Sorek is natural limestone geology.
