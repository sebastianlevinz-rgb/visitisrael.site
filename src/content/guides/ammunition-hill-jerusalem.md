**Ammunition Hill** — Givat HaTachmoshet in Hebrew — is the preserved battlefield of one of the most intense single engagements of the 1967 Six-Day War. On the night of June 5–6, 1967, 218 Israeli paratroopers fought approximately 150 Jordanian Arab Legion defenders through the concrete underground trench complex for control of the northern route into East Jerusalem. The battle lasted four hours. Thirty-six Israelis and an estimated 182 Jordanians were killed.

The entire trench system has been preserved exactly as it was left after the battle. Visitors can walk through the same bunkers, communications trenches, machine-gun positions and command post that were fought over that night — one of the few battlefield sites anywhere in the world where visitors stand in the ground-level geography of the fight itself. Entry is free.

---

## The battle

### Why Ammunition Hill mattered

The hill was a fortified Jordanian military compound guarding the northern corridor between the Mount Scopus enclave (a demilitarised UN-supervised zone since 1948) and the rest of east Jerusalem. It sat directly on the approach road to the Ambassador Hotel district and the northern approaches to the Old City. While Israel's senior commanders hoped the Six-Day War might be fought only on the Egyptian front, Jordan's opening artillery bombardment of west Jerusalem on June 5 brought the Jerusalem front into the war.

Capturing Ammunition Hill was assigned to the 66th Battalion of the 55th Airborne Brigade — paratroopers, many of them reservists, who had been called up only days earlier.

### The night of June 5–6, 1967

The assault began shortly after midnight. The paratroopers advanced in company formation through a shallow wadi and breached the perimeter fence. What followed was a sustained close-quarters battle through the interconnected trenches — a three-dimensional underground maze of concrete passages that the Jordanians had fortified over nineteen years.

Jordanian bunker positions provided overlapping fields of fire. Israeli company commanders made real-time decisions that are still studied in military training: when to advance, when to consolidate, how to clear trenches with grenades and small-arms fire against an entrenched defender. The IDF's official account identifies 36 positions in the trench system that had to be taken individually. The battle continued until dawn.

By early morning on June 6, the hill was under Israeli control. The road north of the Old City walls was open. Later that morning, paratroopers from the same brigade reached the Lions' Gate and entered the Old City.

The paratroopers' commander, Lieutenant Colonel Yossi Yoffe, radioed at 10am on June 7: *"The Temple Mount is in our hands."* The Western Wall, unreachable to Jews since 1948, was accessible again within hours of Ammunition Hill's capture.

## What to see

### The preserved trench system

The original concrete trenches are the heart of the site. Eleven sections are open to visitors:

- **Entry breach point** — the gap in the perimeter fence through which the paratroopers advanced, marked with an explanatory panel
- **Communications trench** — the main artery of the Jordanian defensive position, running approximately 40 metres east-west; overhead cover is original
- **Machine-gun nests** — concrete emplacements with original slit apertures, positioned to cover the approach from the wadi
- **Command post bunker** — the deepest underground section; original communications equipment replica; the focal point of the Jordanian defence
- **Ammunition store** — the function that gave the hill its English name; original reinforced room
- **Jordanian observation post** — panoramic view northward over Jerusalem that explained the strategic value of the position

Audio guides (available in English, Hebrew, Russian) walk visitors through the battle chronologically, explaining what happened at each position. They are the most valuable way to make sense of the trench layout.

### The Paratroopers' Museum

The indoor museum in the main building documents the battle in detail and places it in the context of the 1967 war and Israeli paratrooper history. Exhibits include:

- Archival photographs from the battle, including pre-assault reconnaissance images and post-battle documentation
- Israeli and Jordanian weapons recovered from the site
- Detailed battle maps showing the assault phases
- Personal testimonies from veterans of both sides — the museum unusually includes accounts from Jordanian participants where available
- Unit insignia and equipment from the 55th Airborne Brigade

The museum's treatment of the battle acknowledges that the Jordanian soldiers who died were professional soldiers defending their assigned position. The framing is military-historical rather than triumphalist.

### The memorial park

The outdoor memorial park surrounds the trench complex. Individual plaques name all 36 Israeli paratroopers killed in the battle. A separate Jordanian soldier memorial acknowledges the defenders. The panoramic view northward from the hill — over the French Hill neighbourhood, the Hebrew University campus on Mount Scopus, and the northern Jerusalem ridge — makes clear why this position mattered.

## Practical information

**Address:** Eshkol Boulevard, Ramat Eshkol neighbourhood, Jerusalem (Google Maps: Ammunition Hill / Givat HaTachmoshet)

**Opening hours:** Sunday–Thursday 8:00–18:00; Friday 8:00–14:00; closed Saturday. Verify current hours at [gvahatacharad.co.il](https://gvahatacharad.co.il) before visiting — holiday schedules can vary.

**Entry:** The outdoor trench complex and memorial park are free. The Paratroopers' Museum charges a small admission fee — check the current rate at the official site as it changes.

**Audio guide:** Available in English, Hebrew and Russian. Recommended for visitors without prior knowledge of the battle; the trench layout is not self-explanatory without chronological context.

**Accessibility:** The entire site — trenches, museum and memorial park — is paved and accessible for visitors with limited mobility. The trench passages are wide enough for wheelchairs; some sections have gradual slopes rather than steps.

**Time needed:** 1.5–2 hours for a thorough visit.

**Getting there:**
- **Light rail (Blue Line):** Ammunition Hill station is within walking distance. From City Hall station (near Jaffa Gate) the journey takes approximately 15 minutes.
- **Taxi:** 15 minutes from the Old City / Jaffa Gate area; approximately ₪30–45.
- **Bus:** Routes 25 and 28 stop near the site from Damascus Gate.
- **Car:** Free parking on site. From central Jerusalem, take HaNevi'im Street north and follow signs to Ramat Eshkol / Ammunition Hill.

## Planning your visit

### Best combination: battle site + liberation

The most historically coherent Jerusalem day combines **Ammunition Hill in the morning** (the battle that opened the way) with the **Western Wall and Jewish Quarter in the afternoon** (what the battle made accessible). The light rail's Blue Line connects Ammunition Hill station to the Old City area in under 20 minutes.

If you also want to visit the [Western Wall Tunnels](/western-wall-tunnels-guide) — the underground tour of the Herodian retaining wall — book tickets in advance; they sell out.

### For history-focused itineraries

Ammunition Hill fits naturally into longer heritage itineraries:
- **Six-Day War focus:** Ammunition Hill + the [Jewish Quarter](/jerusalem/old-city) (rebuilt after 1967) + the Western Wall.
- **Modern Israel heritage:** Ammunition Hill + [Yad Vashem](/yad-vashem-visitor-guide) (both northwest/north Jerusalem; share a taxi or use light rail) + [Mount Herzl military cemetery](/zionist-heritage-trail) (adjacent to Yad Vashem; national military cemetery).
- **Jerusalem Trail hikers:** Ammunition Hill is the terminus of Segment 4 of the [Jerusalem Trail](/jerusalem-trail). Hiking in and spending time at the memorial before descending to the light rail station is a natural end to that segment.

### When to visit

The site is open year-round. **Morning visits** (before 11am) avoid the midday heat in summer and typically have fewer group tours, allowing quieter time in the trenches. **Spring and autumn** are the most comfortable seasons weather-wise. Independence Day (Yom HaAtzmaut) and Memorial Day (Yom HaZikaron, the day before) see elevated attendance as Israeli families visit the site; the national atmosphere on those days is profound but the site can be crowded.
