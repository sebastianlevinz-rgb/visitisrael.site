Israel sits on some of the most densely layered archaeological terrain on earth. A journey of 30 kilometres from Jerusalem crosses Bronze Age hillforts, Iron Age cities, Hellenistic farms, Roman roads, Byzantine monasteries and Ottoman aqueducts — often on the same site. The result is that Israel hosts more active archaeological excavations per square kilometre than almost anywhere else in the world, and a growing number of those sites offer **structured opportunities for ordinary travellers to pick up a trowel and join in**.

This guide covers everything from one-day tourist dig programmes to multi-week volunteer excavations: how to book, what to expect on a dig day, and how to build an Israel itinerary around one of the most distinctive travel experiences the country offers.

---

## Why Dig in Israel?

For travellers with an interest in biblical history, ancient civilisations or the archaeology of the ancient Near East, Israel offers something that Egypt, Greece or Italy rarely do: **direct participation in live, academically-supervised excavations**. Active digs at Tel Megiddo, Tel Shiloh, Tel Gezer and dozens of other sites run each year, and many offer structured opportunities for non-specialist visitors.

The physical experience is genuinely unusual. At most tourist sites, the archaeology is behind a fence. In a dig programme, you are *in* the stratigraphy — handling pottery sherds that were last touched 2,500 years ago, recording finds in real field notebooks, working alongside PhD students and senior archaeologists. Even a single day of this tends to leave participants with a different relationship to the ancient past than any museum exhibit achieves.

## Dig for a Day: Tourist Archaeology Programmes

These programmes offer a structured day — or half-day — of real archaeological fieldwork, supervised by professional archaeologists and designed for visitors without prior experience. No qualification is required.

### Tel Megiddo (Armageddon)

**Tel Megiddo National Park**, in the Jezreel Valley 35km southeast of Haifa, is one of the longest-excavated sites in the Middle East. Occupied continuously from approximately 7,000 BCE to the 6th century BCE, the tel carries 26 distinct occupation strata, including Bronze Age temples, an Israelite water tunnel, and a gateway whose period attribution remains a matter of genuine scholarly debate. Megiddo is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is covered by the [Israel National Parks Pass](/israel-national-parks-pass).

The **Megiddo Expedition** (Tel Aviv University) periodically offers structured tourist dig days tied to active excavation seasons, typically running from **April through August**. A dig day involves real fieldwork within designated squares: sifting excavated soil, identifying pottery sherds by period, recording finds, and receiving briefings from senior researchers. Participation fees are typically in the range of ₪250–450 per person; current scheduling and booking are through the official expedition website at **megiddo.tau.ac.il**. Availability is limited and must be arranged in advance — walk-up participation is not offered.

The site also includes the INPA-managed visitor circuit and the Megiddo Museum, making a dig-day visit easy to combine with the full archaeological tour. See our complete [Tel Megiddo & Jezreel Valley guide](/megiddo-jezreel-valley-guide) for site planning details.

### Beit Guvrin and Maresha Caves

**Beit Guvrin-Maresha National Park**, in the Judean foothills 55km southwest of Jerusalem, is best known for its extraordinary **labyrinth of carved chalk caves** — ancient quarries, columbaria (dove houses), oil press installations and underground burial chambers cut by the Sidonian and Jewish communities of the Hellenistic period (3rd–2nd century BCE). The park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is covered by the Israel National Parks Pass.

The site offers a **"Dig for a Day" experience** in partnership with the IAA (Israel Antiquities Authority), typically running from **October through June** to avoid peak summer heat in this exposed chalk landscape. Participants excavate within actively monitored squares and learn to identify find types — pottery, bone, glass, plaster — within their stratigraphic context. Participation fees are in the range of ₪150–250 per adult depending on the specific programme; advance registration is required through **parks.org.il**.

Beit Guvrin is an excellent option for families with older children. The chalk geology makes some finds immediately visible even to an untrained eye, and the underground cave network makes the site dramatic regardless of the dig component.

### Tel Shiloh

**Tel Shiloh** (Khirbet Seilun), in the central hill country north of Jerusalem, is identified in the Hebrew Bible as the site where the Israelite Tabernacle stood for approximately 300 years before the Ark's capture by the Philistines (1 Samuel 4). Active excavations have continued intermittently here since the 1920s.

The Shiloh Heritage Institute periodically offers structured **volunteer dig days** open to individuals and church or synagogue groups, typically in the spring season. These programmes are particularly popular with Christian heritage travellers and Jewish diaspora visitors for whom the Tabernacle period carries religious significance. Participation fees and current scheduling are available through the official Tel Shiloh visitor site. Note that the site is located in Area C of the West Bank — check current entry and travel guidance for your nationality before planning a visit, and see our [Israel National Parks Pass guide](/israel-national-parks-pass) for context on which sites that pass covers.

## Multi-Week Volunteer Programmes

For travellers who can commit one to several weeks, Israel's university-run excavations offer the opportunity to work as genuine field volunteers alongside academic researchers. Several are either free to join or low-cost.

### IAA Volunteer Dig Corps

The **Israel Antiquities Authority Volunteer Dig Corps** offers **free placement on active IAA excavations** across the country — from salvage digs ahead of infrastructure projects to long-running research excavations at major sites. Volunteers receive the dig experience itself at no charge; accommodation and meals are the participant's own responsibility. Apply at least **six months in advance** through **iaa.gov.il** (English application available). The programme is competitive and popular; Hebrew is not required for most excavations, as the field briefings and volunteer orientation are available in English.

Sites vary year to year and are not guaranteed in advance. The IAA assigns based on current excavation needs and group size. Previous participants have worked at sites ranging from Byzantine-period agricultural terraces in the Galilee to Roman urban structures in the Tel Aviv region.

### ICARE Volunteer Programme

**ICARE** (International Community for Archaeological Research and Education; icare-archaeology.org) offers structured multi-week volunteer programmes at selected Israeli sites, typically in two-week sessions through spring and summer. Fees are approximately $900–1,500 USD per session, covering accommodation, meals, field transport and structured academic instruction. This format suits travellers who want the full immersive experience with guaranteed logistics, without the application uncertainty of the free IAA programme.

### University Excavation Programmes

**Hebrew University of Jerusalem**, **Tel Aviv University** and **Bar-Ilan University** all run ongoing excavations and regularly accept international volunteers for active field seasons, typically four to six weeks in summer. Applications are reviewed by the relevant archaeology department; some fieldwork experience is helpful but not required for volunteer roles. Check each university's archaeology department website — the annual cycle of active excavation sites changes based on funding and excavation progress, so what is available in one year may not run the next.

## What Happens on a Dig Day

If you have never joined an excavation, the reality is quite different from what television programmes suggest.

**Early start.** Almost all Israeli field excavations begin at 5:00–6:30 am. Even in spring, working through the cool morning hours before temperatures rise is essential. Tourist day programmes often start later, around 7:00 am, but still finish by early afternoon.

**Supervised fieldwork.** You work within a designated square under direct supervision of a field archaeologist or senior student. The work is physical but not heavy: careful use of a trowel to expose layers, bucket-filling for soil to be sifted through a screen, and examining what comes out. Pottery sherds are the most common find at every site in Israel, and a beginner quickly learns to distinguish coarse Bronze Age cooking ware from fine Hellenistic storage jars by texture and rim profile.

**Significant finds.** Occasionally, objects of direct archaeological interest turn up — seal impressions, coins, architectural elements, organic remains. These are always handled by the supervising archaeologist; the field volunteer records the find location in the field notebook and photographs it in situ.

**Break and close.** After a mid-morning break (water and shade are critical), fieldwork continues until approximately 1:00 pm, when the site typically closes for the day. The afternoon is free for site visits, the on-site museum, or travel to the next destination.

**What to bring:** closed-toe shoes with ankle support; a wide-brimmed hat; 2–3 litres of water per person; SPF 50+ sunscreen; old clothes you don't mind getting dusty; and a camera. Tools are provided.

## Best Season to Dig

**Spring (March–June)** is the most active season for Israeli excavations and offers the most comfortable field conditions. Most university programmes also run spring sessions, and tourist dig-day availability is highest.

**Summer (July–August)** is the traditional university excavation season, though fieldwork stops by midday due to heat. Tourist-oriented dig-day programmes are less likely to run in peak summer — verify directly with individual sites.

**Autumn (October–November)** is the second active window for IAA volunteer programmes and tourist-oriented sites like Beit Guvrin. This is often the best season for first-time visitors combining a dig with the wider tourist circuit, as temperatures are moderate and other attractions are accessible without the summer heat.

**Winter (December–February):** most excavations are in analysis and publication work during this period; active dig-day programmes are uncommon and should not be assumed without direct confirmation.

## Combining With Other Sites

Archaeological Israel lends itself to multi-day circuits:

**Jezreel Valley circuit (1–2 days):** Tel Megiddo dig day → [Beit She'arim necropolis and Roman-era catacombs](/beit-shearim-guide) → [Beit Alfa Byzantine mosaic synagogue](/beit-alfa-guide) → [Beit She'an Roman theatre and Byzantine baths](/beit-shean-guide). See the [Jezreel Valley guide](/megiddo-jezreel-valley-guide) for the full circuit.

**Judean foothills day:** Beit Guvrin dig → [Valley of Elah](/valley-of-elah-guide) (landscape associated with 1 Samuel 17, with confirmed Iron Age IIA archaeology at Khirbet Qeiyafa) → Lachish (Assyrian siege site).

**Dead Sea archaeology morning:** [Qumran](/qumran-guide) (Dead Sea Scrolls discovery site; guided archaeologist walk rather than active dig participation) → Ein Gedi waterfalls → Masada. The Shrine of the Book at the Israel Museum in Jerusalem holds the actual scrolls — worth combining if you are based in the capital.

## Before You Book

- **Confirm dates directly** with the organising institution before booking travel. Dig schedules depend on funding, permits and weather — they are not fixed months in advance.
- **Verify academic affiliation** for any private-operator dig experience before paying. Some operators sell access to archaeological experiences at higher price points; legitimate programmes will clearly identify the supervising institution and archaeologist.
- **IAA free placement is competitive.** Apply early and indicate flexibility on site assignment — popular excavations fill months in advance.
- **The INPA Annual Pass** covers entrance to the main archaeological national parks (Megiddo, Beit Guvrin, Caesarea, Masada and dozens of others). If you plan to visit two or more INPA sites, the pass typically pays for itself. See the full [Israel National Parks Pass guide](/israel-national-parks-pass).
