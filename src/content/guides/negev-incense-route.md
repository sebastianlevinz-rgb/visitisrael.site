The **Negev Incense Route** is one of the ancient world's most ambitious commercial networks — a chain of Nabataean desert cities, caravanserais, and agricultural outposts stretching from the Arabian Peninsula to the Mediterranean coast. Four of those cities survive in the Negev: **Avdat**, **Mamshit**, **Haluza**, and **Shivta**. Together they form a UNESCO World Heritage Site (WHS #1107, inscribed 2005) and one of the most undervisited archaeology circuits in the Middle East.

This guide covers all four cities: what to expect at each, how long to allow, which images appear on INPA passes, and how to put together a self-drive circuit from Beer Sheva or Mitzpe Ramon.

---

## Who were the Nabataeans?

The Nabataeans were a desert-adapted Arab civilisation whose commercial genius lay in mastering water. By engineering an intricate network of runoff dams, cisterns, and check terraces across hyper-arid landscapes, they transformed the Negev into productive agricultural territory — and by controlling the only reliable water sources on the trade route, they effectively controlled the route itself.

At the peak of their power (roughly 100 BCE to 100 CE), the Nabataean kingdom stretched from Petra in the north to Hegra (Mada'in Saleh) in the south and maintained a string of Negev cities that acted as waypoints on the frankincense and myrrh routes from Arabia to Gaza's Mediterranean port. Roman annexation in 106 CE did not end the cities — it redirected them. Several continued to flourish through the Byzantine period (4th–7th centuries CE), when the Negev experienced its densest-ever population, before the Arab conquest and gradual decline in the 7th–8th centuries CE ended their occupation permanently.

The legacy is extraordinary: agricultural terraces, wine presses, church mosaics, and multi-storey mansions — all preserved by the same dry Negev air that made farming the desert so difficult.

## The four cities: what to expect

### 1. Avdat (Oboda) — most developed, most visited

**Why go:** Avdat is the best introduction to Nabataean-Byzantine civilisation in Israel. The acropolis holds two large Byzantine churches (the North Church has a beautifully preserved mosaic pavement), a Nabataean bathhouse, a Roman tower, and the remains of the residential city below.

**What's there:** The excavation covers roughly 15 hectares. The upper acropolis (a 10-minute walk from the entrance) rewards the climb with panoramic views over the central Negev plateau and a clear sense of how the city was organised — Nabataean commercial core at the base, Roman-Byzantine civic and religious structures above. A wine press complex is visible near the entrance; Nabataean wine production was one of the city's economic pillars.

**Practical:** Route 40, approximately 20 km south of Mitzpe Ramon. Open daily 8 am–4 pm (closes 5 pm April–September). INPA ticket required; annual parks pass valid. Allow 2–3 hours for a thorough visit. An INPA audio guide is available via parks.org.il. Combined same-day with [Ein Avdat canyon](#ein-avdat-canyon) makes for a full day.

**Honesty note:** "Avdat" means something like "servant" or "workman" in Nabataean; some sources claim it was named after the Nabataean king Obodas I (Ovdat), possibly buried here — this identification is plausible but not archaeologically confirmed.

### 2. Mamshit (Mampsis) — smallest, best-preserved

**Why go:** Mamshit is the most intact Nabataean city in Israel. The residential buildings are sufficiently well-preserved that you can walk through multi-room mansions and see fresco plasterwork still adhering to walls — a level of preservation rare at any Negev site.

**What's there:** The city occupies a compact 4-hectare area. Key structures include the Large House (a Nabataean nobleman's residence with elaborate interior courtyard), the House of the Frescoes (coloured decorative plaster on walls), a Nabataean caravanserai, a Roman bathhouse, and two Byzantine churches. The East Church has a complete apse mosaic. The site's small scale makes it easy to cover in 90 minutes.

**Practical:** Located near Dimona on Route 25, approximately 40 km east of Beer Sheva. Open daily 8 am–4 pm. INPA ticket required; annual parks pass valid. Allow 1.5–2 hours.

**Honesty note:** Mamshit's frescoes are remarkable survivors; most have deteriorated or been removed to the Israel Antiquities Authority storage. What remains in situ gives you a strong impression of the quality but is not a complete fresco programme.

---

### 3. Haluza (Elusa) — ancient capital, for enthusiasts

**Why go:** Haluza was the Nabataean capital before Petra's dominance and later became the largest Byzantine city in the Negev — at its Byzantine peak, supporting a population estimated at 10,000–20,000 people. The site's scale is extraordinary. The experience of visiting is not.

**What's there:** Haluza is almost entirely unexcavated. What the visitor sees is a large field of mounds, exposed column bases, and scattered architectural fragments across a 100-hectare area. There is a partly-revealed Byzantine theatre (one of very few in the Negev), several exposed building pads, and mosaic fragments visible in eroded sections. No fencing, no facilities, no signage.

**Practical:** Located off Route 222, approximately 20 km south-west of Beer Sheva in the Halutza Sands region. Free entry; no INPA pass required. No facilities. Allow 45–60 minutes. Best suited for visitors with a background in Near Eastern archaeology or those who want to understand the Incense Route's full geographic range. Not suitable as a standalone destination without context.

**Honesty note:** Haluza is listed on the UNESCO inscription but is routinely skipped by general visitors — and understandably so. The scholarly importance (ancient capital; enormous Byzantine city) does not translate into a rewarding raw-site experience. Include it if completeness matters to you; skip it if you want excavated, interpreted sites.

### 4. Shivta (Sobota) — most remote, most atmospheric

**Why go:** Shivta is the most remote of the four cities — and the most rewarding for those willing to make the drive. Three Byzantine churches, extensive wine-production infrastructure, complete cistern networks, and decorated mosaic fragments in situ make Shivta one of the most complete Byzantine townscapes in the Levant. The near-total absence of other visitors gives the site a rare quality of solitude.

**What's there:** The three churches (North, Central, and South) each have surviving mosaic floors in varying states of preservation. The North Church has the finest mosaics; the Central Church has a distinctive triconch apse. Between the churches, the street grid of a 4th–7th-century CE Byzantine town is fully legible — a remarkable planning document in stone. The wine press at the site's southern edge is the largest known Nabataean example in the Negev.

**Practical:** Located on Route 211, approximately 45 km south-west of Beer Sheva and 35 km west of Mitzpe Ramon. INPA ticket required; annual parks pass valid. Open daily 8 am–4 pm. Allow 2–2.5 hours. The final 2 km of access road is unpaved; a high-clearance vehicle is preferable but a standard car can manage cautiously in dry conditions. Do not attempt in wet weather — the Negev sand-loam surface becomes impassable mud.

**Honesty note:** The mosaics at Shivta are exposed to the elements. Some have deteriorated significantly since the site's first documentation in the 19th century. What survives is still exceptional, but do not expect museum-quality condition — the site is actively managed by the Israel Antiquities Authority but full protective roofing has not been installed.

## Ein Avdat canyon

Five kilometres south of the Avdat ruins, **Ein Avdat** (En Avdat) is a slot canyon carved into the Zin limestone plateau by the spring of the same name. The canyon holds year-round freshwater pools, nesting cliffs for griffon vultures and Tristram's grackles, and a desert ecosystem that makes an extreme contrast with the arid plateau above.

The standard visitor route enters from the lower canyon car park (off Route 40; well signed), follows the canyon floor past the main pool and waterfall cascade, and exits via a steep upper section involving metal handrails and one vertical ladder (not suitable for visitors with limited mobility). The full circuit — lower entrance to upper exit — is approximately 2 km and takes 1.5–2 hours. The upper exit returns you to the Avdat car park via a footpath along the canyon rim.

INPA ticket required (same pass covers both Avdat and Ein Avdat on the same day). Open October–May only; summer closure applies due to heat. Arrive with 2 litres of water per person minimum.

## Self-drive circuit: logistics

### Route and timing

A two-day circuit from **Beer Sheva** or **Mitzpe Ramon** covers all four cities comfortably:

**Day 1 — Avdat + Ein Avdat + Mitzpe Ramon**
- Start in Beer Sheva or drive down Route 40 from Tel Aviv/Jerusalem
- Avdat: arrive by 9 am, allow 2–3 hours
- Ein Avdat canyon: afternoon, allow 1.5–2 hours (exit at Avdat upper car park)
- Continue south to Mitzpe Ramon (25 km); overnight base

**Day 2 — Mamshit, Haluza, Shivta (east-then-south loop)**
- Mamshit: 40 km east of Beer Sheva on Route 25 (1.5–2 h)
- Haluza: 20 km south-west of Beer Sheva off Route 222 (45 min; optional)
- Shivta: 45 km south-west of Beer Sheva via Route 211 (2–2.5 h)
- Return to Beer Sheva or continue north to Tel Aviv/Jerusalem

### Getting there

- **From Tel Aviv:** 1.5–2 h to Beer Sheva; Route 6 south then Route 40 south
- **From Jerusalem:** 1.5 h to Beer Sheva via Route 1/35
- **From Eilat:** 2 h north via Route 40 to Mitzpe Ramon; Avdat is 20 km north

See the [driving in Israel guide](/driving-in-israel) for fuel stop planning — petrol available in Beer Sheva, Dimona, and Mitzpe Ramon; no fuel on the western Negev roads near Haluza and Shivta.

### Car requirement

A car is essential. There is no public bus service to Mamshit, Shivta, or Haluza. Beer Sheva Central Bus Station has direct services to Mitzpe Ramon and Dimona, but onward transport to the sites requires a taxi or rental. Hiring a car in Beer Sheva or Tel Aviv and covering all four sites independently is the standard approach. See [DiscoverCars for Negev rental rates](#).

### INPA pass calculation

The annual INPA parks pass covers Avdat, Mamshit, and Shivta (three of the four sites). It also covers [Ein Avdat canyon](/israel-national-parks-pass), Timna Park, the Ramon Crater visitor centre, and dozens of other sites. If your southern Israel trip includes two or more INPA sites beyond the Incense Route cities, the annual pass pays for itself. Use the [INPA pass calculator](/israel-national-parks-pass) to compare the pass cost against individual entry fees for your planned itinerary.

## What to bring

- **Water:** 3 litres per person minimum; no water sales at Haluza or Shivta
- **Sun protection:** hat, factor-50 sunscreen; zero shade at Haluza and much of Shivta
- **Footwear:** closed hiking shoes; site terrain is uneven limestone and compacted earth
- **Cash:** entry paid at INPA machines (card accepted at Avdat and Mamshit; Shivta cash recommended as backup)
- **Fuel:** fill up in Beer Sheva, Dimona, or Mitzpe Ramon before heading to Haluza/Shivta

## Honesty and practical notes

- **"King Solomon's Mines" framing:** Avdat, Mamshit, and Shivta are Nabataean, not Solomonic. Some tour branding uses the "Biblical desert" framing loosely. The sites are authentic and extraordinary on their own terms; they do not need inflated attributions.
- **Summer is genuinely dangerous:** Desert sites reaching 42 °C by midday with no shade are not a marketing caution — people have needed evacuation from Negev sites in summer. Visit October–May.
- **Shivta access track:** The final 2 km unpaved road to Shivta is washboard gravel, not sand. It is passable in dry conditions with a standard car at low speed. After rain it is impassable. Check the weather forecast.
- **Security context:** The Negev Incense Route sites are within Israel proper (Green Line). There are no West Bank access considerations for these specific sites. For general Israel travel security context, see [is Israel safe to visit](/is-israel-safe).
- **UNESCO status and sovereignty:** Avdat, Mamshit, Haluza, and Shivta are inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List under Israel's management. The inscription is not contested.
