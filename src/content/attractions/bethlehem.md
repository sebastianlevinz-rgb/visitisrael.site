Bethlehem (in the West Bank, administered by the Palestinian Authority) is one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited towns and the traditional birthplace of Jesus of Nazareth. Located ten kilometers south of Jerusalem, the small city of roughly twenty-eight thousand residents — a majority Muslim community with a significant Palestinian Christian minority — has welcomed Christian pilgrims for more than seventeen hundred years. The 4th-century Church of the Nativity, inscribed by UNESCO in 2012, stands at the centre of Manger Square as the symbolic heart of Catholic, Greek Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, Protestant, and Evangelical pilgrimage traditions alike.

For a foreign tourist visiting from Jerusalem, Bethlehem fits comfortably into a half-day or full-day itinerary. Most visitors enter via the Israeli border-police checkpoint along Route 60, with passport inspection adding roughly thirty minutes to each leg. Pre-booked tours from Jerusalem handle the crossing and the basilica's entry queue automatically — the simplest path for first-time visitors. This guide walks through everything you need to plan a respectful, well-paced visit.

## About Bethlehem

### Located in the West Bank, administered by the Palestinian Authority

Bethlehem sits in Palestinian Authority Area A under the post-1995 Oslo Accords framework, meaning Palestinian civil and security administration applies inside the city. Foreign tourists may enter freely; the practical implications are confined to the checkpoint logistics on Route 60 and a passport inspection on the return route. Bethlehem's historic Old City — including Manger Square, Star Street, and the Church of the Nativity itself — is fully accessible to international visitors.

### Population and demographics

Bethlehem has roughly twenty-eight thousand residents inside the municipal core, expanding to over two hundred thousand when the adjacent towns of Beit Jala and Beit Sahour are counted. The city is majority Muslim today, with a significant Palestinian Christian minority who form the historic heart of the Bethlehem pilgrimage economy. Christmas remains the high point of the civic calendar; the Greek Orthodox Christmas (January 6-7) and Armenian Christmas (January 18-19) follow the December 24-25 Catholic and Protestant celebrations, giving the town three weeks of overlapping pilgrimage seasons.

### Ancient continuous habitation; UNESCO World Heritage 2012

Bethlehem appears in Bronze Age Egyptian records as Bit-Lahmi, in Hebrew Scripture as the city of King David, and in the Gospel of Luke as the birthplace of Jesus. The Constantinian basilica of the Church of the Nativity, built in the 4th century over the traditional birth cave and rebuilt by Justinian in the 6th century, stands today substantially intact — its mosaic floor and side aisles still bear Byzantine workmanship. UNESCO inscribed "Birthplace of Jesus: Church of the Nativity and the Pilgrimage Route, Bethlehem" in 2012 under the cultural-significance criteria.

## When to Visit Bethlehem

### Christmas season (December — major pilgrimage; book lodging months ahead)

The week of December 22-26 is the most intense pilgrimage period for the Catholic and Protestant calendars. Manger Square fills with a Christmas tree lighting on December 1, midnight Mass at St. Catherine's Church on December 24 (ticketed; book through the Latin Patriarchate of Jerusalem), and processions through the Old City throughout the week. Lodging inside Bethlehem itself books up six months in advance during Christmas; Jerusalem hotels with day-tour shuttles are the practical fallback.

### Greek Orthodox + Armenian Christmas (January)

The Greek Orthodox Patriarchate celebrates Christmas on January 6-7 by the Julian calendar; the Armenian Apostolic Patriarchate on January 18-19. Each tradition runs its own procession and Mass inside the Church of the Nativity, sharing the basilica under the 1852 Status Quo agreement that governs the three custodianships. January is an excellent quieter window for ecumenically minded pilgrims.

### Spring and autumn shoulder seasons

March-May and September-November offer the most comfortable weather and the smallest tour-bus crowds. Mornings are cool (10-15°C); afternoons reach a pleasant 20-25°C. These shoulder months are ideal for combining a Bethlehem half-day with a multi-day Jerusalem itinerary.

## Visiting from Jerusalem — Practical Info

### Distance: 10km south of Jerusalem (40 minutes with checkpoint)

The drive from central Jerusalem to Manger Square covers about ten kilometers along Route 60. Without the checkpoint, the drive takes fifteen minutes; with the checkpoint, allow forty to forty-five minutes each way during weekday daylight hours. Saturday mornings and pre-Christmas peaks can stretch the checkpoint queue to ninety minutes.

### Foreign tourists may enter Area A (Palestinian Authority civil control)

A red sign at the checkpoint reads, in Hebrew, English, and Arabic, that entry to Area A is forbidden to Israeli citizens and dangerous to their lives. The sign does not apply to foreign tourists — you may enter Area A freely with your passport. Most foreign nationalities receive a stamp-on-paper rather than a stamp in the passport at the Israeli border, which preserves clean entry to neighboring Arab states.

### Passport required at the checkpoint

Carry your passport on the entire trip. On the return leg into Israel, an Israeli border-police officer will inspect your passport and ask briefly about the purpose of your visit. The interaction is routine and typically takes under a minute per traveler; the queue itself adds twenty to thirty minutes during peak hours.

### Pre-booked tours from Jerusalem handle the crossing

Most foreign tourists choose a pre-booked half-day or full-day tour from Jerusalem. The tour van handles the checkpoint, the Church of the Nativity entry queue, and the return route — you sit, learn, and walk. Sherut shared-taxi runs from Damascus Gate in Jerusalem to Bethlehem run frequently and are the cheapest option, but require you to navigate the checkpoint and find an onward Palestinian taxi in central Bethlehem on your own.

### Israeli citizens cannot legally enter Area A under most circumstances

Under Israeli law, Israeli citizens are prohibited from entering Area A except under specific permits. Christian and Muslim Israeli residents with religious or family permits are exceptions; foreign tourists with Israeli residency should check with their consulate before traveling.

## Top Things to See in Bethlehem

### Church of the Nativity

The 4th-century Constantinian basilica is the centerpiece of any Bethlehem visit. Built by Emperor Constantine over the traditional birth cave and rebuilt by Justinian in the 6th century, the basilica is the oldest continuously operating Christian church in the world. The Door of Humility — a small entrance only 1.2 meters tall — symbolizes the humble incarnation; visitors stoop to enter. Inside, the Catholic Franciscan custody, the Greek Orthodox Patriarchate of Jerusalem, and the Armenian Apostolic Patriarchate share jurisdiction under the 1852 Status Quo agreement. Mosaics restored between 2013 and 2019 are now visible in their full Byzantine splendor.

### Manger Square and the Grotto of the Nativity

Manger Square is the civic and religious heart of Bethlehem — paved, pedestrianized, framed by the Church of the Nativity on the east, the Mosque of Omar on the west, and the Peace Center on the south. The Grotto of the Nativity, beneath the basilica, is the traditional birth cave; a 14-pointed silver star set in marble marks the precise spot of birth. The Grotto has a separate entry queue and can take an additional thirty minutes during peak season.

### Milk Grotto and St. Catherine's Church

A two-minute walk from the basilica, the Milk Grotto chapel marks the traditional cave where the Holy Family hid before fleeing to Egypt; the white limestone is associated with Marian devotion. St. Catherine's Church, attached to the Church of the Nativity, is the Catholic Franciscan parish where the December 24 midnight Mass is broadcast to the world.

### Bethlehem Old City and Star Street

Star Street is the historic pilgrim route from the city gates to Manger Square. The Old City's narrow alleys host olive-wood workshops, mother-of-pearl carvers, and small bakeries. The route is a fifteen-minute walk on its own; with stops it expands to a comfortable half-hour exploration.

## The Church of the Nativity

### Original 4th-century Constantinian basilica

Built by Emperor Constantine in 327 CE over the traditional Christian-tradition birth cave identified by his mother Helena, the original basilica was octagonal in plan. After a Samaritan revolt in the 6th century, Justinian rebuilt it on the present rectangular nave plan. Both the columns and the Byzantine mosaics in the central nave survive from the Justinianic rebuild.

### Door of Humility — narrow entrance

The main entrance — only 1.2 meters tall and 80 centimeters wide — was reduced during the Crusader and Ottoman eras to prevent armed riders from entering. It is now read symbolically by pilgrims as a reminder of the incarnation's humility. Tour groups queue here in the morning; arriving before 9 AM avoids the worst lines.

### Greek Orthodox iconostasis, Catholic altar, Armenian chapel

Each of the three sharing denominations maintains its own zone inside the basilica. The Greek Orthodox iconostasis dominates the apse; the Catholic Franciscan altar sits in the north transept; the Armenian Apostolic chapel occupies the south transept. The 1852 Status Quo agreement, codified by Ottoman ferman, governs which denomination uses which space and when — a binding agreement that has held for over a century and a half.

### Grotto of the Nativity (silver star)

Beneath the high altar, accessed by twin marble staircases, the Grotto holds the silver fourteen-pointed star reading "Hic de Virgine Maria Jesus Christus natus est" (Here Jesus Christ was born of the Virgin Mary). The star was installed in 1717 by the Catholics, replaced by the Greek Orthodox in 1852, and has been restored several times.

### Recent restoration (mosaics 2013-2019)

A six-year restoration funded by the Palestinian Authority and an international consortium uncovered and conserved the Byzantine wall mosaics, the roof structure, and the windows. The work was completed in 2019; the basilica today shows roughly seventy percent of the original Byzantine mosaic program.

## Christian Pilgrimage Routes

### Star Street (the pilgrim historical route)

Star Street is the traditional pilgrim approach from the city gates to Manger Square, named for the star that, according to the Gospel of Matthew, guided the Magi. Today the route hosts the December 24 procession of the Latin Patriarch from Jerusalem to the basilica, with Boy Scouts, dignitaries, and pilgrims following on foot.

### Mar Saba Monastery (Greek Orthodox; men-only)

Founded in 483 CE by Saint Sabas the Sanctified, Mar Saba clings to the cliffs of the Kidron Valley fifteen kilometers east of Bethlehem. Greek Orthodox monks still inhabit the monastery; entry is restricted to men. The cliffside silhouette is photogenic from the valley road, and a quick ridge visit is included in some Bethlehem-plus-desert tour combinations.

### Herodion (Herod the Great's mountain fortress)

Twelve kilometers south of Bethlehem, the Herodion is the fortress-palace and tomb of Herod the Great, a UNESCO-tentative archaeological site administered by the Israel Nature and Parks Authority. The volcano-cone-shaped hill is visible from much of southern Bethlehem; a half-day combined tour from Jerusalem can include both the Church of the Nativity and Herodion.

## Day Trips from Bethlehem

### Jerusalem (10km north — most visitors come from there)

Most Bethlehem visitors base in Jerusalem; the reverse half-day from Bethlehem back into Jerusalem covers the Western Wall, the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, and the Mount of Olives easily.

### Mar Saba Monastery (Greek Orthodox; men-only)

A half-day combined trip to Mar Saba and back through the Judean Desert is popular among Greek Orthodox pilgrims; the cliffside views work for any visitor.

### Herodion (12 minutes south)

A short detour for archaeologically minded travelers. Combine with Solomon's Pools and a stop at a Palestinian winery in Cremisan for a full-day cultural circuit.

## How to Get to Bethlehem

### From Jerusalem: pre-booked tour, sherut, or taxi

Pre-booked tours from Jerusalem are the most common path. Sherut shared taxis depart from Damascus Gate every fifteen minutes; the ride costs roughly twenty shekels and drops at the checkpoint on the Bethlehem side. Private taxi from West Jerusalem typically runs eighty to one hundred and twenty shekels to the checkpoint, then a Palestinian taxi continues into central Bethlehem.

### From Tel Aviv: rental car or tour (about an hour with checkpoint)

There is no direct public bus from Tel Aviv to Bethlehem; transit routes via Jerusalem. A rental car drive from Tel Aviv takes about an hour to Jerusalem and another forty minutes through the checkpoint to Bethlehem. Many travelers prefer to overnight in Jerusalem and day-trip from there.

### No direct public bus from Tel Aviv; transit through Jerusalem

Public bus connections terminate at Jerusalem's Central Bus Station; from there, sherut or tour onward to Bethlehem. This is the cheapest option but the most logistically complex; budget travelers without time pressure can manage it comfortably.

## Where to Eat in Bethlehem

### Palestinian cuisine — musakhan, maqluba, knafeh

Bethlehem's restaurants serve classic Palestinian cuisine: musakhan (sumac-spiced chicken on flatbread with caramelized onions), maqluba (upside-down rice with eggplant and lamb), and knafeh (cheese pastry soaked in rose syrup) are staples. Restaurants along Manger Square cater to tour groups; quieter family-run kitchens on Star Street and around the souq serve a more local rhythm.

### Manger Square restaurants

Afteem Restaurant on Manger Square is the most famous tourist-facing kitchen, with hummus and falafel that have served Bethlehem visitors for decades. The Tent Restaurant nearby offers a buffet popular with tour buses. Both keep modest pricing relative to Jerusalem.

### Bethlehem winery (Cremisan)

Salesian fathers founded the Cremisan winery in 1885 in the Beit Jala hills west of Bethlehem. The winery produces Cabernet Sauvignon, Chardonnay, and a sweet sacramental wine; tastings are open to visitors by appointment. A half-day cultural circuit can include Bethlehem, Cremisan, and a return to Jerusalem.

## Practical Tips

### Cash — Israeli shekels accepted

Bethlehem merchants accept Israeli shekels universally; some also take Jordanian dinars and US dollars. ATMs are plentiful in central Bethlehem. Credit cards work at hotels and larger restaurants but not at souq stalls.

### Modest dress at religious sites

The Church of the Nativity, St. Catherine's Church, and the Milk Grotto all require modest dress: shoulders and knees covered for both men and women. Shawls are sometimes provided at the basilica entrance; better to bring your own.

### Checkpoint timing — allow extra time

The Israeli border-police checkpoint queue varies from five minutes to ninety minutes depending on day, time, and season. Friday afternoons (when Israelis return for Shabbat) and pre-Christmas peaks are the worst; weekday late mornings are the easiest.

### Passport stamping considerations

Israeli border police now use stamp-on-paper rather than stamp-in-passport for most foreign visitors; the practice was introduced to ease subsequent travel to Lebanon, Iran, and Saudi Arabia. If you wish to avoid any stamp, mention this politely at the checkpoint counter.
