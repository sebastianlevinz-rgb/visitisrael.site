**HaTachana** ("The Station" in Hebrew) is a restored Ottoman-era railway complex at the southern tip of Neve Tzedek. The 22 stone buildings that once formed the Jaffa terminus of the first railway line in Ottoman Palestine now hold restaurants, Israeli design boutiques, small galleries and an outdoor events courtyard. The complex runs two weekly markets and hosts regular evening concerts and cultural events. It sits midway between the Neve Tzedek neighbourhood and Old Jaffa — a natural midpoint for a south Tel Aviv day.

## The railway history

The Jaffa–Jerusalem Railway opened in 1892 — the first railway line in Ottoman Palestine and among the earliest in the broader region. The French company that built it chose the site south of the Jaffa port neighbourhood as the coastal terminus; the Jerusalem terminus is the **First Station** complex in the German Colony, now also a restaurant and market venue.

The line carried passengers and freight through the British Mandate period. After 1948, traffic gradually declined as road and later rail infrastructure improved; the Jaffa terminus was formally closed in the 1940s. The stone buildings — built in a style typical of late-Ottoman public works — were abandoned for decades until a restoration project completed in 2010 returned them to public use as a commercial and cultural complex.

The Ottoman stone construction is the visual character of HaTachana. The buildings are lower and older-looking than anything else in the neighbourhood, and the scale of the complex — 22 separate structures arranged around a central courtyard — gives it the feel of a small village rather than a single venue.

## The weekly markets

**Friday morning organic market (~08:00–14:00):** The Friday market is the busiest and most local-feeling of the two. Vendors sell seasonal produce, artisan cheeses and yoghurts, small-batch olive oils, homemade jams and pickles, fresh herbs, honey, wine and prepared foods. The market reflects the Neve Tzedek neighbourhood's food culture — quality is consistently high, prices are above supermarket level. Arrive before 11:00 for the best selection; the market winds down from 13:00 as vendors pack up before Shabbat. Entry is free.

**Thursday night designer market (~17:00–22:00):** The Thursday evening market brings together Israeli designers and craftspeople — jewellery, hand-printed textiles, leather goods, ceramics, limited-edition clothing and accessories. The courtyard atmosphere is social rather than purely commercial; musicians sometimes play between the stalls. Entry is free.

## Restaurants and cafes

The restaurant cluster at HaTachana leans toward casual-to-mid-range dining in a quieter setting than the Neve Tzedek café strip. The buildings have outdoor terraces and inner courtyard tables under fig and olive trees, which makes the complex one of the more pleasant outdoor-dining environments in south Tel Aviv. Menus reflect the Tel Aviv norm: Israeli-Mediterranean sharing plates, fresh fish, brunch formats available through the afternoon on weekends. Restaurants are closed on Shabbat (Saturday); most reopen Saturday evening.

## Design boutiques and shops

The design boutiques at HaTachana focus on Israeli designers and locally-made goods — ceramics, prints, textile homewares, jewellery and fashion. The shops are clustered mainly in the south-eastern section of the complex. Hours vary by individual shop; most run Sunday through Thursday and close before Shabbat on Friday afternoon.

## Courtyard events

The central courtyard hosts open-air concerts, film screenings and cultural events, particularly in summer (May–October). These range from free community events to ticketed music nights. Check the complex's online calendar at hatachana.co.il or local Tel Aviv events listings (Timeout Israel, Saloona) for current programming.

## A walking route

HaTachana fits naturally into a south Tel Aviv circuit. Start at the southern end of Rothschild Boulevard, walk south-west through **Neve Tzedek** and the Suzanne Dellal Centre courtyard, continue five minutes to HaTachana for coffee or lunch, then walk a further fifteen minutes south along the coastal path to **Old Jaffa** for the Clock Tower, the flea market and the port. The circuit from Rothschild to Old Jaffa covers about four kilometres and takes a comfortable half-day including stops.

## Nearby Attractions

**[Neve Tzedek](/tel-aviv/neve-tzedek)** is five minutes north — the 1887 neighbourhood of restored Templer houses, Shabazi Street cafés and the Suzanne Dellal Centre. **[Old Jaffa](/jaffa-travel-guide)** is fifteen minutes south — the ancient port, the flea market and Abu Hassan hummus. **Alma Beach** and the southern Tayelet are ten minutes west. **[Florentin](/tel-aviv/florentin)** — the street-art and nightlife quarter — is fifteen minutes east along Eilat Street.

## Why Visit

HaTachana offers the rare combination of genuine 19th-century Ottoman architecture in the heart of a modern city and a functioning local market culture. The Friday morning market is one of the most authentic food-and-producer markets in Tel Aviv — less touristic than the Carmel Market, more local in character. The Thursday evening designer market adds a social dimension that most tourist itineraries miss. And the position between Neve Tzedek and Old Jaffa makes HaTachana a natural stop rather than a detour.
